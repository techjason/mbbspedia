## Complications of Pre-eclampsia

***Pre-eclampsia has many consequences to the mother and child*** [1]. ***Think of maternal risk from head to toe — remember the pathophysiology of pre-eclampsia, basically a systemic condition, so can result in many, many problems*** [1].

The complications of pre-eclampsia flow directly from the pathophysiology: **widespread endothelial dysfunction** damages every organ system in the mother, while **chronic placental ischaemia** compromises the fetus. Additionally, some complications arise from the **treatment itself** (iatrogenic) or from the **prematurity** forced by early delivery.

We will systematically cover:
- A. Maternal complications (organised head-to-toe by organ system)
- B. Fetal and neonatal complications
- C. Iatrogenic / treatment-related complications
- D. Long-term consequences

---

### A. Maternal Complications

#### A1. Neurological Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology | Clinical Features | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| ***Eclampsia*** | End-stage cerebral involvement: posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES) → cortical irritability → generalised tonic-clonic seizures. Cerebral vasospasm + autoregulatory failure → vasogenic oedema in posterior circulation (occipital/parietal lobes have less sympathetic innervation → less autoregulatory reserve) | ***Generalised tonic-clonic seizures*** in context of pre-eclampsia; can occur antepartum, intrapartum, or postpartum. ***10% risk of second seizure*** [2] | ***Magnesium sulphate*** [2][3]: loading 4 g IV + maintenance 1–2 g/h. Continue 24 hours post-delivery. ABC, prevent aspiration, stabilise → then deliver |
| ***Stroke (ischaemic or haemorrhagic)*** | Ischaemic: vasospasm → thrombosis in cerebral arteries. Haemorrhagic: severe uncontrolled hypertension → rupture of damaged cerebral arterioles. Haemorrhagic stroke is the leading cause of death from pre-eclampsia/eclampsia | Sudden focal neurological deficit, headache, altered consciousness, hemiplegia, aphasia | ***BP control*** is the key preventive measure — target < 140/90 to prevent ICH [2][3]. ***Hypertension shooting up beyond 160/110 may cause ICH*** [1]. CT brain urgently; neurosurgical consultation |
| ***PRES (posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome)*** | Hypertensive autoregulatory failure → blood-brain barrier breakdown → vasogenic oedema, predominantly in posterior cerebral territory (watershed areas of posterior circulation). "Posterior" because these vessels have less sympathetic innervation, making them more vulnerable | Headache, visual disturbance (cortical blindness, scotomata, photopsia), confusion, seizures. Usually reversible with BP control and delivery | BP control; MgSO₄; MRI shows bilateral symmetric T2/FLAIR hyperintensity in parieto-occipital white matter. Typically resolves within days to weeks if promptly treated |
| **Cortical blindness** | Occipital lobe ischaemia/oedema (PRES) → bilateral visual cortex dysfunction | Complete visual loss with preserved pupillary reflexes (because the lesion is cortical, not in the optic nerve/retina). Patient may deny blindness (Anton syndrome) | Usually reversible with treatment of pre-eclampsia. Can be confirmed on MRI |

<Callout title="Eclampsia: The Numbers" type="error">
***Eclampsia occurs in < 1% of pre-eclampsia cases*** [1], but when it does, there is a ***10% risk of a second seizure*** [2]. This is why MgSO₄ is continued for 24 hours after delivery or last seizure — to cover this recurrence window. ***Remember postpartum presentation of eclampsia is possible*** [1] because circulating anti-angiogenic factors persist after delivery. ***Eclampsia is the 2nd leading cause of maternal mortality in HK (related to eclampsia, stroke)*** [1].
</Callout>

#### A2. Cardiovascular Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology | Clinical Features | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Severe hypertension / Hypertensive emergency** | Profound ↑ SVR from endothelial dysfunction, loss of prostacyclin/NO, ↑ endothelin-1/TxA₂. ***Eclampsia or severe pre-eclampsia is a compelling indication for acute BP control*** [7][8] | BP ≥ 160/110 (or > 180/120 in hypertensive emergency) | ***IV labetalol / hydralazine*** [1]. ***Target BP < 140 in first hour for pre-eclampsia/eclampsia*** [8] |
| ***Pulmonary oedema*** | Multi-factorial: (1) ↑ capillary permeability from endothelial damage → capillary leak, (2) iatrogenic fluid overload, (3) ↓ oncotic pressure from hypoalbuminaemia (proteinuria), (4) postpartum fluid mobilisation from extravascular space (days 2–5) | Acute dyspnoea, orthopnoea, pink frothy sputum, bilateral crackles, hypoxia. CXR: bilateral alveolar shadowing | Oxygen, sit upright, IV furosemide (cautiously — intravascular volume may already be depleted), fluid restriction, consider non-invasive ventilation. ***Fluid balance crucial — don't just pump fluids into patient*** [1] |
| **Cardiac failure** | Afterload mismatch (severe ↑ SVR → ↑ LV wall stress), diastolic dysfunction, myocardial ischaemia | Dyspnoea, raised JVP, gallop rhythm, pulmonary oedema | Afterload reduction (antihypertensives), diuretics, cardiac monitoring; echocardiography |

#### A3. Hepatic Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology | Clinical Features | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| ***HELLP syndrome*** | Extreme manifestation of the microangiopathic process: hepatic sinusoidal fibrin deposition → periportal necrosis → Haemolysis + Elevated Liver enzymes + Low Platelets. Occurs in 10–20% of severe pre-eclampsia [2][3] | ***RUQ/epigastric pain***, nausea/vomiting, jaundice, malaise. Lab: schistocytes on PBS, LDH > 600, AST ≥ 70, platelets < 100. May occur ***without significant hypertension*** — diagnostic trap | Delivery (definitive). Supportive: blood products (platelets if < 50 and bleeding/pre-operative; FFP if DIC). Dexamethasone may transiently improve platelets. ***Screen and manage associated complication: HELLP*** [2] |
| **Hepatic subcapsular haematoma** | Periportal necrosis → haemorrhage → blood tracks under Glisson's capsule → haematoma forms between liver parenchyma and capsule | Severe RUQ pain, right shoulder tip pain (diaphragmatic irritation), hepatomegaly. USS/CT: subcapsular collection | Conservative if intact: strict BP control, avoid palpation of liver (can rupture it!), serial imaging, correct coagulopathy |
| ***Hepatic rupture*** | Subcapsular haematoma ruptures through Glisson's capsule → catastrophic haemoperitoneum | Sudden haemodynamic collapse, distended abdomen, peritonitis, shock. Mortality 50–75% | Surgical emergency: laparotomy, packing, ligation of bleeding vessels; massive transfusion protocol; ICU |
| ***Liver failure*** | Extensive periportal necrosis; can overlap with AFLP | Coagulopathy, encephalopathy, hypoglycaemia, jaundice | Supportive; liver transplant in extremis; differentiate from AFLP |

<Callout title="HELLP Without Hypertension">
Up to 15–20% of HELLP syndrome cases present ***without significant hypertension***, and ~10% present ***without proteinuria***. This makes it one of the most dangerous diagnostic traps in obstetrics. If a pregnant woman in the third trimester presents with nausea, vomiting, RUQ pain, and abnormal bloods — think HELLP even if the BP is "normal."
</Callout>

#### A4. Renal Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology | Clinical Features | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| ***Acute renal failure (1–5%)*** [2] | Glomerular endotheliosis → ↓ GFR. Renal vasospasm → ischaemia. Intravascular volume depletion (haemoconcentration from capillary leak). In severe cases → acute tubular necrosis (ATN) or bilateral cortical necrosis (rare but devastating) | Oliguria ( < 400 mL/24h or < 0.5 mL/kg/h), rising creatinine, fluid overload, hyperkalaemia, metabolic acidosis | Strict I/O, cautious fluid management (balance between dehydration and overload), avoid nephrotoxins, correct electrolytes. Renal replacement therapy/dialysis if refractory [11]. Usually reversible after delivery |
| **Bilateral cortical necrosis** | Most severe form: complete ischaemic necrosis of renal cortex from prolonged vasospasm + DIC. Irreversible | Anuria, renal failure not recovering after delivery | Chronic dialysis; renal transplant |

#### A5. Haematological Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology | Clinical Features | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| ***DIC (disseminated intravascular coagulation)*** | Widespread endothelial damage → activation of coagulation cascade → consumption of clotting factors and platelets → paradoxical bleeding. ***Release of placental materials into circulation → pro-coagulant effect*** [6] | Bleeding from puncture sites, mucosal bleeding, petechiae; paradoxically also microvascular thrombosis (organ ischaemia). Lab: ↑ PT, ↑ APTT, ↓ fibrinogen, ↑↑ D-dimer, ↓ platelets, schistocytes | Treat the cause (delivery); blood product support (FFP for clotting factors, cryoprecipitate for fibrinogen < 1 g/L, platelet transfusion if < 50 with active bleeding). ***Screen and manage: coagulopathy*** [2] |
| **Thrombocytopenia** | Platelet consumption at damaged endothelial surfaces (microangiopathic process) | Petechiae, bruising, bleeding risk during delivery/surgery | Platelet transfusion if < 50 × 10⁹/L and surgical intervention planned, or active bleeding. Avoid regional anaesthesia if platelets < 75–80 |
| **MAHA (microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia)** | RBCs sheared by fibrin strands in damaged microvasculature → intravascular haemolysis [6] | Pallor, jaundice, dark urine. Lab: schistocytes on PBS, ↑ LDH, ↓ haptoglobin, ↑ indirect bilirubin | Part of HELLP; treat underlying cause. Transfuse packed RBCs if symptomatic anaemia |
| **Venous thromboembolism** | ***Pregnancy increases risk of thromboembolic complications, pre-eclampsia also a risk factor*** [1]. Virchow's triad fully activated: endothelial damage + hypercoagulability (↑ clotting factors, ↓ protein S) + stasis (bed rest, immobility) | DVT: unilateral leg swelling, calf pain. PE: dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, haemodynamic instability | ***Thromboprophylaxis: pressure stockings ± LMWH*** [1]. Therapeutic anticoagulation with LMWH if confirmed VTE [12] |

#### A6. Placental Complications

| Complication | Pathophysiology | Clinical Features | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| ***Placental abruption (1–4%)*** [2] | Vasospasm in decidual arteries → haemorrhage behind placenta → premature separation from uterine wall. Pre-eclampsia damages the spiral arteries → they are prone to rupture | Sudden-onset painful vaginal bleeding (revealed) or concealed abruption (pain without bleeding), "woody-hard" tender uterus, fetal distress, maternal shock | Emergency delivery (usually caesarean). Massive transfusion if needed. DIC commonly accompanies severe abruption |

#### A7. Anaesthetic Complications

***Pre-existing obstetric disease e.g., pre-eclampsia*** contributes to ***difficult intubation*** (due to ***weight gain and oedema***) [13]. The laryngeal and pharyngeal oedema from pre-eclampsia (generalised capillary leak) narrows the airway, making intubation 8× more common to be difficult compared to non-pregnant patients [13].

***Placenta bed has no autoregulation to compensate for drop in BP*** [13] — so during regional anaesthesia (spinal/epidural), the sympathetic blockade → ↓ SVR → ↓ BP → further compromises an already ischaemic uteroplacental circulation. However, regional anaesthesia is still preferred over GA because GA carries additional risks (aspiration, difficult airway, neonatal sedation).

**Thrombocytopenia** complicates the decision for regional anaesthesia — if platelets < 75–80 × 10⁹/L, epidural/spinal is generally avoided due to risk of epidural haematoma.

### B. Fetal and Neonatal Complications

***Fetal: Remember pathophysiology of pre-eclampsia, basically poor perfusion to placenta, meaning that: Baby grows less → IUGR, potential preterm delivery. Preterm and low birth weight has many long-term complications (NEC, cardiovascular etc.)*** [1].

#### B1. Direct Fetal Complications (from Placental Insufficiency)

| Complication | Pathophysiology | Incidence [2] |
|---|---|---|
| ***Preterm delivery (15–67%)*** | Either spontaneous (from abruption/fetal distress) or iatrogenic (delivery indicated for maternal/fetal safety) | Most common fetal complication |
| ***IUGR (10–25%)*** | Chronic placental insufficiency → ↓ nutrient and O₂ delivery → asymmetric growth restriction (brain-sparing: head circumference relatively preserved, abdominal circumference ↓ because liver glycogen stores are depleted first) | Second most common |
| **Oligohydramnios** | ↓ fetal renal perfusion → ↓ fetal urine output → less amniotic fluid | Variable |
| **Fetal distress / non-reassuring CTG** | Acute placental insufficiency → fetal hypoxia → late decelerations, reduced variability | During labour especially |
| ***Hypoxia — neurological injury ( < 1%)*** | Severe acute placental insufficiency → fetal asphyxia → hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy (HIE) | Rare but devastating |
| ***Perinatal death (1–2%)*** | Severe placental insufficiency, acute abruption, or extreme prematurity | Depends on gestation at onset and severity |
| ***Stillbirth*** | Chronic or acute placental failure → fetal demise in utero | More common in early-onset severe pre-eclampsia |

#### B2. Complications of Prematurity (from Iatrogenic Early Delivery)

***The timing of delivery is a balance between severity of pre-eclampsia and risk of prematurity*** [2]. When the baby must be delivered early to save the mother, the neonate faces the consequences of immaturity:

| Complication | Pathophysiology | Prevention/Management |
|---|---|---|
| **Respiratory distress syndrome (RDS)** | Immature type II pneumocytes → insufficient surfactant → alveolar collapse → hypoxia | Antenatal corticosteroids (betamethasone/dexamethasone if < 34 weeks); postnatal surfactant replacement; CPAP/mechanical ventilation |
| **Intraventricular haemorrhage (IVH)** | Fragile germinal matrix capillaries in preterm brain → rupture with haemodynamic fluctuations | Antenatal corticosteroids; MgSO₄ for neuroprotection ( < 32 weeks); gentle handling |
| ***Necrotising enterocolitis (NEC)*** | Immature gut mucosa + ischaemia → bacterial translocation → bowel necrosis | Breast milk feeding; careful introduction of enteral feeds; monitor for abdominal distension, bloody stools |
| **Retinopathy of prematurity (ROP)** | Immature retinal vasculature → aberrant neovascularisation in response to oxygen therapy | Controlled oxygen administration; screening eye exams |
| **Sepsis** | Immature immune system → susceptibility to nosocomial infections | Infection control; empirical antibiotics if suspected |
| **Neurodevelopmental delay** | Hypoxic-ischaemic injury + IVH + white matter damage (periventricular leukomalacia) | Long-term developmental follow-up |
| ***Long-term cardiovascular morbidity associated with low birth weight*** | Barker hypothesis / developmental origins of health and disease (DOHaD): fetal programming in adverse intrauterine environment → epigenetic changes → ↑ lifetime risk of hypertension, diabetes, cardiovascular disease [2] | Long-term cardiovascular screening |

<Callout title="The Fetal Programming Concept">
Low-birth-weight infants (whether from IUGR or prematurity) have altered cardiovascular and metabolic programming. The "thrifty phenotype" hypothesis proposes that a fetus adapts to a nutrient-poor environment by reducing insulin sensitivity, altering nephron number, and reprogramming vascular reactivity. These adaptations, beneficial in utero, become maladaptive in postnatal life → ↑ risk of hypertension, type 2 diabetes, coronary heart disease, and stroke in adulthood. This means that pre-eclampsia has ***transgenerational consequences***.
</Callout>

#### B3. Mirror Syndrome (Rare)

***Mother may develop polyhydramnios-related complications or even mirror syndrome (maternal pre-eclampsia associated with fetal/placental hydrops)*** [14]. In mirror syndrome, the fetus has hydrops fetalis (from any cause — immune or non-immune), and the mother develops a "mirror" picture of generalised oedema, hypertension, and proteinuria resembling pre-eclampsia. The mechanism is poorly understood but may involve massive placental oedema → release of anti-angiogenic factors.

### C. Iatrogenic / Treatment-Related Complications

| Complication | Cause | Mechanism | Prevention |
|---|---|---|---|
| ***Magnesium toxicity*** | MgSO₄ infusion | ***Magnesium sulfate has a very low therapeutic range → can enter toxic ranges very easily*** [1]. Mg²⁺ is renally excreted → ***any cause of reduced urinary output, remember to be careful with magnesium dosage*** [1]. Toxicity causes loss of reflexes → respiratory depression → cardiac arrest [10] | Hourly monitoring of reflexes, RR, urine output. ***Antidote: calcium gluconate*** (note: lecture slides say "calcium carbonate" but the correct antidote is calcium gluconate 10% IV [1]) |
| **Iatrogenic pulmonary oedema** | Overzealous IV fluid administration | ***Leaky vessels → third-spacing → fluid overload*** [1] | Restrict fluids to ~80 mL/h; strict I/O monitoring |
| **Maternal hypotension from antihypertensives** | Over-aggressive BP lowering | ↓ uteroplacental perfusion → fetal distress. ↓ cerebral perfusion → maternal ischaemic stroke | Controlled gradual reduction; do not drop BP too fast |
| **Complications of prematurity** | Early delivery to save mother | See section B2 above | Corticosteroids before 34 weeks; MgSO₄ for neuroprotection before 32 weeks; deliver in tertiary centre with NICU |
| **Surgical complications** | Emergency caesarean section | Wound infection, haemorrhage (especially if DIC/thrombocytopenia), anaesthetic complications | Correct coagulopathy pre-operatively; thromboprophylaxis; antibiotics |

### D. Long-Term Maternal Consequences

This is increasingly recognised as one of the most important aspects of pre-eclampsia — the disease does NOT end at delivery.

***Long-term cardiovascular morbidity*** [2] in women with history of pre-eclampsia:

| Long-Term Risk | Relative Risk (vs women without pre-eclampsia) | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Chronic hypertension** | 3–4× | Pre-eclampsia unmasks women with subclinical endothelial dysfunction and vascular disease. Persistent vascular remodelling after pre-eclampsia. Shared risk factors (obesity, insulin resistance) |
| **Ischaemic heart disease** | 2× | Endothelial dysfunction, accelerated atherosclerosis, metabolic syndrome |
| **Stroke** | 2× | Vascular damage, chronic hypertension |
| **Chronic kidney disease** | 4–5× | Residual renal injury from glomerular endotheliosis; hypertensive nephrosclerosis |
| **Type 2 diabetes mellitus** | 2× | Insulin resistance, metabolic programming |
| **Recurrence of pre-eclampsia** | 15–20% in next pregnancy | Underlying maternal susceptibility persists |
| **Venous thromboembolism** | 1.5–2× | Persistent prothrombotic tendency; endothelial dysfunction |

**Implications for follow-up**:
- Annual BP and metabolic screening (fasting glucose, lipids, BMI, urinalysis) lifelong
- Lifestyle modification counselling (diet, exercise, weight management, smoking cessation)
- Aspirin prophylaxis in future pregnancies (start before 16 weeks)
- Consider pre-eclampsia as a ***cardiovascular risk equivalent*** — these women deserve the same attention as patients with established cardiovascular disease

***Prognosis depends on whether there is superimposed pre-eclampsia (17%), deterioration of renal function, and progression of underlying disorder (e.g., SLE)*** [2] — particularly relevant for women with chronic hypertension.

<Callout title="Intellectual Disability as a Downstream Consequence">
Pre-eclampsia is listed as an environmental cause of intellectual disability through the prenatal pathway: ***pregnancy complications, e.g., placental insufficiency, pre-eclampsia, IUGR, APH*** → fetal hypoxia → hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy → neurodevelopmental impairment [15]. This underscores the importance of early detection and timely delivery to prevent catastrophic fetal neurological injury.
</Callout>

<Callout title="High Yield Summary">

**Maternal complications (head-to-toe)**:
1. ***Eclampsia ( < 1%)*** — generalised tonic-clonic seizures; 10% risk of recurrence; leading cause of maternal death via ICH
2. ***Stroke*** — haemorrhagic (from uncontrolled HTN) or ischaemic (vasospasm); preventable with BP < 160/110
3. ***Pulmonary oedema*** — capillary leak + fluid overload + hypoalbuminaemia; restrict fluids
4. ***HELLP syndrome*** — Haemolysis + Elevated Liver enzymes + Low Platelets; can occur without HTN
5. ***Hepatic rupture*** — rare but ~50% mortality; from subcapsular haematoma rupture
6. ***Acute renal failure (1–5%)*** — glomerular endotheliosis + renal vasospasm; usually reversible
7. ***DIC*** — consumption coagulopathy; treat cause (deliver) + blood product support
8. ***Placental abruption (1–4%)*** — decidual vessel rupture → haemorrhage → fetal distress
9. ***VTE*** — dual prothrombotic state; thromboprophylaxis with stockings ± LMWH

**Fetal complications**:
1. ***Preterm delivery (15–67%)*** — most common
2. ***IUGR (10–25%)*** — chronic placental insufficiency
3. ***Perinatal death (1–2%)***
4. ***Long-term cardiovascular morbidity*** — fetal programming / Barker hypothesis
5. Complications of prematurity: RDS, IVH, NEC, ROP, neurodevelopmental delay

**Treatment-related**: MgSO₄ toxicity (low therapeutic index; antidote = calcium gluconate); iatrogenic pulmonary oedema; complications of prematurity from early delivery.

**Long-term maternal**: 2–4× increased lifetime risk of chronic HTN, IHD, stroke, CKD, T2DM. Annual cardiovascular screening recommended.

</Callout>

# Cyanosis (Neonatal/Infant)

## Definition

Cyanosis derives from the Greek *kyanos* (κύανος) meaning "dark blue." It is the visible bluish-purple discolouration of the skin and mucous membranes caused by an increased concentration of deoxygenated haemoglobin (deoxyHb) in the superficial capillary blood.

***Cyanosis is apparent in infants and children when ≥3–5 g/dL of reduced (deoxygenated) haemoglobin is present in the blood*** [1]. This is an absolute concentration — not a percentage — which is why the total haemoglobin concentration profoundly influences clinical detection:

- **Anaemia masks cyanosis**: A severely anaemic neonate (say Hb 8 g/dL) would need >60% desaturation to reach 5 g/dL deoxyHb — by that point the child is near death. So you see **pallor, not cyanosis**.
- **Polycythaemia causes apparent cyanosis**: A polycythaemic neonate (Hb 22 g/dL) only needs ~23% desaturation to reach 5 g/dL deoxyHb — so they look cyanosed at relatively high SpO₂ [1][2].

> ***Note the confounding influence of anaemia or polycythaemia*** — this is explicitly tested and a classic exam pitfall [1].

<Callout title="Central vs Peripheral Cyanosis" type="idea">

| Feature | Central Cyanosis | Peripheral Cyanosis |
|---|---|---|
| **Where to look** | **Tongue**, oral mucosa, lips | Nail beds, fingers, toes, perioral area |
| **Mechanism** | ↑deoxyHb in **arterial** blood (systemic desaturation) | Local ↑O₂ extraction or ↓local blood flow → local ↑deoxyHb |
| **Warm extremities?** | Yes (good perfusion) | No (cold, poorly perfused) |
| **Improves with warming?** | No | Yes (peripheral causes) |
| **Significance** | Always pathological in neonates | May be benign (acrocyanosis of newborn) or pathological (shock) |

In neonates, **acrocyanosis** (bluish hands and feet with a warm, pink trunk) is extremely common in the first 24–48 hours and is benign — it results from peripheral vasomotor instability and high oxygen extraction in cool extremities. **Central cyanosis is always abnormal after the first few minutes of life.**
</Callout>

***'Traumatic' cyanosis*** refers to localised cyanosis of the presenting part (e.g., face, scalp) after a difficult delivery due to venous congestion/ecchymosis — this is benign, self-limiting, and must be distinguished from true central cyanosis [1].

## Epidemiology and Risk Factors

### Epidemiology (Hong Kong Context)

- **Congenital heart disease (CHD)**: Incidence ~8–10 per 1,000 live births globally. In Hong Kong, approximately 800–1,000 new CHD cases per year. Cyanotic CHD accounts for ~25–30% of all CHD presenting in the neonatal period.
- **Critical CHD** (duct-dependent lesions requiring intervention within 28 days): ~2.5–3 per 1,000 live births.
- **Respiratory causes**: Most common overall cause of neonatal cyanosis. Respiratory distress syndrome (RDS) incidence correlates with gestational age — 93% at < 28 weeks, dropping to < 1% at ≥ 38 weeks [2].
- **G6PD deficiency**: Hong Kong prevalence 4.5% in males, 0.5% in females — relevant because severe neonatal jaundice with associated haemolysis can cause anaemia, and G6PD-deficient neonates are screened at birth under the Hong Kong Newborn Screening Programme [3].
- **Persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn (PPHN)**: ~2 per 1,000 live births.

### Risk Factors for Neonatal Cyanosis

| Category | Specific Risk Factors |
|---|---|
| **Prematurity** | RDS (surfactant deficiency), apnoea of prematurity, IVH |
| **Maternal** | Maternal diabetes (RDS, CHD), maternal drug abuse (CNS depression), pethidine/opioids before delivery, maternal rubella (PDA, peripheral PS) |
| **Perinatal** | Birth asphyxia (HIE), meconium-stained liquor (MAS), caesarean section (TTN — lack of thoracic squeeze), prolonged rupture of membranes (sepsis/pneumonia) |
| **Genetic/Syndromic** | Trisomy 21 (AVSD, TOF), Trisomy 13/18 (complex CHD), Turner syndrome (CoA), DiGeorge/22q11 deletion (conotruncal defects — TOF, truncus arteriosus, IAA), Williams syndrome (supravalvar AS, peripheral PS) |
| **Other** | Congenital diaphragmatic hernia (CDH) — usually left-sided with pulmonary hypoplasia, exposure to oxidising agents (methaemoglobinaemia) |

## Anatomy and Physiology: Fetal-to-Neonatal Circulatory Transition

Understanding cyanosis requires understanding **why** the circulation changes at birth, because most cardiac causes of cyanosis relate to failures of this transition.

### Fetal Circulation

In utero, the **placenta** is the gas exchange organ — the lungs are fluid-filled and receive only ~10% of combined ventricular output. Three key shunts exist:

1. **Ductus venosus**: Oxygenated blood from the umbilical vein bypasses hepatic sinusoids → enters IVC directly → streams preferentially across the foramen ovale to the left atrium → LV → ascending aorta → brain and coronary arteries (highest O₂ delivery to the most metabolically active organs).

2. **Foramen ovale**: A flap valve between the atria. In the fetus, right atrial pressure (RAP) slightly exceeds left atrial pressure (LAP) → blood flows R-to-L. The crista dividens on the interatrial septum directs IVC blood (relatively well-oxygenated) preferentially through the foramen ovale.

3. **Ductus arteriosus (DA)**: Connects the main pulmonary artery (distal to the left subclavian artery origin) to the descending aorta. Since pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) is very high in the fetus, ~60% of RV output is shunted R-to-L via the DA into the descending aorta → lower body and placenta [4].

The DA is kept patent by:
- **Low PaO₂** (fetal PaO₂ ~25–30 mmHg)
- **Circulating prostaglandin E₂ (PGE₂)**, produced mainly by the placenta

### Transition at Birth

At the first breath:
1. **Lung expansion** → fluid clearance → dramatic ↓PVR (lungs go from high-resistance to low-resistance vascular bed)
2. **↑PaO₂** from ~30 to ~60–100 mmHg → direct vasoconstriction of the DA smooth muscle
3. **Clamping of umbilical cord** → removal of the low-resistance placental circuit → ↑systemic vascular resistance (SVR) → ↑LAP
4. **↑Pulmonary blood flow** → ↑pulmonary venous return → ↑LAP → LAP > RAP → **functional closure of foramen ovale** (flap valve shuts)
5. **↑PaO₂ + ↓circulating PGE₂** (no more placenta) → **functional closure of DA** within 10–15 hours; anatomical closure (fibrosis → ligamentum arteriosum) by 2–3 weeks [4]

### Why This Matters for Cyanosis

- **Duct-dependent pulmonary circulation** (e.g., critical PS, pulmonary atresia, severe TOF): Pulmonary blood flow depends on L-to-R flow through the DA. When the DA closes → catastrophic ↓pulmonary blood flow → severe cyanosis.
- **Duct-dependent systemic circulation** (e.g., critical coarctation of aorta, hypoplastic left heart syndrome, interrupted aortic arch): Systemic perfusion to the lower body depends on R-to-L flow through the DA. When the DA closes → shock + metabolic acidosis ± differential cyanosis (lower body cyanotic, upper body pink).
- **Transposition physiology** (d-TGA): Two parallel circuits exist. The DA and foramen ovale allow the only mixing between the two circuits. Closure of either → profound cyanosis.

<Callout title="Duct-Dependent Lesions — Life-Saving Concept" type="error">
If you suspect a duct-dependent lesion, **start prostaglandin E₁ (PGE₁/alprostadil) infusion immediately** to keep the DA open. Do NOT wait for echocardiography confirmation. The side effects (apnoea, fever, hypotension) are manageable; ductal closure in these lesions is fatal.
</Callout>

## Etiology of Neonatal/Infant Cyanosis

***The main causes of neonatal cyanosis are classified by system*** [1][2]:

### Systematic Classification

| System | Conditions | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| ***Respiratory*** | ***Congenital pneumonia*** | Early-onset (GBS, E. coli); late-onset (nosocomial) |
| | ***Meconium aspiration syndrome (MAS)*** | Term/post-term; meconium-stained liquor |
| | ***Transient tachypnoea of the newborn (TTN)*** | Post-C/S; retained fetal lung fluid; self-limiting |
| | ***Respiratory distress syndrome (RDS)*** | Preterm; surfactant deficiency |
| | ***Congenital diaphragmatic hernia (CDH)*** | Pulmonary hypoplasia; bowel in thorax |
| ***Cardiovascular*** | ***Cyanotic congenital heart disease*** | See classification below |
| | ***Persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn (PPHN)*** | Failure of PVR to drop; R-to-L shunting at ductal/atrial level |
| ***Neurological*** | ***Asphyxia (Hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy)*** | Birth asphyxia; poor respiratory drive |
| | ***Congenital central hypoventilation syndrome (CCHS)*** | PHOX2B gene mutation; "Ondine's curse" |
| | ***Drugs*** (e.g., maternal drug abuse, pethidine before delivery) | CNS/respiratory depression in neonate |
| | ***Intracranial pathology*** | IVH, seizures → apnoea |
| ***Others*** | ***Congenital methaemoglobinaemia*** | NADH-cytochrome b5 reductase deficiency [5] |
| | ***Septicaemia*** | Poor perfusion + tissue O₂ extraction |
| | ***Hypoglycaemia*** | Impaired CNS function → apnoea/cyanosis |

### Pathophysiology by System

#### A. Respiratory Causes

The fundamental mechanism is **impaired gas exchange at the alveolar level** — whether from V/Q mismatch, shunting, diffusion impairment, or physical obstruction.

**1. Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)**
- Surfactant (produced by type II pneumocytes from ~32 weeks) reduces alveolar surface tension → prevents alveolar collapse at end-expiration.
- In preterm infants < 30–32 weeks: insufficient surfactant → widespread atelectasis → ↓surface area for gas exchange + ↑diffusion distance (thickened hyaline membranes) → hypoxaemia → cyanosis [2].
- The clinical hallmark is **expiratory grunting** — the neonate partially closes the glottis during expiration to auto-PEEP and prevent alveolar collapse.

**2. Meconium Aspiration Syndrome (MAS)**
- Meconium in the airways causes: (a) mechanical obstruction (ball-valve effect → air trapping and hyperinflation distal to obstruction), (b) chemical pneumonitis (inflammation → ↓compliance, ↑capillary permeability), (c) surfactant inactivation, (d) secondary PPHN.
- Typically occurs in term or post-term infants (meconium passage is rare before 34 weeks due to immature gut motility).

**3. Transient Tachypnoea of the Newborn (TTN)**
- During vaginal delivery, thoracic squeeze and catecholamine surge activate ENaC channels on the pulmonary epithelium → rapid resorption of fetal lung fluid.
- In C/S (especially elective without labour): this mechanism is absent → retained fluid → "wet lungs" → ↑airway resistance + ↓compliance → tachypnoea and mild cyanosis.
- Self-limiting (usually resolves within 24–72 hours).

**4. Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia (CDH)**
- Failure of closure of the pleuroperitoneal canal (most commonly the left-sided foramen of Bochdalek, ~85%) → abdominal viscera herniate into the thorax → compression of the ipsilateral (and sometimes contralateral) lung → pulmonary hypoplasia.
- The hypoplastic lung has fewer alveoli AND abnormal pulmonary vasculature (fewer, thicker-walled arterioles) → ↑PVR → PPHN component.
- Presents with **scaphoid abdomen** (bowel is in the chest), respiratory distress, and cyanosis from birth.

**5. Congenital Pneumonia**
- Vertical transmission: GBS, E. coli, Listeria (ascending infection or during passage through birth canal).
- Inflammatory exudate fills alveoli → intrapulmonary shunting (perfused but non-ventilated alveoli) → hypoxaemia.

#### B. Cardiovascular Causes

***Cardiac causes of central cyanosis involve systemic venous blood bypassing the lung*** [1]:

- ***Right-to-left shunts***: Deoxygenated systemic venous blood enters the systemic arterial circulation without passing through the lungs (e.g., TOF, pulmonary atresia with VSD).
- ***Transposition haemodynamics***: Two parallel circuits — systemic venous blood recirculates through the body, pulmonary venous blood recirculates through the lungs. Without mixing (via ASD/VSD/PDA), profound cyanosis occurs [1].
- ***Common mixing conditions***: Blood from pulmonary and systemic venous returns mix at the atrial, ventricular, or arterial level (e.g., TAPVC, univentricular heart, truncus arteriosus) → the resulting "mixed" blood has an intermediate oxygen saturation → cyanosis [1].

***In the majority of cyanotic CHD, there is reduced pulmonary flow*** due to:
- ***Pulmonary outflow obstruction*** (e.g., infundibular/valvar PS in TOF)
- ***Pulmonary atresia*** (no antegrade flow to lungs at all) [1]

#### C. Persistent Pulmonary Hypertension of the Newborn (PPHN)

PPHN is the failure of the normal postnatal decrease in pulmonary vascular resistance. The PVR remains supra-systemic → R-to-L shunting at the level of the DA and/or foramen ovale → systemic desaturation.

Causes/associations: MAS, CDH, sepsis, RDS, perinatal asphyxia, maternal SSRI use (late pregnancy).

Key clinical feature: **labile hypoxaemia** — SpO₂ fluctuates dramatically with handling/stimulation. **Pre- and post-ductal SpO₂ difference > 5–10%** (pre-ductal higher) is characteristic.

#### D. Neurological Causes

The mechanism is **inadequate respiratory drive → hypoventilation → hypercapnia + hypoxaemia → cyanosis**.

- **Asphyxia/HIE**: Hypoxic injury to brainstem respiratory centres → apnoea or shallow, irregular breathing.
- **CCHS (Ondine's curse)**: Autosomal dominant mutation in ***PHOX2B*** gene → absent central chemosensitivity to CO₂ → no drive to breathe, especially during sleep. "CCHS" breaks down: "congenital" = from birth, "central" = brainstem, "hypoventilation" = inadequate breathing.
- **Maternal opioids/pethidine**: Cross the placenta → neonatal CNS depression → apnoea. Reversed by naloxone.

#### E. Methaemoglobinaemia

Methaemoglobin (MetHb) contains iron in the **Fe³⁺ (ferric)** state instead of the normal Fe²⁺ (ferrous) state → cannot bind O₂ → functional anaemia. Additionally, the remaining Fe²⁺ haem groups have ↑O₂ affinity (left-shift of ODC) → impaired O₂ unloading at tissues [5].

Clinical clue: ***Brownish appearance*** — the blood is chocolate-brown and does NOT turn red when exposed to oxygen (unlike deoxyHb, which does) [5].

Causes:
- **Congenital**: NADH-cytochrome b5 reductase deficiency or cytochrome b5 deficiency [5]
- **Acquired**: Drugs/chemicals — dapsone, topical anaesthetics (benzocaine, prilocaine, lidocaine), nitrates/nitrites, metoclopramide, aniline dyes [5]

Treatment: supplementary O₂ + **IV 1% methylene blue** (acts as an electron carrier to reduce Fe³⁺ back to Fe²⁺ via NADPH-MetHb reductase pathway — note: this requires G6PD for NADPH generation, so methylene blue is **contraindicated in G6PD deficiency**) [5].

## Classification of Cyanotic Congenital Heart Disease

***Cyanotic CHD can be classified by mechanism*** [1][2][4]:

| Category | Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| ***RVOT obstruction with shunting*** | ***R-to-L shunt at ventricular level***: Obstruction to pulmonary outflow → RV pressure exceeds LV pressure → blood shunts R-to-L through a VSD | ***Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF)***, ***Pulmonary atresia with VSD (PAVSD)*** |
| | ***R-to-L shunt at atrial level***: Severe RVOT obstruction → blood cannot exit RV → backs up → RA pressure exceeds LA pressure → R-to-L atrial shunt | ***Pulmonary atresia with intact ventricular septum (PAIVS)*** |
| ***Common mixing conditions*** | ***Mixing at atrial/venous level*** | ***Totally anomalous pulmonary venous connection (TAPVC)*** |
| | ***Mixing at ventricular level*** | ***Univentricular heart*** (single ventricle) |
| | ***Mixing at arterial level*** | ***Persistent truncus arteriosus*** |
| ***Transposition physiology*** | Parallel circuits with inadequate mixing | ***d-Transposition of the great arteries (d-TGA)*** |
| ***Other*** | Various | ***Ebstein anomaly*** (severe TR → functional pulmonary atresia → R-to-L atrial shunt), ***Hypoplastic left heart syndrome (HLHS)*** (duct-dependent systemic circulation) |

### Pathophysiology of Key Lesions

#### Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF)

***Most common cause of cyanosis in infancy at 1 year*** [4]. The tetrad:
1. ***Pulmonary stenosis*** (usually infundibular — the key determinant of severity)
2. ***VSD*** (large, non-restrictive, malalignment type)
3. ***Overriding aorta*** (straddles the VSD, receiving blood from both ventricles)
4. ***RV hypertrophy*** (secondary to outflow obstruction)

Pathogenesis: Unequal division of the conotruncal (bulbar) septum → anterior deviation → narrowing of RVOT + failure to fuse with the interventricular septum (creating VSD + overriding aorta) [4].

***The main haemodynamic determinant is RVOT obstruction*** [4]:
- **Severe obstruction** → RV pressure ≥ LV pressure → R-to-L shunting through VSD → cyanosis from birth
- **Moderate obstruction** → balanced shunting → mild or intermittent cyanosis
- **Mild obstruction** → net L-to-R shunting → ***"pink Fallot"*** → presents with heart failure (like a large VSD), not cyanosis [4]

#### d-Transposition of the Great Arteries (d-TGA)

The aorta arises from the RV (anterior) and the pulmonary artery from the LV (posterior) — the great arteries are switched. This creates **two parallel circuits**:
- Systemic circuit: RA → RV → Aorta → Body → RA (deoxygenated blood recirculates)
- Pulmonary circuit: LA → LV → PA → Lungs → LA (oxygenated blood recirculates)

Without mixing between the two circuits, this is incompatible with life. Mixing occurs via:
- **Foramen ovale** (bidirectional shunting)
- **PDA** (allows some mixing)
- **ASD or VSD** if present

d-TGA classically presents with **profound cyanosis within hours of birth** that is **minimally responsive to supplemental O₂** (because the problem is inadequate mixing, not inadequate ventilation). Emergency **Rashkind balloon atrial septostomy** (catheter-based creation/enlargement of ASD) is life-saving to improve mixing.

#### Totally Anomalous Pulmonary Venous Connection (TAPVC)

All four pulmonary veins drain anomalously (not into the LA). Types by drainage site:
- **Supracardiac** (~45%): drain via vertical vein → innominate vein → SVC
- **Cardiac** (~25%): drain into coronary sinus or directly into RA
- **Infracardiac** (~25%): drain below diaphragm (via descending vein → portal vein/IVC/hepatic veins) — **highest risk of obstruction**
- **Mixed** (~5%)

All systemic and pulmonary venous blood mixes in the RA → common mixing → the degree of cyanosis depends on the volume of pulmonary blood flow (more pulmonary flow → better mixed → less cyanosis). An ASD/PFO is obligatory for survival (allows mixed blood to reach LA → LV → systemic circulation).

**Obstructed TAPVC** (especially infracardiac) presents as a **neonatal emergency** with severe cyanosis, pulmonary oedema, and a CXR that can mimic RDS (***hazy lung fields***) [2]. This is a surgical emergency.

#### Hypoplastic Left Heart Syndrome (HLHS)

The left-sided structures (mitral valve, LV, aortic valve, ascending aorta) are severely underdeveloped. Systemic circulation is entirely dependent on:
1. R-to-L shunting at the atrial level (through ASD/PFO) to receive pulmonary venous return
2. R-to-L shunting through the DA to supply the systemic circulation

Ductal closure → cardiovascular collapse, not primarily cyanosis (it's a duct-dependent *systemic* circulation). Cyanosis is mild-to-moderate because of common mixing in the RA.

## Clinical Features

### A. Symptoms (with Pathophysiological Basis)

| Symptom | Pathophysiological Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Cyanosis from birth** | Suggests duct-dependent pulmonary circulation (e.g., critical PS, PAIVS, severe TOF) or transposition physiology (d-TGA) — immediate systemic desaturation |
| **Cyanosis developing over days/weeks** | Progressive RVOT obstruction (infundibular hypertrophy in TOF) or gradual ductal closure in duct-dependent lesions |
| **Worsening cyanosis with crying/feeding** | ↑Oxygen consumption + ↑intrathoracic pressure → ↑R-to-L shunting (especially in TOF — dynamic infundibular obstruction) |
| ***Hypercyanotic (Tet/Fallot) spells*** | ***Transient near-occlusion of RVOT*** → profound R-to-L shunting → severe cyanosis, often with irritability then limpness/syncope. Typically in TOF, usually 2–4 months of age [4]. Spasm of the infundibular muscle + ↓SVR (crying, dehydration) → vicious cycle |
| **Tachypnoea/respiratory distress** | If respiratory cause: direct lung pathology. If cardiac cause: pulmonary oedema from obstructed TAPVC, or metabolic acidosis from tissue hypoxia driving compensatory hyperventilation. Note: tachypnoea WITHOUT significant retractions ("happy tachypnoea") suggests cardiac rather than pulmonary disease |
| **Poor feeding / failure to thrive** | ↑metabolic demand from chronic hypoxaemia + heart failure; feeding requires significant O₂ consumption (coordinated suck-swallow-breathe) |
| **Diaphoresis during feeds** | Sympathetic activation due to heart failure and ↑work of feeding |
| ***Fallot's sign: squatting*** | ***Older children with TOF assume a squatting position after exercise. Mechanism: ↑SVR (kinking femoral arteries) → ↓R-to-L shunting; ↑venous return (compressing venous capacitance) → ↑pulmonary blood flow*** [4] |
| **Apnoea / shallow breathing** | CNS causes: asphyxia, drugs, CCHS → inadequate respiratory drive |
| **Seizures** | Severe hypoxaemia → cerebral hypoxia; or underlying intracranial pathology |

### B. Signs (with Pathophysiological Basis)

#### General Inspection

| Sign | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| ***Central cyanosis*** (tongue, oral mucosa) | ≥3–5 g/dL deoxyHb in arterial blood [1] |
| **Peripheral cyanosis / acrocyanosis** | Peripheral vasoconstriction + high O₂ extraction in extremities — benign in first 48 hours of life |
| **Clubbing** | Chronic hypoxaemia (usually develops after several months) → megakaryocyte fragments bypass lung filtration → lodge in nail bed → release PDGF/VEGF → soft tissue hypertrophy. Uncommon in neonates (takes months to develop) |
| **Failure to thrive / poor growth** | Chronic hypoxaemia + heart failure → ↑metabolic rate, ↓caloric intake |
| ***Scaphoid abdomen*** | CDH — bowel contents herniated into thorax → empty abdomen |
| **Plethoric / ruddy appearance** | Polycythaemia (secondary to chronic hypoxaemia → ↑EPO → ↑RBC mass) |

#### Respiratory Examination

| Sign | Significance/Mechanism |
|---|---|
| ***Shallow breathing and apnoea*** | ***→ CNS cause*** [2] |
| ***Respiratory distress*** (tachypnoea, grunting, nasal flaring, intercostal/subcostal recession) | ***→ Respiratory cause*** [2]. Grunting = auto-PEEP to prevent alveolar collapse. Recession = high negative intrathoracic pressure generation against non-compliant lungs |
| ***Normal breathing pattern (± murmurs)*** | ***→ CVS cause*** [2] — cardiac cyanosis typically presents with "comfortable" tachypnoea without significant respiratory distress (until decompensation) |
| **Unilateral ↓air entry + shifted mediastinum** | CDH (bowel sounds in chest) or tension pneumothorax |
| **Widespread crackles** | Pulmonary oedema (obstructed TAPVC, HLHS, large L-to-R shunts with failure) or pneumonia |

<Callout title="Key Differentiating Clue on the Ward" type="idea">
***The breathing pattern helps differentiate causes*** [2]:
- **Shallow/apnoeic** → think CNS
- **Distressed (grunting, retracting)** → think lungs
- **Comfortable tachypnoea, no distress, ± murmur** → think heart

This is a classic clinical pearl and is explicitly tested.
</Callout>

#### Cardiovascular Examination

| Sign | Lesion(s) | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| ***Single S2 / inaudible P2*** | TOF, pulmonary atresia, truncus arteriosus | P2 is soft/absent because the pulmonary valve is stenotic/atretic → reduced flow across it → reduced closing sound [4] |
| **Loud S2** | d-TGA (anterior aorta transmits closure sound loudly), PPHN (loud P2 from ↑PVR) | |
| ***Ejection systolic murmur (ESM) at left upper sternal border (LUSB)*** | TOF (due to PS), critical PS | ***Due to pulmonary stenosis, NOT the VSD*** (which is too large/non-restrictive to generate a significant gradient → soft PSM) [4] |
| ***Murmur disappears during Tet spell*** | TOF | ***↑RVOT obstruction → ↓pulmonary flow → ↓turbulence across RVOT → softer/absent murmur*** [4] |
| **No murmur** | d-TGA (without VSD), PPHN, TAPVC | Equal pressures across septum or parallel circuits without turbulence |
| **Continuous murmur** | PDA (if present and large) | Continuous pressure gradient aorta → PA throughout cardiac cycle (systole and diastole) [4] |
| **Hepatomegaly** | Right heart failure | Venous congestion → hepatic distension |
| **Gallop rhythm (S3)** | Volume overload / heart failure | Rapid ventricular filling into a failing ventricle |
| ***Precordial bulge + RV impulse ± systolic thrill*** | TOF, other lesions with RV pressure overload | Chronic RV pressure overload → RVH → prominent parasternal impulse [4] |

#### Pre-ductal vs Post-ductal SpO₂

| Pattern | Interpretation | Example Lesions |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-ductal SpO₂ > post-ductal SpO₂** (difference > 5–10%) | R-to-L ductal shunting → deoxygenated blood enters descending aorta | PPHN, CoA with ductal shunting, IAA |
| **Both pre- and post-ductal SpO₂ low, no difference** | Complete intracardiac mixing or transposition | d-TGA, TAPVC, truncus arteriosus, univentricular heart |
| **Post-ductal SpO₂ > pre-ductal SpO₂** (reverse differential) | Rare: d-TGA with CoA or d-TGA with PPHN → oxygenated blood from LV enters PA → DA → descending aorta | d-TGA + CoA |

> **Pre-ductal** = right hand/right ear (supplied by aorta before DA insertion); **Post-ductal** = either foot (supplied by aorta after DA insertion). The left hand can be pre- or post-ductal depending on the origin of the left subclavian artery relative to the DA.

### C. Differentiation Between Causes — Clinical Approach

***The differentiation between respiratory, cardiovascular, and CNS causes uses a combination of clinical features, CXR, hyperoxia test, and bedside echocardiography*** [2]:

| Tool | Respiratory | Cardiovascular | CNS |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Clinical** | ***Respiratory distress*** | ***Normal breathing ± murmurs*** | ***Shallow breathing / apnoea*** |
| ***CXR*** | ***Hazy lung fields*** (parenchymal disease) | ***Dark/oligaemic lung fields*** (most cyanotic CHD with ↓pulmonary flow); OR cardiomegaly | Normal lung fields |
| | | ***Hazy lung fields with small heart in obstructed TAPVC*** (mimics RDS — classic trap!) [2] | |
| ***Hyperoxia (nitrogen washout) test*** | ***PaO₂ > 150 mmHg (>20 kPa) after 10 min of 100% O₂*** (parenchymal disease responds) | ***PaO₂ remains < 100 mmHg (< 15 kPa)*** — fixed R-to-L shunt not responsive to ↑FiO₂ | Intermediate response (may improve with O₂ but depends on degree of hypoventilation) |
| ***Bedside echo*** | Normal cardiac structures | ***Directly visualises cardiac defects*** [2] | Normal |

#### The Hyperoxia Test — In Detail

***Traditionally used to differentiate respiratory vs cardiovascular causes*** [2]:

- **Method**: Administer ***100% O₂ for 10 minutes*** → measure ***PaO₂ from right radial artery*** (pre-ductal, to avoid confounding by ductal shunting) [2].
- **Interpretation**:
  - ***PaO₂ > 150 mmHg (> 20 kPa)***: Lung pathology — the O₂ can reach alveoli and diffuse into blood
  - ***PaO₂ < 100 mmHg (< 15 kPa)***: Fixed R-to-L shunt (cyanotic CHD) — supplemental O₂ cannot oxygenate blood that bypasses the lungs
  - Intermediate values: PPHN, some mixing lesions

<Callout title="Danger of the Hyperoxia Test" type="error">
***↑FiO₂ can promote ductal closure in duct-dependent circulation → potentially dangerous!*** [2] High oxygen is a potent stimulus for DA constriction. If the infant has a duct-dependent pulmonary lesion, the hyperoxia test may precipitate ductal closure and cardiovascular collapse. Always have PGE₁ ready and perform the test with caution. Many centres now prefer **bedside echocardiography** as the primary diagnostic tool, as it ***directly visualises cardiac defects*** without the risk of ductal closure [2].
</Callout>

### D. Specific CXR Patterns

| CXR Appearance | Likely Diagnosis |
|---|---|
| **"Boot-shaped" heart** (coeur en sabot) + oligaemic lungs | TOF — upturned apex from RVH + concave PA segment |
| **"Egg-on-a-string"** (narrow mediastinum) + plethoric lungs | d-TGA — narrow pedicle because great arteries are parallel (not crossed), ↑pulmonary flow |
| **"Snowman" / "figure-of-8"** sign + plethoric lungs | Supracardiac TAPVC — dilated vertical vein + innominate vein + SVC form the "snowman" silhouette |
| **Hazy lung fields + small heart** | Obstructed TAPVC (mimics RDS) |
| **Massive cardiomegaly ("wall-to-wall" heart)** | Ebstein anomaly (massively dilated RA from atrialised RV) |
| **Bowel gas in thorax + absent diaphragm** | CDH |
| **Ground-glass/reticulogranular + air bronchograms** | RDS |

| Heart rate (bpm) | 100–170 | 100–170 | 100–160 | 100–150 |
| Respiratory rate | 30–60 | 30–50 | 25–40 | 20–30 |
| SpO₂ (normal) | ≥95% (by 10 min of life) | ≥95% | ≥95% | ≥95% |
| Hb (g/dL) | 14–20 | 10–14 | 9.5–13.5 | 10–13 |
| Systolic BP (mmHg) | 60–80 | 70–100 | 80–100 | 80–100 |

> The neonatal SpO₂ screening cut-off for critical CHD is **< 95% in either limb** or **> 3% difference between pre- and post-ductal SpO₂**, measured after 24 hours of age or before discharge.

## Family-Centred Care and Communication

- **Communication with caregivers**: Parents of a cyanotic newborn are terrified. Explain simply: "Your baby's blood is not carrying enough oxygen. We are doing tests to find out why and starting treatment to help."
- **Consent/assent**: In neonates, consent is obtained from parents/legal guardians. For emergency interventions (PGE₁ infusion, Rashkind septostomy), proceed under emergency consent provisions while informing parents as soon as possible.
- **Involve the family**: Encourage skin-to-skin contact when safe, support breastfeeding (expressed breast milk if baby cannot feed), explain every procedure.
- **Developmental considerations**: Chronic cyanosis affects neurodevelopmental outcomes — ensure early referral for developmental follow-up.

<Callout title="High Yield Summary">

1. **Cyanosis = ≥3–5 g/dL deoxyHb** → influenced by total Hb (anaemia masks it, polycythaemia exaggerates it)
2. **Central vs peripheral**: Central = tongue/mucosa (arterial desaturation, always pathological); Peripheral = extremities (may be benign acrocyanosis)
3. **Three main categories**: Respiratory (most common overall), Cardiovascular (cyanotic CHD), Neurological (CNS depression/apnoea)
4. **Clinical differentiation**: Respiratory distress → lungs; Normal breathing + murmur → heart; Apnoea/shallow breathing → CNS
5. **CXR patterns**: Oligaemic lungs → cyanotic CHD with ↓pulmonary flow; Hazy lungs → respiratory disease OR obstructed TAPVC (trap!)
6. **Hyperoxia test**: PaO₂ > 150 mmHg = lungs; < 100 mmHg = cyanotic CHD. CAUTION: can close the ductus
7. **Duct-dependent lesions**: Start PGE₁ immediately if suspected — do NOT wait for echo
8. **TOF**: Most common cyanotic CHD beyond neonatal period; severity depends on RVOT obstruction; Tet spells = RVOT spasm → severe cyanosis
9. **d-TGA**: Parallel circuits; needs mixing (PDA/ASD) to survive; Rashkind septostomy is life-saving
10. **PPHN**: Failure of postnatal ↓PVR; labile SpO₂; pre-post ductal SpO₂ difference
11. **MetHb**: Chocolate-brown blood not responsive to O₂; treat with methylene blue (contraindicated in G6PD deficiency)
12. **Pre-ductal SpO₂** = right hand; **Post-ductal** = foot
</Callout>

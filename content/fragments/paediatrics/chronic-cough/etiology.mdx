## Definition

Chronic cough in children is **not** defined the same way as in adults — this is a critical distinction for paediatric practice.

- ***American College of Chest Physicians (ACCP) Guideline (Chest 2006)***: **Paediatric ( < 15 years) chronic cough is defined as a daily cough lasting for > 4 weeks** [1].
- ***British Thoracic Society (BTS, Thorax 2007)***: Defines chronic cough as **a cough lasting > 8 weeks**, and recognises a **'grey' area of 'subacute cough' between 2–8 weeks** [1].
- ***The 4-week time frame was chosen because of the natural history of URI in children (lasting 1–3 weeks)***; ***1 study showed 10% of preschool children are still coughing 25 days after a URI*** [1].
- ***This is different from the definition in adults*** (where chronic cough is typically > 8 weeks) [1].

The BTS further **subdivides** chronic cough into:
- ***Specific cough***: **cough with signs and symptoms suggestive of an associated problem** [1].
- ***Nonspecific cough***: **dry cough in the absence of an identifiable respiratory disease of known aetiology** [1].

> The word "cough" comes from Old English *cohhian*. Understanding the reflex: cough is a protective mechanism — a forceful expulsion of air from the lungs via a closed glottis that suddenly opens. It serves to clear the airways of secretions, foreign bodies, and irritants.

<Callout title="Why is the paediatric definition shorter (4 weeks vs 8 weeks)?">
Children have a different natural history of respiratory infections compared to adults. Post-viral cough in children typically resolves within 1–3 weeks, and by 4 weeks most self-limiting causes have resolved. Using 4 weeks as a cut-off ensures earlier investigation of potentially serious pathology (e.g., cystic fibrosis, foreign body, primary ciliary dyskinesia) in a population that cannot always articulate symptoms clearly.
</Callout>

## Epidemiology

### Global and Hong Kong Context
- Cough is the **single most common reason** for paediatric outpatient visits globally.
- Chronic cough prevalence in children varies widely by region: approximately **5–10%** of school-age children report chronic cough in community surveys [2].
- In **Hong Kong**, the high population density, subtropical climate (favouring house dust mite sensitisation), and air pollution contribute to a high burden of atopic disease (including asthma — prevalence ~8.6%, with a decreasing trend) [2][3].
- ***Causes of cough in children may be different from adults*** [1].
- ***Manifestations may be different from adults*** [1].
- Post-infectious cough (following viral URTIs) is the most common transient cause in young children — preschool children have an average of **6–8 URTIs per year**, each potentially associated with cough lasting 1–3 weeks, meaning a "normal" child can appear to cough almost continuously through winter months.

### Age-Related Patterns
| Age Group | Common Aetiologies |
|---|---|
| Infants (0–1 year) | Congenital anomalies (tracheo-oesophageal fistula, vascular ring), aspiration (GORD, swallowing dysfunction), cystic fibrosis, primary ciliary dyskinesia |
| Toddlers (1–3 years) | **Foreign body aspiration** (peak age!), post-infectious cough, asthma, protracted bacterial bronchitis |
| Preschool (3–5 years) | Recurrent viral-induced wheeze, asthma, protracted bacterial bronchitis |
| School-age (5–12 years) | Asthma, post-nasal drip (allergic rhinitis), psychogenic/habit cough |
| Adolescents (12–18 years) | Asthma, GORD, psychogenic cough, smoking/vaping |

## Risk Factors

### Host Factors
- **Atopy / family history of atopy**: Strongest risk factor for asthma-related chronic cough. Atopic children have airway hyper-responsiveness due to Th2-driven inflammation → eosinophilic infiltration → mucosal oedema and mucus hypersecretion [2][3].
- **Prematurity / bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD)**: Damaged, remodelled airways from barotrauma and oxygen toxicity in the neonatal period → chronic cough and wheeze.
- **Immunodeficiency**: Primary immunodeficiency (PID) → recurrent/chronic lower respiratory infections → chronic cough with suppurative lung disease (see GC 144) [4].
- **Congenital airway anomalies**: Tracheomalacia, bronchomalacia, vascular rings → chronic barking or brassy cough from infancy.
- **Neurodevelopmental abnormality**: ***Aspiration lung disease*** — children with cerebral palsy, bulbar dysfunction, or severe developmental delay have impaired swallowing coordination → chronic aspiration → recurrent pneumonia and chronic wet cough [1].
- **Genetic**: Cystic fibrosis (CFTR mutation, AR inheritance — commoner in Caucasians, incidence ~1/2500 live births, but rarer in Hong Kong Chinese; ~1/25,000 in East Asians), primary ciliary dyskinesia (AR, ~1/15,000) [3].
- **Obesity**: Increasingly relevant in Hong Kong children; obesity → ↑ intra-abdominal pressure → GORD → chronic cough; also associated with more difficult-to-control asthma [2].

### Environmental Factors
- **Tobacco smoke exposure (passive smoking)**: Impairs mucociliary clearance → chronic airway irritation; strongly associated with chronic cough and wheezing in children.
- **Air pollution**: Outdoor (PM2.5, NO₂) and indoor (incense burning, cooking fumes — relevant in Hong Kong) → airway inflammation.
- **Allergens**: ***Indoor allergens: fecal pellets of house dust mites, pets, cockroaches*** [2][3]. House dust mites thrive in Hong Kong's humid subtropical climate. ***Outdoor allergens: Alternaria (a genus of Ascomycete fungi)*** [2][3].
- **Infections**: Recurrent viral URTIs are the most common trigger for cough. Pertussis (whooping cough) must always be considered, even in vaccinated children (waning immunity).
- ***Exercise, cold air*** — triggers for cough-variant asthma [2][3].

## Anatomy and Physiology of the Cough Reflex (Paediatric Considerations)

### The Cough Reflex Arc
Cough is a **vagally-mediated reflex** involving:

1. **Afferent limb**: Sensory receptors (chemoreceptors, mechanoreceptors, C-fibre nociceptors) in the mucosa of the pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi, and lung parenchyma → signals travel via the **vagus nerve (CN X)** (and to a lesser extent glossopharyngeal nerve CN IX for pharyngeal receptors) to the **cough centre** in the medulla oblongata [5].
2. **Central processing**: The **nucleus tractus solitarius** (NTS) in the medulla integrates afferent signals and generates the cough motor pattern.
3. **Efferent limb**: Motor output via the vagus nerve (to larynx and bronchial smooth muscle), phrenic nerve (diaphragm), and spinal motor nerves (intercostal and abdominal muscles).

### Phases of Cough
1. **Inspiratory phase**: Deep inspiration to maximise lung volume.
2. **Compressive phase**: Glottis closes, expiratory muscles contract → intrathoracic pressure rises to ~300 mmHg.
3. **Expulsive phase**: Glottis opens abruptly → air expelled at velocities up to 500 mph → clears mucus, foreign material.

### Why Paediatric Airways Are Different
- **Smaller absolute airway calibre**: By Poiseuille's law, resistance is inversely proportional to the fourth power of the radius (R ∝ 1/r⁴). Even minor mucosal oedema (e.g., 1 mm swelling) causes a proportionally much greater increase in resistance in a small child's airway than in an adult's. This is why children are more symptomatic with viral infections.
- **More compliant chest wall**: Infants have a very compliant rib cage (cartilaginous ribs) → less effective at maintaining negative intrathoracic pressure → more prone to airway collapse, especially during expiration.
- **Fewer collateral ventilation channels**: Pores of Kohn and canals of Lambert are underdeveloped in young children → atelectasis occurs more readily when airways are obstructed (e.g., mucus plugging, foreign body).
- **Immature immune system**: Lower levels of secretory IgA, fewer memory T and B cells → more frequent and sometimes more severe respiratory infections.
- **Higher metabolic rate and oxygen consumption**: Children desaturate faster during respiratory compromise.

<Callout title="Paediatric Airway Pearl" type="idea">
A 1 mm circumferential mucosal swelling in an infant's trachea (4 mm diameter) reduces the cross-sectional area by ~75% and increases resistance by ~16-fold. The same 1 mm swelling in an adult trachea (16 mm) reduces area by only ~44%. This is why croup in a toddler causes stridor but is trivial in an adult.
</Callout>

## Aetiology (Focus on Hong Kong)

The causes of chronic cough in children are **fundamentally different** from those in adults. In adults, the classic triad of post-nasal drip, asthma, and GORD accounts for most chronic cough. In children, the differential is broader and the approach must be age-stratified.

### Classification by Cough Character

***The BTS subdivides chronic cough into specific cough and nonspecific cough*** [1]:

#### A. Specific Cough — Associated with Pointers to Underlying Disease

***Specific cough is associated with signs and symptoms of an underlying respiratory or systemic disease*** [1]:

| ***Abnormality*** | ***Examples of Aetiology*** |
|---|---|
| ***Wheeze*** | ***Intrathoracic airway lesion (e.g., asthma, foreign body)*** |
| ***Crepitations*** | ***Parenchymal disease*** |
| ***Chest pain*** | ***Arrhythmia, asthma, increased respiratory distress (parenchymal disease)*** |
| ***Chest wall deformity*** | ***Chronic airway or parenchymal disease*** |
| ***Digital clubbing*** | ***Chronic suppurative lung disease*** |
| ***Daily moist/productive cough*** | ***Suppurative lung disease*** |
| ***Failure to thrive*** | ***Serious systemic including pulmonary illness*** |
| ***Feeding difficulties*** | ***Serious systemic including pulmonary illness, aspiration*** |
| ***Hypoxia/cyanosis*** | ***Airway or parenchymal disease, cardiac disease*** |
| ***Neurodevelopmental abnormality*** | ***Aspiration lung disease*** |
| ***Recurrent pneumonia*** | ***Immunodeficiency, congenital lung abnormalities, tracheo-oesophageal H fistula*** |

#### B. Nonspecific Cough
- **Dry cough** in an otherwise well child, with **no identifiable respiratory disease** and **normal growth and examination findings**.
- Often represents **post-viral cough**, **habit/psychogenic cough**, or early/mild **cough-variant asthma**.

### Major Aetiologies and Their Pathophysiology

#### 1. Asthma / Cough-Variant Asthma

- **Most common cause** of chronic cough in school-age children in Hong Kong.
- ***Asthma is defined as a chronic inflammatory disorder of airways, leading to widespread but variable airflow obstruction, that is often reversible either spontaneously or with treatment. Inflammation is associated with bronchial hyper-responsiveness to a variety of stimuli*** [2][3].
- ***Atopic asthma***: **predisposition to synthesize IgE to common allergens**, ***usually in children and young adults with history of infantile eczema and allergic rhinitis*** [2][3].
- **Cough-variant asthma**: Cough is the **sole or predominant** symptom — ***may be the only manifestation in cough-variant asthma*** [5]. The cough is typically **dry, worse at night or with exercise/cold air/allergen exposure**, **intermittent**, and **sometimes productive with mucoid sputum** [5].
- **Pathophysiology**: Allergen exposure → Th2 cell activation → IL-4, IL-5, IL-13 release → IgE production (IL-4), eosinophil recruitment (IL-5), mucus hypersecretion and airway remodelling (IL-13) → bronchial smooth muscle contraction + mucosal oedema + mucus plugging → variable airflow obstruction → cough triggered by stimulation of airway sensory receptors by inflammation and mucus.

#### 2. Protracted Bacterial Bronchitis (PBB)

- **Most common cause of chronic wet cough in preschool children** (ages 1–6 years) — increasingly recognised in Hong Kong.
- **Definition**: Chronic wet cough > 4 weeks duration, absence of specific cough pointers, resolution of cough with a **2–4 week course of appropriate antibiotics** (amoxicillin-clavulanate).
- **Pathophysiology**: Persistent bacterial infection (commonly non-typeable *Haemophilus influenzae*, *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Moraxella catarrhalis*) of the lower airways → neutrophilic inflammation of the bronchial mucosa → impaired mucociliary clearance → chronic wet cough. If untreated, may progress to bronchiectasis.
- **Why does it persist?** Unlike adults, children's immature immune systems and smaller airways with fewer collateral ventilation channels predispose to bacterial persistence in the lower airways even after the initial viral infection has cleared.

#### 3. Post-Infectious Cough

- Cough persisting after an acute respiratory infection (typically viral URTI), lasting > 4 weeks but usually self-limiting by 6–8 weeks.
- **Pathophysiology**: Viral infection damages the respiratory epithelium → exposed sensory nerve endings (C-fibres) → transient airway hyper-responsiveness → heightened cough reflex sensitivity. Epithelial regeneration takes weeks.
- Common viruses: rhinovirus, RSV, influenza, parainfluenza, adenovirus, Mycoplasma pneumoniae (technically atypical bacteria), Bordetella pertussis.

#### 4. Upper Airway Cough Syndrome (UACS) / Post-Nasal Drip

- Previously called "post-nasal drip syndrome."
- **Pathophysiology**: Allergic rhinitis (very common in Hong Kong — prevalence ~40% in children) or chronic rhinosinusitis → mucus drips posteriorly from the nasopharynx onto the pharyngeal and laryngeal mucosa → stimulates cough receptors → chronic cough with throat clearing. The cough is typically described as a **throat-clearing, hawking type** [5].
- Children may also have nasal congestion, sneezing, itchy eyes (allergic triad), halitosis, and mouth breathing.

#### 5. Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD)

- ***GORD is a condition which develops when the reflux of stomach contents causes troublesome symptoms and/or complications*** [6][7].
- ***Extra-oesophageal symptoms include chronic cough, hoarseness, throat tightness (laryngopharyngeal reflux)*** [7].
- **Pathophysiology in children**: Reflux of acidic gastric content → direct stimulation of oesophageal vagal afferents → cough reflex (oesophago-bronchial reflex). Additionally, micro-aspiration of refluxate into the larynx/trachea → direct chemical irritation of airway mucosa → chronic cough and hoarseness.
- In **infants**, physiological GOR is extremely common (resolves by 12–18 months in most). GORD is diagnosed when reflux causes **symptoms** (irritability, feeding refusal, failure to thrive, apnoea, chronic cough) or **complications** (oesophagitis, aspiration pneumonia, Sandifer syndrome).
- Risk factors include ***increased intra-abdominal pressure (obesity, chronic cough — note the bidirectional relationship!), chronic constipation*** [6][7].

#### 6. Foreign Body Aspiration

- **Peak age: toddlers (1–3 years)** — this is when children are mobile, putting everything in their mouths, but have immature swallowing coordination and incomplete dentition (poor grinding of food).
- **Pathophysiology**: Foreign body lodges in a bronchus (usually **right main bronchus** in older children due to its wider diameter and more vertical orientation; but in young children the angle difference is less pronounced) → initial choking episode (may be witnessed or unwitnessed) → partial obstruction → **ball-valve mechanism** (air enters on inspiration when bronchus dilates, but cannot escape on expiration when bronchus narrows around the FB) → air trapping → hyperinflation of the affected lobe. Over time, persistent obstruction → atelectasis, recurrent infection, and granulation tissue formation → chronic cough (often wet), recurrent pneumonia in the same lobe, and unilateral wheeze.
- ***A child with wheeze may have a foreign body*** [1].

<Callout title="Foreign Body — The 'Missed Diagnosis'" type="error">
Up to 40% of foreign body aspirations are initially missed. Always ask about a choking episode. If a child has chronic cough with unilateral wheeze or recurrent pneumonia localised to one lobe, think foreign body until proven otherwise, even if the initial choking event was weeks ago.
</Callout>

#### 7. Cystic Fibrosis (CF)

- ***Life-threatening condition due to CFTR mutation (AR inheritance), most common in Caucasians (incidence 1/2500 live births, carrier rate 1/25)*** [3].
- Rare in Hong Kong Chinese (~1/25,000), but still must be considered in any child with chronic wet cough, failure to thrive, and recurrent lower respiratory infections.
- ***Classical triad: elevated sweat chloride + recurrent lung infections + pancreatic insufficiency*** [3].
- ***Pathophysiology: CFTR gene on chromosome 7 codes for a cAMP-dependent chloride channel → abnormal ion transport across epithelial cells → impaired ciliary function → retention of thick, mucopurulent secretions → increased risk of lung infections*** [3].
- ***Lung disease: vicious cycle of infection, inflammation, and lung damage → chronic/recurrent lung infections ± bronchiectasis*** [3].
- ***Common pathogens: S. aureus, H. influenzae (early), P. aeruginosa, Burkholderia cepacia (late)*** [3].
- ***Presentation: persistent wet cough, copious purulent sputum ± haemoptysis, pneumothorax; PE: clubbing, chest hyperinflation, coarse inspiratory crackles ± expiratory wheeze*** [3].

#### 8. Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia (PCD)

- AR condition (~1/15,000) caused by defective motile cilia ultrastructure (e.g., absent or dysmorphic dynein arms) → impaired mucociliary clearance.
- "Dyskinesia" → "dys" = abnormal, "kinesia" = movement — the cilia move abnormally or not at all.
- **Clinical features**: Chronic wet cough from infancy, recurrent otitis media, chronic rhinosinusitis, bronchiectasis, and **situs inversus** in ~50% (Kartagener syndrome = PCD + situs inversus + bronchiectasis).
- **Neonatal history**: Often unexplained neonatal respiratory distress at term.

#### 9. Bronchiectasis

- Irreversible dilatation of bronchi due to chronic/recurrent infection and inflammation → destruction of bronchial wall elastic tissue and smooth muscle.
- ***Chronic, very productive with blood; increased yellow foul-smelling sputum in postural changes; halitosis, chronic SOBOE and wasting*** [5].
- **Causes in children**: Cystic fibrosis, PCD, post-infectious (adenovirus, measles, pertussis, TB), immunodeficiency, foreign body.
- **Pathophysiology**: The "vicious cycle" — infection → inflammation → airway wall destruction → impaired clearance → more infection.

#### 10. Psychogenic / Habit Cough

- Diagnosis of exclusion — **school-age children and adolescents**.
- Characteristically: **loud, honking/barking, repetitive, dry** cough that is present **only when awake** (completely absent during sleep — this is the key diagnostic clue).
- No underlying organic disease; often related to stress or anxiety.
- Not responsive to cough suppressants or bronchodilators.

#### 11. Aspiration Syndromes

- ***Neurodevelopmental abnormality → aspiration lung disease*** [1].
- ***Feeding difficulties → serious systemic including pulmonary illness, aspiration*** [1].
- Causes include: H-type tracheo-oesophageal fistula (subtle — presents with chronic cough and recurrent pneumonia rather than the obvious atresia presentation), laryngeal cleft, swallowing dysfunction (cerebral palsy, myopathy), GORD with aspiration.
- **Pathophysiology**: Aspiration of oropharyngeal/gastric contents into the tracheobronchial tree → chemical pneumonitis (acidic material) and/or bacterial infection → chronic wet cough, recurrent pneumonia (often in dependent lung zones — right middle and lower lobes in upright position, posterior segments of upper lobes in supine position).

#### 12. Tuberculosis (TB)

- Highly relevant in Hong Kong (intermediate TB burden area, ~4000 notified cases/year).
- Paediatric TB often presents with chronic cough, failure to thrive, and low-grade fever.
- ***Long history of productive cough, often with blood; associated with low-grade fever and constitutional symptoms*** [5].
- **Primary TB** in children often involves hilar lymphadenopathy → can compress bronchi → chronic cough, lobar collapse.
- Always consider TB in any Hong Kong child with chronic cough, especially with TB contact history or immigrant/refugee background.

#### 13. Immunodeficiency

- ***Recurrent pneumonia → immunodeficiency, congenital lung abnormalities, tracheo-oesophageal H fistula*** [1].
- Primary immunodeficiency (PID) should be considered in children with chronic cough + recurrent sinopulmonary infections + failure to thrive [4].
- Examples: common variable immunodeficiency (CVID), X-linked agammaglobulinaemia (Bruton's), IgA deficiency, chronic granulomatous disease.

#### 14. Congenital Anomalies

- Tracheomalacia/bronchomalacia: Inadequate cartilaginous support → dynamic airway collapse → chronic barking/brassy cough, especially with crying or feeding.
- Vascular ring (e.g., double aortic arch, aberrant right subclavian with ligamentum arteriosum): Compresses trachea/oesophagus → stridor + chronic cough + dysphagia.
- Congenital pulmonary airway malformation (CPAM): May present with recurrent infection in the same lung segment.

#### 15. Medications

- ***ACE inhibitors***: Dry cough occurring 1–2 weeks after initiation of therapy; ***due to decreased breakdown of bradykinin by ACE*** [5]. Less commonly used in children than adults, but relevant in children with hypertension or heart failure.

### By Duration
| Category | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Acute cough | < 2 weeks | Usually viral URTI |
| Subacute cough | 2–4 weeks (ACCP) or 2–8 weeks (BTS) | The "grey area" — often post-infectious |
| ***Chronic cough*** | ***> 4 weeks (ACCP) or > 8 weeks (BTS)*** | Requires systematic evaluation |

### By Character
| Type | Description | Implications |
|---|---|---|
| **Dry cough** | Non-productive, no sputum | Asthma, cough-variant asthma, post-viral, habit cough, ILD, ACEI |
| **Wet/productive cough** | Associated with sputum/mucus | Suppurative lung disease (PBB, bronchiectasis, CF, PCD), aspiration |
| **Barking/croupy cough** | Harsh, seal-like | Croup (acute), tracheomalacia, subglottic pathology |
| **Staccato cough** | Short, choppy | Chlamydial pneumonia in young infants |
| **Paroxysmal/whooping cough** | Spasms of coughing followed by inspiratory "whoop" | Pertussis, parapertussis |
| **Brassy/honking cough** | Loud, metallic quality | Tracheal/large airway pathology, habit cough, psychogenic |

### By Aetiology (Broad Categories)
1. **Infectious** (post-viral, PBB, TB, pertussis, chronic suppurative lung disease)
2. **Allergic/Atopic** (asthma, allergic rhinitis/UACS)
3. **Structural/Congenital** (airway anomalies, vascular ring, foreign body)
4. **Genetic** (CF, PCD)
5. **Aspiration** (GORD, swallowing dysfunction, TEF)
6. **Immunodeficiency** (primary or secondary)
7. **Psychogenic/Habit**
8. **Drug-induced** (ACEI)

## Clinical Features

***A child with a cough may have a self-limiting problem or a serious illness*** [1].
***Importance of history and physical examination*** [1].
***All children with chronic cough should have thorough clinical review to identify possible underlying respiratory and/or systemic illness*** [1].

### Symptoms (with Pathophysiological Basis)

| Symptom | Pathophysiological Basis | Points Towards |
|---|---|---|
| **Dry cough, worse at night, with exercise** | Airway hyper-responsiveness → circadian variation in bronchial tone (worse at night due to ↓ cortisol, ↑ vagal tone) + exercise/cold air triggers bronchospasm | Asthma / cough-variant asthma |
| **Chronic wet/productive cough** | ***Daily moist/productive cough → suppurative lung disease*** [1]. Persistent bacterial infection → neutrophilic inflammation → mucus hypersecretion | PBB, bronchiectasis, CF, PCD |
| **Copious purulent/foul-smelling sputum** | Destroyed bronchial walls with pooled infected secretions → anaerobic bacterial overgrowth → malodorous sputum | Bronchiectasis |
| **Cough with postural change** | Sputum pools in dependent bronchiectatic segments; position change mobilises secretions → stimulates cough receptors | Bronchiectasis, GORD (supine → reflux), post-nasal drip |
| **Throat clearing, nasal congestion, sneezing** | Allergic rhinitis → mucus overproduction in nose/sinuses → posterior drip onto pharyngeal cough receptors | UACS / post-nasal drip |
| **Heartburn, regurgitation, vomiting, feeding refusal** | Reflux of gastric acid → oesophago-bronchial vagal reflex + micro-aspiration → cough; acid damage to oesophageal mucosa → heartburn | GORD |
| **Cough during/after feeds** | Aspiration of milk/food into airways due to swallowing incoordination or TEF; stimulates irritant receptors in larynx/trachea → cough | Aspiration syndrome, GORD, TEF |
| ***Feeding difficulties*** | ***Serious systemic including pulmonary illness, aspiration*** [1] | Aspiration, neurodevelopmental disease, CF |
| ***Failure to thrive*** | ***Serious systemic including pulmonary illness*** [1]. Chronic infection → ↑ metabolic demand + ↓ nutrient absorption (CF: pancreatic insufficiency) + ↓ intake (dyspnoea) | CF, PID, bronchiectasis, TB |
| **Paroxysmal cough with inspiratory whoop** | Pertussis toxin → destruction of ciliated epithelium → thick tenacious mucus → severe coughing spasms; whoop from forced inspiration through a narrowed/oedematous glottis | Pertussis |
| ***Chest pain*** | ***Arrhythmia, asthma, increased respiratory distress (parenchymal disease)*** [1]. Pleuritic pain from pleural irritation; chest wall pain from forceful coughing | Asthma, pneumonia, pneumothorax |
| **Haemoptysis** | Erosion of inflamed bronchial vessels (bronchiectasis), caseous necrosis near vessels (TB), or vascular malformation | Bronchiectasis, CF, TB |
| ***Recurrent pneumonia*** | ***Immunodeficiency, congenital lung abnormalities, tracheo-oesophageal H fistula*** [1]. Impaired immunity → failure to clear pathogens; structural anomalies → recurrent infection in same lobe | PID, structural anomaly, FB, aspiration |
| **Loud, honking cough absent during sleep** | No organic airway pathology; the cough is cortically mediated (conscious behaviour) → disappears during sleep when cortical input is suppressed | Habit/psychogenic cough |
| **Neonatal-onset cough, chronic rhinitis since birth** | Defective cilia from birth → impaired mucociliary clearance of respiratory and nasal mucosa from day 1 | PCD |
| **History of choking episode** | Foreign body lodged in airway → chronic irritation, ball-valve obstruction | Foreign body aspiration |

### Signs (with Pathophysiological Basis)

| Sign | Pathophysiological Basis | Points Towards |
|---|---|---|
| ***Wheeze*** | ***Intrathoracic airway lesion (e.g., asthma, foreign body)*** [1]. Narrowed intrathoracic airways → turbulent airflow during expiration (when intrathoracic airways naturally narrow) | Asthma, foreign body, bronchiolitis |
| **Stridor** | Extrathoracic or upper airway narrowing → turbulent airflow predominantly on inspiration (extrathoracic airways collapse on inspiration due to subatmospheric luminal pressure) | Croup, tracheomalacia, vascular ring, laryngeal lesion |
| **Monophonic wheeze** | Single-pitch wheeze suggests localised large airway obstruction (single narrowed point generates one frequency) vs polyphonic in asthma (multiple airways of different calibre) | Foreign body, endobronchial tumour, central airway lesion |
| ***Crepitations (crackles)*** | ***Parenchymal disease*** [1]. Sudden opening of previously closed small airways during inspiration → crackling sounds; or fluid/secretions in airways | Pneumonia, bronchiectasis, ILD, CF |
| ***Digital clubbing*** | ***Chronic suppurative lung disease*** [1]. Chronic hypoxia → ↑ VEGF and PDGF → proliferation of connective tissue in fingertips; also related to platelet-derived growth factors bypassing the pulmonary filter in shunting/suppurative disease | CF, bronchiectasis, ILD, empyema, congenital heart disease |
| ***Chest wall deformity*** | ***Chronic airway or parenchymal disease*** [1]. Harrison's sulci (inward pulling of the lower chest at diaphragmatic insertions) from chronic increased work of breathing; barrel chest from air trapping | Chronic severe asthma, CF, bronchiectasis |
| **Hyperinflation** | Air trapping (obstructive airway disease) → ↑ FRC → barrel-shaped chest, ↓ liver dullness, hyperresonant percussion | Asthma, CF, foreign body (unilateral) |
| ***Hypoxia/cyanosis*** | ***Airway or parenchymal disease, cardiac disease*** [1]. V/Q mismatch or shunting → deoxygenated haemoglobin > 5 g/dL → clinical cyanosis | Severe parenchymal disease, cardiac disease, airway obstruction |
| **Nasal polyps** | Chronic inflammation → mucosal swelling → polyp formation; in children, nasal polyps strongly suggest CF or PCD (unlike adults where they are usually benign) | CF, PCD, allergic rhinitis (adolescents) |
| **Situs inversus / dextrocardia** | Cilia are also responsible for left-right organogenesis during embryonic development (nodal cilia at the primitive node). If cilia are dysmotile → random organ situs → 50% chance of situs inversus | PCD (Kartagener syndrome) |
| ***Neurodevelopmental abnormality*** | ***Aspiration lung disease*** [1]. Impaired bulbar function → incoordinate swallowing → chronic aspiration | Cerebral palsy, neuromuscular disease |
| **Tracheal tug** | Severe airway obstruction → ↑ negative intrathoracic pressure on inspiration → trachea pulled downward | Severe obstruction (asthma, croup, foreign body) |
| **Growth parameters (weight, height, OFC)** | Chronic disease → ↑ metabolic demand + ↓ intake → faltering growth; specific patterns in CF (↓ weight > ↓ height) | CF, PID, TB, bronchiectasis |
| **Eczematous skin changes** | Th2-mediated inflammation → IgE-driven dermatitis; part of the "atopic march" (eczema → food allergy → allergic rhinitis → asthma) | Atopy → asthma, allergic rhinitis |

<Callout title="Red Flags in Paediatric Chronic Cough" type="error">
***Specific cough pointers that should prompt urgent investigation***:
- ***Failure to thrive***
- ***Digital clubbing***
- ***Daily moist/productive cough***
- ***Recurrent pneumonia***
- ***Hypoxia/cyanosis***
- ***Feeding difficulties***
- ***Neurodevelopmental abnormality***
- ***Chest wall deformity***
- ***Haemoptysis***

Any of these moves the child from "nonspecific cough" to "specific cough" and demands a thorough workup. ***Cough treatment should be based on aetiology*** [1]. ***No evidence to support medicine for symptomatic relief*** of cough in children [1].
</Callout>

## Clinical Approach to Chronic Cough in Children

***Importance of history and physical examination*** [1].

### History Framework

1. **Characterise the cough**:
   - **Duration**: > 4 weeks = chronic (ACCP)
   - **Character**: Dry vs wet/productive; barking vs honking vs paroxysmal
   - **Timing**: Diurnal pattern (worse at night → asthma; absent at night → habit cough), seasonal, relation to feeds/position
   - **Triggers**: Exercise, cold air, allergens, feeding, position
   - **Progression**: Getting worse, stable, or improving?

2. **Associated symptoms**:
   - Wheeze, dyspnoea, exercise intolerance
   - Nasal symptoms (rhinorrhoea, congestion, sneezing)
   - GI symptoms (vomiting, heartburn, feeding difficulty)
   - Constitutional symptoms (fever, weight loss, night sweats → TB)
   - Steatorrhoea (bulky, greasy stools → CF)

3. **Past history**:
   - Neonatal history (prematurity, respiratory distress, need for ventilation)
   - History of choking episode (foreign body!)
   - Recurrent infections (pneumonia, sinusitis, otitis media → PID, PCD, CF)
   - Previous TB contact or travel

4. **Atopic history** (personal and family):
   - Eczema, allergic rhinitis, food allergy, asthma
   - Family history of atopy

5. **Birth and developmental history**:
   - Developmental milestones (neurodevelopmental abnormality → aspiration)
   - Growth (faltering growth → serious systemic disease)

6. **Immunisation history**:
   - Pertussis vaccination (waning immunity possible)
   - BCG (TB risk assessment)

7. **Environmental and social history**:
   - Passive smoke exposure
   - Pets, home environment (carpet, humidity, cockroaches — relevant in HK)
   - School attendance (psychogenic cough often triggered by school stress)

8. **Drug history**:
   - ACE inhibitors (dry cough)
   - Previous cough medications and response

9. **Family history**:
   - CF, PCD, immunodeficiency (consanguinity → AR conditions)
   - TB contacts

### Physical Examination Framework

1. **General**: Growth parameters (plot on growth chart!), nutritional status, dysmorphic features
2. **ENT**: Nasal polyps, allergic shiners, nasal crease, mouth breathing, tonsillar hypertrophy, post-nasal drip (cobblestoning of posterior pharynx)
3. **Respiratory**: Inspection (chest wall deformity, Harrison's sulci, tracheal tug), auscultation (wheeze — diffuse vs localised, crackles, differential air entry), percussion (hyperresonance → air trapping)
4. **Cardiovascular**: Murmurs (congenital heart disease → cardiac cause of cough; left-to-right shunts can cause pulmonary congestion), apex position (dextrocardia → PCD)
5. **Hands**: Clubbing, cyanosis, eczema
6. **Abdomen**: Hepatomegaly (CF liver disease), situs inversus
7. **Skin**: Eczema (atopy), scars (previous surgery)
8. **Neurodevelopmental assessment**: Tone, oromotor function (aspiration risk)

---

> **Key Teaching Point**: ***Causes of cough in children may be different from adults. Manifestations may be different from adults.*** [1]. The classic adult triad of post-nasal drip, asthma, and GORD does not simply apply to children. Protracted bacterial bronchitis (virtually non-existent as a diagnosis in adults), foreign body aspiration, congenital anomalies, CF, and PCD are important paediatric-specific diagnoses. Always think about the child's age and developmental stage.

> ***Cough treatment should be based on aetiology. No evidence to support medicine for symptomatic relief*** [1]. Over-the-counter cough medicines are not only ineffective in children but can be harmful (e.g., codeine-containing preparations are contraindicated in children < 12 years due to variable CYP2D6 metabolism → risk of fatal respiratory depression).

> ***Importance of education*** [1] — educating families that cough is a protective reflex, that repeated URTIs are normal in young children (especially in childcare settings), and that not every cough needs antibiotics or cough medicine is a crucial part of management.

<Callout title="High Yield Summary">

**Definition**: Paediatric chronic cough = daily cough > 4 weeks (ACCP) or > 8 weeks (BTS). Different from adults.

**Classification**: Specific cough (with pointers to underlying disease) vs Nonspecific cough (isolated dry cough, well child).

**Key aetiologies by age**:
- Infants: congenital anomalies, aspiration/GORD, CF, PCD
- Toddlers: foreign body (peak age!), PBB, asthma
- School-age: asthma (most common), UACS, habit cough
- All ages: TB (Hong Kong!), immunodeficiency

**Red flags** (specific cough pointers): failure to thrive, clubbing, daily wet cough, recurrent pneumonia, hypoxia, feeding difficulty, neurodevelopmental abnormality, chest wall deformity.

**Protracted bacterial bronchitis**: Most common cause of chronic wet cough in preschoolers; responds to 2–4 weeks antibiotics.

**Cough-variant asthma**: Cough as sole symptom; dry, worse at night/exercise; trial of ICS is diagnostic and therapeutic.

**Foreign body**: Always ask about choking episode; unilateral wheeze + recurrent same-lobe pneumonia.

**CF**: Chronic wet cough + FTT + steatorrhoea → sweat test.

**PCD**: Neonatal respiratory distress + chronic wet cough from birth + chronic rhinosinusitis + situs inversus (50%).

**Habit cough**: Loud, honking, absent during sleep; diagnosis of exclusion.

**Management principle**: Treat the cause, not the cough. No evidence for OTC cough medicines in children.

</Callout>

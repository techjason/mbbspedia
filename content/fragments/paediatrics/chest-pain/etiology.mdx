# Chest Pain in Paediatrics

## Definition

Chest pain in the paediatric population refers to any pain or discomfort perceived in the thoracic region—anterior, posterior, or lateral chest wall—in a patient from infancy through adolescence (typically 0–18 years). While it is one of the most common reasons for paediatric emergency department visits and cardiology referrals, it is overwhelmingly **non-cardiac** in origin in children, in stark contrast to adults where cardiac ischaemia dominates the differential.

The word "chest" derives from Old English *cest* (box/container), and "pain" from Latin *poena* (penalty/suffering). In paediatrics, the challenge is distinguishing the rare but serious cardiac causes from the vast majority of benign musculoskeletal, respiratory, and idiopathic aetiologies.

<Callout title="Key Paediatric Principle">
In adults, chest pain = "rule out ACS until proven otherwise." In children, chest pain is **rarely cardiac** ( < 5% of cases). The approach must be tailored to paediatric physiology, common paediatric conditions, and the child's developmental stage. However, you must still systematically exclude serious causes.
</Callout>

---

## Epidemiology

### Incidence and Prevalence
- Chest pain accounts for **0.3–0.6% of all paediatric ED visits** [1]
- It is the **second most common reason for paediatric cardiology referral** (after murmurs) [1]
- Peak incidence: **12–14 years** (early adolescence), though can present at any age
- Slight male predominance in some series, though this varies

### Aetiology Distribution (Focus on Hong Kong and East Asian Paediatric Populations)
- **Idiopathic/unknown**: 20–45%
- **Musculoskeletal**: 15–31% (most common identifiable cause)
- **Respiratory**: 10–20%
- **Gastrointestinal**: 5–10%
- **Psychogenic**: 5–15% (higher in adolescents, especially with exam stress—highly relevant in Hong Kong's high-pressure academic environment)
- ***Cardiac: < 5%*** (but this is the group you cannot miss)
- **Miscellaneous**: breast tenderness (thelarche/gynaecomastia), herpes zoster, sickle cell disease (less common in HK)

### Hong Kong Context
- Kawasaki disease is an important consideration in young children (< 5 years) with chest pain and fever, given its relatively higher incidence in East Asian populations (incidence ~70–100/100,000 children < 5 years in HK/Japan vs ~25/100,000 in Western countries)
- Congenital heart disease prevalence is ~8–10/1,000 live births; some lesions (e.g., anomalous coronary arteries, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy) can present with exertional chest pain
- Psychogenic causes may be under-recognised due to stigma around mental health

## Risk Factors

### For serious (cardiac) causes
- **Family history**: sudden cardiac death (SCD) < 50 years, cardiomyopathy, long QT syndrome, Marfan syndrome, familial hyperlipidaemia
- **Personal history**: known congenital heart disease (especially post-surgical), Kawasaki disease, Marfan syndrome or other connective tissue disorders
- **Exertional symptoms**: pain specifically triggered by exercise (suggests coronary anomaly, HOCM, or arrhythmia)
- **Syncope or near-syncope** with chest pain
- **Palpitations** with chest pain
- **Illicit drug use** in adolescents (cocaine, amphetamines → coronary vasospasm/MI)

### For non-cardiac causes
- **Musculoskeletal**: recent trauma, heavy backpack use, new physical activity, costochondritis (repetitive cough)
- **Respiratory**: asthma (very common in HK, prevalence ~10% in school-age children), recent respiratory infection
- **GI**: obesity, high-fat diet, carbonated drinks
- **Psychogenic**: anxiety disorders, academic stress, bullying, family dysfunction, history of somatisation

## Anatomy and Function (Paediatric Thorax)

Understanding thoracic anatomy is essential because chest pain can originate from any structure within or around the thorax. The key structures and their pain-generating mechanisms are:

### 1. Chest Wall (Most Common Source of Pain in Children)
- **Ribs, costal cartilages, intercostal muscles, pectoral muscles**: somatic innervation via intercostal nerves (T1–T12)
- Pain from these structures is **well-localised, sharp, reproducible on palpation**, and worsened by movement/respiration
- In children, costal cartilages are more pliable (less calcified than adults) → costochondritis is very common
- **Costochondral junctions**: the interface between bone (rib) and cartilage. Inflammation here = costochondritis

### 2. Pleura
- **Parietal pleura**: innervated by intercostal nerves (somatic) → pain is sharp, localised, pleuritic (worse with inspiration/cough)
- **Visceral pleura**: innervated by autonomic nerves → no pain sensation
- This is why pneumothorax, pleuritis, and pleural effusion cause sharp, pleuritic chest pain (parietal pleura is irritated)

### 3. Lungs and Airways
- Lung parenchyma itself has no somatic innervation → pneumonia causes pain only when the parietal pleura or chest wall is involved
- Tracheobronchial tree (large airways): pain from cough-related strain, foreign body, or inflammation is often described as retrosternal "burning" or "rawness"

### 4. Oesophagus
- Shares segmental innervation with the heart (T1–T5 sympathetic afferents) → oesophageal pain can mimic cardiac pain
- GERD, oesophagitis, oesophageal spasm, and foreign bodies can all cause retrosternal chest pain

### 5. Heart and Great Vessels
- **Myocardium**: innervated by cardiac sympathetic afferents (T1–T5) → pain is referred to the chest, left arm, jaw (same dermatomes)
- **Pericardium**: the visceral pericardium has no somatic innervation, but the **parietal pericardium** (especially its diaphragmatic surface) is innervated by the phrenic nerve (C3–C5) → pericardial pain is positional (worse lying flat, better leaning forward) and may radiate to the left shoulder/trapezius ridge
- **Aorta**: pain from aortic pathology (dissection, aneurysm) is severe, tearing, radiating to the back—rare in children but possible in Marfan syndrome or Turner syndrome

### 6. Diaphragm
- Innervated centrally by phrenic nerve (C3–C5) → diaphragmatic irritation causes referred pain to the shoulder tip
- Innervated peripherally by lower intercostal nerves → pain referred to lower chest/upper abdomen

### 7. Mediastinal Structures
- Lymph nodes, thymus, thoracic duct
- Mediastinal masses (lymphoma, thymoma) can cause chest pain from compression or invasion

### Paediatric-Specific Anatomical Considerations
- The paediatric thorax is more compliant (less ossified ribs) → chest wall deformity (pectus excavatum/carinatum) is more common and can contribute to chest pain
- The thymus is relatively large in young children → important to not mistake a normal thymus for a mediastinal mass on CXR
- The child's airway is narrower → foreign body aspiration is more common in young children (< 3 years)

## Aetiology and Pathophysiology (Paediatric Focus, Hong Kong Relevant)

### A. Musculoskeletal Causes (Most Common Identifiable Category: 15–31%)

#### 1. Costochondritis
- **Definition**: Inflammation of costochondral junctions (where rib meets costal cartilage)
- "Costo" = rib, "chondr" = cartilage, "itis" = inflammation
- **Pathophysiology**: The costochondral junctions are sites of mechanical stress. Repeated microtrauma (coughing, physical activity, heavy backpacks) → local inflammation → pain
- In children, cartilage is more pliable and growth plates are active → susceptible to mechanical stress
- **Clinical features**: Sharp, localised pain at one or more costochondral junctions (usually 2nd–5th), **reproducible on palpation**; worsened by deep breathing, coughing, or trunk movement
- No swelling (contrast with Tietze syndrome, which has visible swelling)

#### 2. Tietze Syndrome
- Costochondritis **with visible/palpable swelling** at the costochondral junction
- Rare in children; when it occurs, usually affects a single costochondral junction (typically 2nd or 3rd)

#### 3. Musculoskeletal Strain
- From sports, exercise, heavy backpack use, direct trauma
- Intercostal muscle strain, pectoral muscle strain, rib contusion/fracture
- **Pathophysiology**: Mechanical injury → local tissue damage → inflammatory mediator release → pain

#### 4. Precordial Catch Syndrome (Texidor's Twinge)
- **Very common** in adolescents and young adults
- Sudden onset of sharp, well-localised left-sided chest pain at rest
- Lasts seconds to minutes, worsened by deep inspiration, relieved by shallow breathing or a forced deep breath that "pops" it
- **Pathophysiology**: Poorly understood; thought to be due to localised pleural irritation or intercostal muscle spasm. Completely benign.

#### 5. Slipping Rib Syndrome
- Hypermobility of the 8th, 9th, or 10th ribs (these ribs have cartilaginous, rather than bony, attachment to the rib above) → they "slip" under the rib above → pain in the lower chest/upper abdomen
- **Pathophysiology**: Mechanical subluxation → irritation of the intercostal nerve

#### 6. Chest Wall Deformity
- **Pectus excavatum** ("funnel chest"): depression of the sternum. Can cause chest pain from abnormal chest wall mechanics. Rarely, severe forms compress the heart → decreased cardiac output on exertion
- **Pectus carinatum** ("pigeon chest"): protrusion of the sternum

### B. Respiratory Causes (10–20%)

#### 1. Asthma / Exercise-Induced Bronchospasm
- ***Very common in Hong Kong*** (prevalence ~10% in school-age children)
- **Pathophysiology**: Bronchial hyperreactivity → bronchospasm → air trapping → chest tightness/discomfort (not true "pain" per se, but children may describe it as "pain"). The sensation is due to increased work of breathing and hyperinflation stretching the chest wall
- Exertional chest "tightness" in a child may be the sole presentation of asthma (without classic wheeze)

#### 2. Pneumonia
- **Pathophysiology**: Infection → alveolar consolidation → when extends to parietal pleura, causes pleuritic chest pain. Diaphragmatic involvement → referred shoulder-tip pain
- Common organisms: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Mycoplasma pneumoniae (school-age children), viral (RSV, influenza—especially in younger children)

#### 3. Pneumothorax
- Primary spontaneous pneumothorax: tall, thin adolescent males (same as adults)
- Secondary: in asthma, CF, Marfan syndrome
- **Pathophysiology**: Air enters the pleural space → loss of negative intrapleural pressure → lung collapses → sudden pleuritic chest pain + dyspnoea
- ***Sudden onset, unilateral pleuritic chest pain + SOB*** [2]

#### 4. Pleural Effusion / Pleuritis
- Can complicate pneumonia (parapneumonic effusion/empyema), TB (important in HK), SLE, malignancy
- **Pathophysiology**: Inflammation/infection of parietal pleura → sharp pleuritic pain; effusion accumulation → dyspnoea from lung compression

#### 5. Foreign Body Aspiration
- Especially in children < 3 years
- Unilateral wheeze, cough, ± chest pain
- **Pathophysiology**: Foreign body → airway obstruction → distal air trapping or atelectasis → chest discomfort

### C. Cardiac Causes ( < 5%, but MUST NOT MISS)

<Callout title="Red Flag" type="error">
Cardiac causes of chest pain are rare in children but can be life-threatening. Always screen for: exertional chest pain, syncope/near-syncope, palpitations, family history of SCD or cardiomyopathy, and known congenital heart disease.
</Callout>

#### 1. Pericarditis / Myopericarditis
- **Most common cardiac cause of chest pain in children**
- "Peri" = around, "card" = heart, "itis" = inflammation
- **Aetiology**: Viral (Coxsackie B, echovirus, adenovirus, influenza—most common in children), autoimmune (SLE, JIA), post-cardiac surgery (postpericardiotomy syndrome), idiopathic, rarely TB or purulent
- **Pathophysiology**: Inflammation of the pericardium → irritation of the parietal pericardium (innervated by phrenic nerve and intercostal nerves) → chest pain
  - Pain is **positional**: worse lying supine (because the inflamed pericardium rubs against the heart), better leaning forward (because the heart falls away from the anterior pericardium)
  - **Pericardial friction rub**: scratchy, high-pitched sound heard with the diaphragm of the stethoscope; best heard at the left sternal border with the patient leaning forward at end-expiration
  - If significant effusion develops → tamponade physiology (Beck's triad: hypotension, muffled heart sounds, raised JVP—though JVP assessment is difficult in young children)

#### 2. Myocarditis
- Inflammation of the myocardium itself
- **Aetiology**: Viral (Coxsackie B, parvovirus B19, adenovirus, SARS-CoV-2/MIS-C), autoimmune
- **Pathophysiology**: Viral invasion → myocyte necrosis → inflammatory infiltrate → impaired contractility → heart failure symptoms (dyspnoea, fatigue, chest pain, tachycardia out of proportion to fever)
- May present with chest pain + signs of heart failure or arrhythmia
- ***MIS-C (Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome in Children)***: post-COVID-19 inflammatory condition that can cause myocarditis, coronary artery abnormalities, and shock—important consideration in current practice

#### 3. Arrhythmias
- **Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT)**: most common pathological arrhythmia in children
  - Children (especially younger ones) may describe the sensation of rapid heart rate as "chest pain" rather than "palpitations" because they lack the vocabulary
  - **Pathophysiology**: Accessory pathway or re-entrant circuit → sudden onset of very rapid heart rate (often > 220 bpm in infants, > 180 bpm in older children) → reduced diastolic filling time → subendocardial ischaemia (relative) → chest discomfort
- **Ventricular tachycardia**: much rarer; associated with channelopathies (long QT syndrome, Brugada syndrome), HOCM, myocarditis
- **Premature ventricular complexes (PVCs)**: common, usually benign; children may perceive the "thump" of the post-ectopic beat as chest pain

#### 4. Structural Heart Disease Causing Exertional Chest Pain

##### a. ***Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HOCM/HCM)***
- **Pathophysiology**: Asymmetric septal hypertrophy → dynamic LVOT obstruction (worse during exercise when contractility increases and preload decreases) → decreased cardiac output + subendocardial ischaemia (the thickened muscle has increased oxygen demand but compressed intramural coronary vessels)
- ***Leading cause of sudden cardiac death in young athletes*** [3]
- May present with exertional chest pain, syncope, dyspnoea, or SCD as the first manifestation
- Autosomal dominant inheritance in ~60%; sarcomere protein gene mutations (β-myosin heavy chain, myosin-binding protein C most common)
- ***In Hong Kong/East Asia, apical HCM variant is more common (25–30% of HCM in HK/Japan vs ~1–2% elsewhere)*** [3]

##### b. Anomalous Origin of Coronary Artery
- Particularly **anomalous left coronary artery from the right sinus of Valsalva (ALCA)**: the aberrant coronary passes between the aorta and pulmonary artery
- **Pathophysiology**: During exercise, the aorta and PA dilate → compress the anomalous coronary → myocardial ischaemia → chest pain or sudden death
- Important cause of **exertional syncope and sudden cardiac death in children and young athletes**

##### c. Anomalous Left Coronary Artery from Pulmonary Artery (ALCAPA / Bland-White-Garland Syndrome)
- ***Left coronary artery arises from the pulmonary artery instead of the aorta***
- **Pathophysiology**: After birth, as pulmonary artery pressure drops, perfusion pressure to the left coronary drops → myocardial ischaemia → typically presents in **infancy** (2–4 months) with:
  - Crying/feeding-related distress (equivalent of "exertional angina" in an infant)
  - Heart failure, poor feeding, diaphoresis
  - Characteristic ECG: deep Q waves and ST changes in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5–V6)
- Older children may have developed collaterals and present with mitral regurgitation or dilated cardiomyopathy

##### d. ***Aortic Stenosis (AS)***
- Congenital aortic stenosis (bicuspid aortic valve is the most common cause)
- **Pathophysiology**: Fixed outflow obstruction → LV pressure overload → LV hypertrophy → increased myocardial oxygen demand with limited supply → exertional chest pain, syncope, dyspnoea
- ***Classic triad of severe AS: exertional chest pain, syncope, heart failure*** [3]

##### e. ***Pulmonary Hypertension***
- In children: idiopathic PAH, Eisenmenger syndrome (from uncorrected L→R shunts), chronic lung disease
- **Pathophysiology**: Increased RV afterload → RV hypertrophy → RV subendocardial ischaemia → exertional chest pain; also inadequate increase in cardiac output during exercise → syncope

#### 5. Kawasaki Disease (Coronary Artery Sequelae)
- ***Highly relevant in Hong Kong/East Asian populations***
- **Pathophysiology**: Kawasaki disease → coronary artery vasculitis → coronary artery aneurysms (in 25% if untreated, < 5% if treated with IVIG) → long-term risk of coronary artery stenosis/thrombosis → myocardial ischaemia
- Children/adolescents with a **history of Kawasaki disease** presenting with chest pain must be evaluated for coronary artery complications
- This is one of the few situations where true **myocardial ischaemia/infarction** occurs in the paediatric age group

#### 6. Aortic Dissection (Rare in Children)
- Consider in **Marfan syndrome**, **Turner syndrome**, **Loeys-Dietz syndrome**, **Ehlers-Danlos syndrome type IV**
- **Pathophysiology**: Connective tissue disorder → weakened aortic media → intimal tear → blood dissects into the media creating a false lumen → severe tearing chest pain radiating to the back [4]
- Acute onset, maximal at onset, "tearing" quality

#### 7. Coronary Artery Disease (Extremely Rare)
- ***Familial hyperlipidaemia*** (homozygous form): can cause premature atherosclerosis and even MI in childhood/adolescence
- Post-cardiac transplant: accelerated transplant vasculopathy
- Drug-induced: **cocaine, amphetamine** use in adolescents → coronary vasospasm → MI

### D. Gastrointestinal Causes (5–10%)

#### 1. Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)
- Common in infants and children; can present as retrosternal burning chest pain, especially postprandial
- **Pathophysiology**: Transient LES relaxation → reflux of acidic gastric content → oesophageal mucosal irritation → retrosternal burning pain [5]
- In children, may be associated with cough, wheeze, or hoarseness (extra-oesophageal symptoms)
- Oesophageal and cardiac afferents share the same spinal cord segments (T1–T5) → oesophageal pain can mimic cardiac pain (this is why GERD is an important differential for chest pain even in adults) [5]

#### 2. Oesophagitis
- **Eosinophilic oesophagitis (EoE)**: increasingly recognised, especially in children with atopic history. Presents with dysphagia, food impaction, and chest pain
- Pill oesophagitis (e.g., doxycycline, NSAIDs)
- Infectious oesophagitis (Candida in immunocompromised)

#### 3. Foreign Body Ingestion
- Especially in young children ( < 5 years)
- Button batteries → caustic injury to oesophageal wall → severe chest pain (EMERGENCY)
- Coins → oesophageal impaction → retrosternal discomfort

#### 4. Peptic Ulcer Disease
- Less common in children than adults but can occur, especially with *H. pylori* infection or NSAID use [6]

### E. Psychogenic / Functional Causes (5–15%)

#### 1. Anxiety and Panic Disorder
- **Very common in adolescents, especially in Hong Kong's high-pressure academic environment**
- ***Panic attacks: abrupt surge of intense fear with somatic symptoms (chest pain, palpitations, SOB, dizziness, paraesthesiae)*** [7]
- **Pathophysiology**: Hyperventilation → respiratory alkalosis → decreased ionised calcium → perioral/peripheral paraesthesiae; also hyperventilation → chest wall muscle fatigue → chest tightness
- Sympathetic activation → tachycardia, diaphoresis, chest tightness
- May also have hyperawareness of normal physiological sensations (interoceptive sensitivity)

#### 2. Somatisation / Somatic Symptom Disorder
- Chronic/recurrent chest pain without organic cause, with excessive thoughts/behaviours related to the symptoms [7]
- More common in children with personal/family history of chronic illness, anxiety, depression, or psychosocial stressors

#### 3. Depression
- Can present with somatic complaints including chest pain, especially in adolescents

### F. Other / Miscellaneous Causes

#### 1. Breast-Related
- **Thelarche** (breast development) in girls: breast budding can cause chest wall tenderness
- **Gynaecomastia** in boys (pubertal or drug-induced): tenderness over breast tissue
- These are important to consider in adolescents and are frequently overlooked

#### 2. Herpes Zoster
- Dermatomal vesicular rash with preceding pain
- Uncommon in children unless immunocompromised

#### 3. Sickle Cell Disease
- Acute chest syndrome: chest pain + fever + new pulmonary infiltrate on CXR
- More relevant in populations with high SCD prevalence (less common in HK Chinese but present in South Asian and African populations in HK)

#### 4. Mediastinal Mass
- Lymphoma (Hodgkin's lymphoma peaks in adolescence)
- Anterior mediastinal mass → chest pain, cough, SVC syndrome, respiratory compromise (especially when supine—important anaesthetic consideration)

## Classification of Paediatric Chest Pain

### By Acuity

| Acute (seconds to hours) | Subacute/Chronic (days to weeks+) |
|---|---|
| Pneumothorax | Costochondritis |
| Trauma (rib fracture, contusion) | Musculoskeletal strain |
| Foreign body aspiration/ingestion | Asthma/exercise-induced bronchospasm |
| Acute pericarditis/myocarditis | GERD |
| Arrhythmia (SVT) | Psychogenic/anxiety |
| PE (rare in children) | Precordial catch syndrome |
| Aortic dissection (Marfan) | Mediastinal mass |
| Acute chest syndrome (SCD) | Post-Kawasaki coronary disease |

### By Organ System

| System | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Musculoskeletal** | Costochondritis, Tietze, precordial catch, strain, slipping rib, pectus deformity |
| **Respiratory** | Asthma, pneumonia, pneumothorax, pleural effusion, foreign body |
| **Cardiac** | Pericarditis, myocarditis, arrhythmia, HOCM, coronary anomaly, Kawasaki sequelae, AS |
| **Gastrointestinal** | GERD, oesophagitis (EoE, pill), foreign body ingestion, PUD |
| **Psychogenic** | Anxiety, panic disorder, somatisation, depression |
| **Other** | Breast development/gynaecomastia, herpes zoster, sickle cell, mediastinal mass |

### By Severity (Clinical Urgency)

**Life-threatening (must exclude first):**
1. Tension pneumothorax
2. Cardiac tamponade
3. Myocarditis with cardiogenic shock
4. Arrhythmia with haemodynamic compromise
5. Aortic dissection (in connective tissue disorders)
6. Pulmonary embolism (rare but possible, especially in adolescents with risk factors)
7. Foreign body airway obstruction

**Serious but not immediately life-threatening:**
1. Pericarditis
2. Significant pneumothorax
3. Pneumonia
4. Coronary artery anomaly (between episodes)
5. Kawasaki disease sequelae

**Benign (majority):**
1. Costochondritis
2. Precordial catch
3. Musculoskeletal strain
4. Mild asthma
5. GERD
6. Psychogenic

## Clinical Features

### A. Symptoms (with Pathophysiological Basis)

The OPQRST framework is useful for characterising chest pain in children (adapted from adult practice but with paediatric nuances):

| Feature | Detail | Paediatric Nuance |
|---|---|---|
| **O**nset | Sudden vs gradual | Sudden, maximal at onset → pneumothorax, PE; gradual → musculoskeletal, GERD |
| **P**rovocation / Palliation | What triggers/relieves it | Exertional → cardiac (HOCM, coronary anomaly, AS); positional → pericarditis, GERD; palpation-reproducible → musculoskeletal |
| **Q**uality | Sharp, dull, burning, squeezing | Sharp + pleuritic → pleural/chest wall; burning/retrosternal → oesophageal; pressure/squeezing → cardiac (rare); "can't breathe" → asthma |
| **R**egion / Radiation | Where exactly + where it spreads | Left shoulder → pericarditis (phrenic nerve); jaw/left arm → myocardial (rare); back → aortic dissection |
| **S**everity | Pain scale | Less reliable in young children |
| **T**iming / Duration | How long, pattern | Seconds → precordial catch; minutes → angina; hours → pericarditis, musculoskeletal; chronic/recurrent → idiopathic, psychogenic |

#### Detailed Symptom Descriptions by Aetiology:

**1. Musculoskeletal Pain:**
- **Sharp, well-localised** pain (the child can point with one finger)
- ***Reproducible with palpation*** (pathognomonic—when you press on the exact spot and reproduce the pain, it is almost certainly musculoskeletal)
- Worsened by movement, deep breathing, coughing, twisting
- Duration: hours to days; may be recurrent
- **Why reproducible?** Because the pain source is the chest wall itself—applying pressure directly stimulates the inflamed/injured tissue and local nociceptors

**2. Respiratory Pain:**
- **Pleuritic**: sharp, worsened specifically by **inspiration** (because inspiration expands the chest and moves the inflamed parietal pleura)
- May be associated with cough, dyspnoea, fever
- Asthma: more "tightness" or "can't breathe" than true pain
  - **Why tightness?** Bronchospasm → air trapping → hyperinflation → increased elastic recoil force needed → sensation of chest restriction

**3. Cardiac Pain:**
- ***Exertional***: pain that is specifically triggered by exercise and relieved by rest suggests cardiac origin (HOCM, coronary anomaly, AS)
  - **Why exertional?** During exercise, myocardial oxygen demand increases. If supply is limited (fixed obstruction in AS, dynamic obstruction in HOCM, compressed anomalous coronary), ischaemia occurs → metabolite (adenosine, lactate) accumulation → stimulation of cardiac sympathetic afferents → chest pain
- Pericarditis: ***retrosternal pain, worse lying flat, better leaning forward***
  - **Why positional?** Lying flat → the inflamed posterior pericardium rubs against the heart with each beat. Leaning forward → heart falls anteriorly, away from the posterior pericardial surface
  - **Radiation to left shoulder/trapezius ridge**: referred pain via the phrenic nerve (C3–C5 shares sensory territory with the shoulder)
- Associated **syncope**: suggests inadequate cardiac output (HOCM with LVOT obstruction, severe AS, arrhythmia, coronary anomaly)
- Associated **palpitations**: suggests arrhythmia as the cause or concomitant finding

**4. Gastrointestinal Pain:**
- ***Retrosternal burning*** (heartburn), worse postprandially, worse lying down, relieved by antacids [5]
- **Why retrosternal?** The oesophagus lies directly behind the sternum. Acid reflux causes oesophageal mucosal inflammation, stimulating visceral afferents that project to the same spinal segments as cardiac afferents (T1–T5) → "non-cardiac chest pain"
- May be associated with acid taste, regurgitation, dysphagia
- ***Water brash*** (reflex salivary hypersecretion when acid reaches the upper oesophagus) [5]

**5. Psychogenic Pain:**
- Typically vague, poorly localised, variable in character
- May be associated with **hyperventilation** symptoms: light-headedness, perioral/extremity tingling (due to respiratory alkalosis → decreased ionised Ca²⁺)
- Often occurs in the context of **emotional stress** (exams, family problems, bullying)
- May be associated with other somatic complaints (headaches, abdominal pain)
- ***Panic attack features***: sudden onset, associated with palpitations, sweating, trembling, sense of impending doom, peaking in minutes [7]

**6. Age-Specific Presentations:**

| Age Group | Common Causes | Presentation Nuances |
|---|---|---|
| **Infant ( < 1 year)** | ALCAPA, myocarditis, MIS-C | Cannot verbalise pain; presents as irritability during feeds, diaphoresis, poor feeding, tachypnoea |
| **Toddler (1–3 years)** | Foreign body, myocarditis, Kawasaki sequelae | May point to chest but cannot describe quality; look for behavioural changes |
| **School-age (4–11 years)** | Asthma, costochondritis, pneumonia, psychogenic | Can localise pain; may describe it in simple terms ("it hurts here"); consider exercise-induced bronchospasm |
| **Adolescent (12–18 years)** | Musculoskeletal, psychogenic, GERD, precordial catch, HOCM, SVT, drug use | Can describe OPQRST; consider anxiety/panic disorder, substance use, Marfan (tall thin habitus) |

### B. Signs (with Pathophysiological Basis)

#### General Inspection
- **Marfanoid habitus** (tall, thin, arm span > height, arachnodactyly, pectus deformity, high-arched palate) → concern for Marfan syndrome → aortic dissection, mitral valve prolapse
- **Turner syndrome features** (short stature, webbed neck, shield chest) → bicuspid aortic valve, coarctation, aortic dissection risk
- **Pectus excavatum/carinatum** → musculoskeletal cause, or in severe pectus excavatum, cardiac compression
- **Cushingoid features** (if on steroids) → could indicate immunosuppression (infectious cause)
- **Respiratory distress**: tachypnoea, use of accessory muscles, nasal flaring, intercostal/subcostal retractions → respiratory cause
- **Cyanosis**: central cyanosis → serious cardiac or respiratory pathology
- **Diaphoresis**: autonomic activation → cardiac cause, anxiety/panic
- **Fever**: infectious cause (pneumonia, pericarditis, myocarditis)

#### Vital Signs (Age-Appropriate Normal Values!)

<Callout title="Know Your Normal Paediatric Vital Signs" type="error">
A common exam mistake is using adult normal ranges. Always know age-appropriate normals:
- **Heart rate**: Newborn 120–160, Infant 100–150, Toddler 80–130, School-age 70–110, Adolescent 60–100
- **Respiratory rate**: Newborn 30–60, Infant 25–50, Toddler 20–30, School-age 18–25, Adolescent 12–20
- **Blood pressure**: use percentile charts (5th percentile for age = lower limit). Generally systolic: ~70 + (2 × age in years) for children > 1 year
- **Oxygen saturation**: should be ≥ 95% (≥ 92% may be acceptable in known chronic lung disease)
</Callout>

- **Tachycardia**: can indicate pain, fever, anxiety, anaemia, dehydration, heart failure, arrhythmia, or haemodynamic compromise
- **Hypotension**: late sign in children (they compensate with tachycardia first) → when present, indicates significant haemodynamic compromise (tension pneumothorax, tamponade, massive PE, cardiogenic shock)
- **Hypertension**: can accompany pain or anxiety; also consider coarctation (upper limb hypertension with weak femoral pulses)
- **Pulse oximetry**: ↓SpO₂ → respiratory or cardiac cause
- **4-limb BP**: discrepancy between upper and lower limbs → coarctation of the aorta

#### Cardiovascular Examination
- **Inspection**: visible praecordial pulsation (RV heave), scars (previous cardiac surgery)
- **Palpation**:
  - **Displaced apex beat**: dilated cardiomyopathy, large pericardial effusion (difficult to locate)
  - **Heave**: left parasternal heave (RV hypertrophy → pulmonary hypertension); apical heave (LV hypertrophy → AS, HOCM)
  - **Thrills**: palpable murmurs indicating turbulent flow (AS, VSD)
- **Auscultation**:
  - ***Pericardial friction rub***: scratchy, "like walking on fresh snow," best heard with diaphragm at left sternal border, patient leaning forward → **pericarditis**
  - **Gallop rhythm (S3)**: volume overload, dilated cardiomyopathy, heart failure
  - **Ejection systolic murmur at right upper sternal border radiating to carotids**: aortic stenosis
  - **Harsh systolic murmur at left sternal border increasing with Valsalva**: HOCM (because Valsalva reduces preload → less ventricular volume → more obstruction)
  - ***Early diastolic murmur***: aortic regurgitation (consider aortic dissection if acute onset with tearing pain) [4]
  - **Muffled heart sounds**: pericardial effusion/tamponade
  - **Murmurs of congenital heart disease**: VSD (pansystolic at LLSE), ASD (fixed split S2), PDA (continuous machinery murmur)

#### Respiratory Examination
- **Decreased breath sounds + hyperresonance**: pneumothorax
- **Crackles**: pneumonia, pulmonary oedema (from heart failure)
- **Wheeze**: asthma, foreign body (unilateral wheeze)
- **Dullness to percussion + decreased breath sounds**: pleural effusion, consolidation
- **Tracheal deviation**: tension pneumothorax (away from affected side), large effusion (away), collapse (towards)

#### Chest Wall Examination
- ***Tenderness on palpation*** that ***reproduces the pain***: strongly suggests musculoskeletal cause (costochondritis, strain)
  - **Why?** Direct pressure on inflamed costochondral junction or strained muscle stimulates local nociceptors → exact reproduction of the presenting pain
- Visible swelling at costochondral junction: Tietze syndrome
- Crepitus: subcutaneous emphysema (pneumomediastinum, rib fracture with pneumothorax)

#### Abdominal Examination
- Epigastric tenderness: GERD, peptic ulcer disease
- Hepatomegaly: right heart failure (hepatic congestion), myocarditis

#### Skin Examination
- Vesicular rash in dermatomal distribution: herpes zoster
- Erythema marginatum, subcutaneous nodules: acute rheumatic fever
- Café-au-lait spots + pectus: neurofibromatosis (can have pheochromocytoma → hypertensive crises)

## Communication and Family-Centred Care Considerations

- **With the child**: Use age-appropriate language. For younger children, ask "does your tummy/chest hurt?" or use a visual analogue pain scale (Wong-Baker FACES). For adolescents, use HEADSS assessment (Home, Education, Activities, Drugs, Sexuality, Suicide/depression) to screen for psychosocial contributors.
- **With caregivers**: Parents are often very anxious when their child has chest pain (they worry about "heart attack"). Acknowledge their concern, explain that cardiac causes are rare in children, but reassure them that you will thoroughly evaluate.
- **Consent/Assent**: For investigations and treatment, obtain parental consent for children < 16 years. For adolescents, assess Gillick competence and involve them in decision-making. If an adolescent discloses substance use, discuss confidentiality sensitively.
- **Sensitive history**: In adolescents, ask about drug use (cocaine, energy drinks, vaping), sexual activity (pregnancy → PE risk in rare cases), and mental health—ideally with the adolescent alone for part of the consultation.

<Callout title="High Yield Summary">

**1. Paediatric chest pain is overwhelmingly NON-CARDIAC ( > 95%).**

**2. Most common identifiable causes: musculoskeletal (costochondritis, precordial catch, strain) > respiratory (asthma, pneumonia) > GI (GERD) > psychogenic (anxiety/panic).**

**3. Cardiac causes are rare but life-threatening—always screen for RED FLAGS:**
- Exertional chest pain
- Syncope/near-syncope
- Palpitations
- Family history of SCD < 50 years, cardiomyopathy, long QT
- Known congenital heart disease or Kawasaki disease history
- Marfanoid habitus
- Abnormal cardiac exam

**4. Most common cardiac cause of chest pain in children = pericarditis/myocarditis (not ACS!).**

**5. In Hong Kong: Consider Kawasaki disease sequelae (coronary aneurysms), apical HCM variant, high prevalence of asthma, and psychogenic causes in context of academic stress.**

**6. Key clinical sign: chest wall tenderness that reproduces the pain = musculoskeletal (most specific finding in paediatric chest pain workup).**

**7. In infants, "chest pain" manifests as irritability, poor feeding, diaphoresis during feeds (anginal equivalent in ALCAPA).**

**8. Know age-appropriate vital sign normal ranges—do not use adult values!**

**9. ***Pericarditis***: retrosternal pain worse lying flat, better leaning forward, friction rub, diffuse ST elevation on ECG.**

**10. ***HOCM***: leading cause of SCD in young athletes; exertional chest pain/syncope; ejection systolic murmur that increases with Valsalva.**
</Callout>

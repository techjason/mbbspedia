# Innocent Murmur in Paediatrics

## Definition

An **innocent murmur** (also called a **functional**, **physiological**, or **benign** murmur) is a heart murmur heard in a structurally and functionally normal heart. The word "innocent" tells you exactly what it means — it causes no harm, requires no treatment, and has no haemodynamic consequence. These murmurs are produced by normal blood flow patterns through normal cardiac structures, creating audible vibrations. They are extremely common in children and represent one of the most frequent reasons for paediatric cardiology referral.

> The key concept: **An innocent murmur is a diagnosis of exclusion** — you must be confident there is no underlying structural heart disease before labelling a murmur as innocent. However, in experienced hands, clinical features alone are often sufficient to make this diagnosis without further investigation.

<Callout title="Core Principle">
An innocent murmur = normal blood flow through a normal heart producing sound. It is NOT "quiet pathology" — it is physiological turbulence made audible because children have thin chest walls and vigorous circulation.
</Callout>

---

## Epidemiology

- **Prevalence**: Extraordinarily common — up to **50–80% of all children** will have an innocent murmur detected at some point during childhood [1][2]
- **Peak detection**: Most commonly detected between **ages 3–7 years**, when cardiac output is relatively high for body size, and the chest wall is thin
- **Neonatal period**: Up to 0.5–0.9% of neonates have a murmur detected in the first 48 hours; ~50% of these turn out to be innocent, but ALL neonatal murmurs warrant careful assessment because the transitional circulation (closing ductus arteriosus, falling PVR) can mask significant lesions [1]
- **Sex**: No significant sex predilection
- **Natural history**: Most innocent murmurs diminish or disappear by adolescence as the chest wall thickens, heart rate slows, and relative cardiac output decreases
- **Referral burden**: Innocent murmurs account for a large proportion of paediatric cardiology referrals — distinguishing them from pathological murmurs is a critical clinical skill

<Callout title="Why So Common in Children?" type="idea">
Children have:
1. **Thin chest walls** — sound transmits more easily to stethoscope
2. **Higher cardiac output relative to body size** — more vigorous flow = more turbulence
3. **Higher heart rates** — faster flow velocities
4. **Shorter distances** between heart and stethoscope

All of these amplify normal flow sounds. As children grow, the chest wall thickens and these murmurs become inaudible.
</Callout>

## Risk Factors / Conditions That Increase Detection

These are not "risk factors" for disease (since innocent murmurs are normal), but rather **conditions that increase the likelihood of hearing an innocent murmur**:

- **High-output states**: These increase cardiac output and flow velocity, making innocent murmurs louder and more easily detectable:
  - ***Fever*** (↑metabolic rate → ↑CO)
  - ***Anaemia*** (↓viscosity + ↑CO to compensate for reduced O₂ carrying capacity → ↑turbulence)
  - ***Exercise / anxiety / excitement*** (↑sympathetic drive → ↑HR and contractility)
  - ***Hyperthyroidism*** (↑metabolic rate → ↑CO)
  - ***Pregnancy*** (in adolescents — ↑blood volume)
- **Thin body habitus**: Less tissue between heart and stethoscope
- **Chest wall deformities**: Pectus excavatum brings the heart closer to the anterior chest wall

> **Why does anaemia cause a louder murmur?** Because haemoglobin is reduced → the body compensates with ↑cardiac output and ↑stroke volume to maintain O₂ delivery. This ↑flow velocity through normal valves creates more turbulence (Reynolds number increases). Additionally, ↓blood viscosity itself lowers the threshold for turbulent flow (turbulence ∝ velocity × diameter / viscosity).

## Anatomy and Function: The Normal Paediatric Heart

To understand innocent murmurs, you need to understand normal cardiac anatomy and flow patterns:

### Relevant Anatomy
- **Right ventricular outflow tract (RVOT)**: Blood flows from the RV through the infundibulum into the pulmonary artery. This is the most common site for the classic **Still's murmur** vibrations and **pulmonary flow murmur**
- **Pulmonary artery and its branches**: The main PA bifurcates into left and right PAs. In neonates and infants, the **branch PAs are relatively small** compared to the main PA → physiological turbulence at the branch points → **peripheral pulmonary stenosis (PPS) murmur**
- **Aorta and head/neck vessels**: Flow through the brachiocephalic vessels (especially the brachiocephalic trunk and subclavian arteries) can produce a **supraclavicular/carotid bruit**
- **Jugular veins**: Continuous low-pressure flow in the internal jugular veins can produce a **venous hum**
- **Cardiac valves**: All are structurally normal in innocent murmurs — the sound is from flow turbulence, not valve pathology

### Relevant Physiology
- **Laminar vs turbulent flow**: Blood normally flows in a laminar (smooth, layered) pattern. When flow velocity increases past a critical threshold (determined by the **Reynolds number**), flow becomes turbulent and produces audible vibrations
  - **Reynolds number = (velocity × diameter × density) / viscosity**
  - Turbulence is favoured by: ↑velocity, ↑diameter, ↓viscosity
- In children, the combination of high cardiac output, relatively small vessel calibre at certain points, and thin chest walls means that normal turbulence is easily heard

### Fetal-to-Neonatal Transition (Why Timing Matters)
- **In utero**: High PVR, patent ductus arteriosus (PDA), patent foramen ovale (PFO) — most blood bypasses the lungs
- **At birth**: PVR falls rapidly over hours to days; PDA functionally closes (usually by 24–48h, anatomically by 2–3 weeks); PFO closes functionally
- **Clinical implication**: A murmur heard in the first 24 hours may reflect transitional flow (e.g., closing PDA) rather than structural disease, but may also be the first sign of a congenital heart defect (CHD). ***All neonatal murmurs warrant careful assessment*** [1]

## Aetiology and Pathophysiology of Specific Innocent Murmurs

Innocent murmurs are not one entity — there are several **distinct types**, each with a specific anatomical origin and pathophysiological basis. Understanding these individually is important for both diagnosis and for explaining findings to families.

### 1. Still's Murmur (Vibratory Innocent Murmur)

- **The most common innocent murmur in children** [1][2]
- ***Age***: Typically **2–7 years** (school-age children), but can be heard from infancy to adolescence
- **Site of origin**: Vibration of normal cardiac structures, likely the **left ventricular outflow tract (LVOT)** or **chordae tendineae** or the **pulmonary valve leaflets** — exact mechanism debated, but the vibratory quality is characteristic
- **Character**: ***Low-frequency, musical, vibratory, or "twanging string" quality*** — often described as a "buzzing" or "humming" sound. This vibratory quality is the hallmark [1]
- **Timing**: ***Systolic*** — typically early-to-mid systolic (short ejection murmur)
- **Location**: Best heard at the ***lower left sternal border (LLSB)*** or between the LLSB and apex
- **Grade**: Usually ***Grade 1–3/6*** (NEVER ≥4/6, i.e., never associated with a thrill)
- **Radiation**: Minimal — does not radiate widely
- **Variation**: 
  - ***Louder when supine, with fever, exercise, or anaemia*** (all ↑CO)
  - ***Softer or disappears with sitting/standing*** (↓venous return → ↓CO) or with Valsalva
- **Pathophysiology**: The exact mechanism is still debated, but the leading theory involves low-frequency vibration of normal cardiac structures (possibly false tendons/chordae in the LV, or the pulmonic valve cusps) during systolic ejection. The vibratory character distinguishes it from the "blowing" quality of pathological murmurs

> **Why does it disappear on standing?** Standing reduces venous return (blood pools in the legs due to gravity) → ↓preload → ↓stroke volume → ↓flow velocity → less turbulence → murmur fades or disappears.

### 2. Pulmonary Flow Murmur (Pulmonary Ejection Murmur)

- **Common in older children, adolescents, and young adults**
- ***Age***: **8–14 years** typically, but can be heard at any age
- **Site of origin**: Turbulent flow across the **normal pulmonary valve** into the pulmonary artery during systole
- **Character**: ***Soft, blowing, ejection systolic murmur*** (crescendo-decrescendo)
- **Timing**: ***Systolic*** — ejection-type (crescendo-decrescendo with a gap after S1)
- **Location**: Best heard at the ***upper left sternal border (ULSB / pulmonary area)***
- **Grade**: Usually Grade 1–2/6 (soft)
- **Radiation**: Minimal
- **Variation**: Similar to Still's — louder with ↑CO states
- **Pathophysiology**: Blood ejected from the RV into the PA produces turbulence as it passes through the valve. In a thin child with a high cardiac output, this normal turbulence is easily heard. The pulmonary valve area is close to the anterior chest wall, enhancing audibility

<Callout title="Still's vs Pulmonary Flow Murmur" type="idea">
- **Still's**: Musical/vibratory, LLSB, younger children (2–7y)
- **Pulmonary flow**: Blowing/ejection, LUSB, older children/adolescents

Both are systolic, both change with position, both are benign. The key distinction is the **quality** (vibratory vs blowing) and **location** (LLSB vs LUSB).
</Callout>

### 3. Peripheral Pulmonary Stenosis (PPS) Murmur / Physiological Peripheral Pulmonary Artery Stenosis

- ***Age***: **Neonates and young infants** (typically < 6 months, resolves by 3–6 months)
- **Site of origin**: Turbulence at the **branch pulmonary arteries** where they arise from the main PA
- **Character**: ***Soft, blowing, ejection systolic murmur***
- **Timing**: Systolic
- **Location**: Best heard at the ***LUSB*** with **radiation to both axillae and the back** — this bilateral radiation pattern is the hallmark
- **Grade**: Usually Grade 1–2/6
- **Pathophysiology**: In the neonate, the ***main pulmonary artery is relatively large compared to the branch PAs*** (the branches have not yet fully developed). This creates a relative narrowing at the branch points → turbulence as blood flows from a wider conduit into narrower branches. As the infant grows and the branch PAs enlarge, the disproportion resolves and the murmur disappears [1]
- **Important differential**: Must be distinguished from true (pathological) **peripheral pulmonary stenosis** (e.g., Williams syndrome, Alagille syndrome, rubella embryopathy) — in these conditions, the murmur persists and the obstruction is fixed

<Callout title="Key Point: Neonatal Murmur" type="error">
***A murmur in the first 24–48 hours of life should NOT be automatically called innocent.*** The transitional circulation means that even serious lesions (e.g., coarctation, critical AS or PS, TGA) may initially be silent because the PDA is still open. Always re-examine after 6 weeks. However, peripheral pulmonary stenosis murmur is a well-recognised innocent murmur in healthy neonates that radiates to both axillae and the back.
</Callout>

### 4. Venous Hum

- **The most common continuous innocent murmur**
- ***Age***: **3–6 years** (preschool/early school-age)
- **Site of origin**: Turbulence in the **internal jugular veins** as blood returns to the heart, at the point where the jugular vein meets the brachiocephalic vein (or SVC)
- **Character**: ***Low-pitched, continuous "humming" or "roaring" sound*** — present in both systole and diastole
- **Timing**: ***Continuous*** — this is the key feature. It is the ONLY common continuous innocent murmur
- **Location**: Best heard in the ***right (or left) supraclavicular fossa*** or over the upper anterior chest/infraclavicular area, more prominent on the **right side** (because the right internal jugular vein is larger and more direct)
- **Grade**: Usually Grade 1–3/6
- **Variation**: This is the most posturally responsive murmur:
  - ***Louder when sitting or standing*** (↑venous return through jugular veins in upright position due to gravity-assisted drainage)
  - ***Disappears when supine*** (venous flow pattern changes, jugular veins distend, flow becomes less turbulent)
  - ***Disappears or changes with head turning*** (compression of jugular vein)
  - ***Disappears with gentle pressure over the ipsilateral jugular vein*** (directly occluding the source of turbulence)
- **Pathophysiology**: Venous blood draining from the head via the jugular veins produces turbulence at points where veins converge or change calibre. In children, the veins are relatively large compared to the thoracic inlet, and flow is brisk. The continuous nature reflects the fact that venous return is a continuous process (not pulsatile like arterial flow)
- **Critical differential**: ***Patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)*** — also a continuous murmur, but PDA is loudest at the **left infraclavicular area/LUSB**, does NOT disappear with supine position or head turning, and has a "machinery" quality with systolic accentuation

<Callout title="Venous Hum vs PDA" type="error">
Both are continuous murmurs. This is a CLASSIC exam trap.

| Feature | Venous Hum | PDA |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| Location | Supraclavicular / upper chest | Left infraclavicular / LUSB |
| Position | Disappears supine | Unchanged with position |
| Head turning | Disappears | No change |
| Jugular compression | Disappears | No change |
| Quality | Low-pitched hum | "Machinery" with systolic accentuation |
| Thrill | Never | May be present if large |

If a "continuous murmur" disappears when the child lies down → it's a venous hum. If it doesn't → think PDA.
</Callout>

### 5. Supraclavicular / Carotid Bruit (Brachiocephalic Systolic Murmur)

- ***Age***: **Older children and adolescents**
- **Site of origin**: Turbulence in the **brachiocephalic (innominate) artery**, carotid arteries, or subclavian arteries as they arise from the aortic arch
- **Character**: ***Brief, low-to-medium frequency, blowing ejection systolic murmur***
- **Timing**: Systolic — brief and early
- **Location**: ***Supraclavicular fossa***, often bilateral but more prominent on the right. May radiate into the neck
- **Grade**: Usually Grade 1–2/6
- **Variation**: 
  - Reduced or eliminated by **hyperextension of the shoulders** (pulling shoulders backwards compresses the subclavian artery, altering flow and abolishing the murmur) — this is a useful bedside manoeuvre
  - Does NOT radiate down to the precordium (which distinguishes it from aortic stenosis)
- **Pathophysiology**: As blood exits the aortic arch into the brachiocephalic vessels, there is a change in vessel calibre and direction → turbulence, especially at the origin of the brachiocephalic trunk. In children with vigorous circulation, this turbulence is audible

### 6. Mammary Souffle (Less Common, Pubertal)

- ***Age***: **Adolescent females** (during puberty or pregnancy/lactation)
- **Site of origin**: Increased blood flow through the **mammary arteries** supplying the developing breast tissue
- **Character**: Systolic or continuous murmur over the breast
- **Timing**: Can be systolic or continuous
- **Pathophysiology**: Hypervascular breast tissue during puberty or lactation has increased arterial flow → audible turbulence

## Classification of Innocent Murmurs

### By Timing

| Timing | Types |
|--------|-------|
| **Systolic** | Still's murmur, Pulmonary flow murmur, Peripheral pulmonary stenosis, Supraclavicular bruit |
| **Continuous** | Venous hum, (Mammary souffle — can be either) |

> ***There is NO innocent diastolic murmur.*** Any isolated diastolic murmur in a child is pathological until proven otherwise [1][2].

<Callout title="GOLDEN RULE" type="error">
***A diastolic murmur is NEVER innocent.*** If you hear a diastolic murmur, it requires investigation — think aortic regurgitation, mitral stenosis, or pulmonary regurgitation in the context of pulmonary hypertension. This is an absolute exam favourite.
</Callout>

### By Age of Typical Presentation

| Age | Common Innocent Murmur |
|-----|----------------------|
| **Neonate (0–6 months)** | Peripheral pulmonary stenosis murmur |
| **Toddler/Preschool (2–6 years)** | Still's murmur, Venous hum |
| **School-age (6–12 years)** | Still's murmur, Pulmonary flow murmur |
| **Adolescent** | Pulmonary flow murmur, Supraclavicular bruit |

### Summary of the "6 S's" Mnemonic for Characteristics of Innocent Murmurs

> **Mnemonic: The 7 S's of Innocent Murmurs**
> 1. **S**oft (Grade ≤ 3/6, no thrill)
> 2. **S**ystolic (or continuous for venous hum — NEVER diastolic)
> 3. **S**hort duration
> 4. **S**ingle (no associated clicks or gallops)
> 5. **S**mall area (localised, limited radiation)
> 6. **S**itting/standing changes (varies with position)
> 7. **S**ymptomatic of nothing (asymptomatic child, normal growth, no cardiac symptoms)

## Clinical Features

### A. Symptoms (History)

**By definition, children with innocent murmurs are asymptomatic from a cardiovascular perspective.** The murmur is detected incidentally during routine examination or during an examination prompted by an intercurrent illness (e.g., fever, URTI).

Key history points that SUPPORT an innocent murmur:

| History Feature | Significance | Pathophysiological Basis |
|----------------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Asymptomatic** | No exercise intolerance, no syncope, no chest pain, no palpitations, no dyspnoea | Normal cardiac function → no haemodynamic compromise |
| **Normal feeding** (infants) | No excessive sweating, no tiring during feeds, no prolonged feeds | Heart failure from structural lesions causes ↑sympathetic drive during feeding (an "exercise equivalent" for infants) → diaphoresis and fatigue |
| **Normal growth and development** | Plotting on appropriate centiles, meeting milestones | Significant CHD with haemodynamic compromise → failure to thrive due to ↑metabolic demands and ↓nutrient absorption (gut hypoperfusion) |
| **No cyanotic spells** | No blue episodes, no squatting behaviour | Cyanotic CHD → R-to-L shunting → desaturated blood enters systemic circulation |
| **No recurrent respiratory infections** | No frequent chest infections or wheeze | L-to-R shunts (e.g., large VSD, ASD) → ↑pulmonary blood flow → pulmonary congestion → predisposition to respiratory infections |
| **Murmur noted during illness** | Fever, anaemia, or anxiety → murmur detected | High-output state ↑CO → amplifies normal flow turbulence → previously inaudible murmur becomes audible |
| **No family history of** sudden cardiac death, cardiomyopathy, Marfan syndrome, long QT | Absence of inherited cardiac conditions | Genetic conditions predispose to structural/electrical heart disease |
| **No maternal risk factors** | No maternal diabetes, no teratogen exposure, no rubella | These are risk factors for CHD in the offspring |

History features that should make you **CONCERNED for pathological murmur**:

- ***Poor feeding, diaphoresis during feeds, prolonged feeding times*** → heart failure
- ***Failure to thrive*** → chronic haemodynamic compromise
- ***Cyanosis (central)*** → R-to-L shunting or mixing lesion
- ***Syncope or exercise intolerance*** → outflow obstruction (e.g., aortic stenosis, HOCM) or arrhythmia
- ***Chest pain with exertion*** → coronary anomaly or outflow obstruction
- ***Palpitations*** → arrhythmia (may coexist with structural disease)
- ***Recurrent lower respiratory tract infections*** → pulmonary overcirculation from L-to-R shunt
- ***Family history of congenital heart disease, sudden death < 40y, cardiomyopathy, Marfan, long QT syndrome***

### B. Signs (Physical Examination)

The physical examination is the CORNERSTONE of differentiating innocent from pathological murmurs. A systematic cardiovascular examination in a child includes:

#### General Inspection
- **Well-appearing child**: Active, pink, comfortable, age-appropriate growth — supports innocence
- **No dysmorphic features**: Dysmorphic features (e.g., Down syndrome → AVSD; Turner syndrome → coarctation; Williams syndrome → supravalvular AS/PPS; Noonan syndrome → pulmonary stenosis; Marfan → MVP/aortic root dilatation) increase suspicion for associated CHD
- **No cyanosis**: Central cyanosis (lips, tongue) = pathological; peripheral cyanosis (acrocyanosis) in neonates can be normal
- **No clubbing**: Digital clubbing = chronic hypoxia → cyanotic CHD
- **No signs of heart failure**: No tachypnoea, no hepatomegaly, no oedema, no failure to thrive

#### Vital Signs (Age-Appropriate Normal Values)
- **Heart rate**: Must interpret for age (neonates ~120–160 bpm, infants ~100–150, children ~70–120, adolescents ~60–100)
- **Respiratory rate**: Neonates ~30–60, infants ~25–40, children ~20–30, adolescents ~12–20
- **Blood pressure**: Must use appropriate cuff size; compare with age/sex/height percentile charts. **Four-limb BP** if coarctation suspected (> 20 mmHg gradient between upper and lower limbs)
- **Oxygen saturation**: Normal ≥ 95% in room air. ***Pre- and post-ductal saturations*** in neonates (right hand = pre-ductal; either foot = post-ductal) — a > 3% difference or any reading < 95% warrants investigation (neonatal pulse oximetry screening)

#### Peripheral Signs
- **Pulses**: 
  - ***All four limbs palpated*** — femoral pulses are KEY
  - **Absent or weak femoral pulses** → coarctation of the aorta (the most important thing to check — if you check nothing else, check femoral pulses!)
  - **Radio-femoral delay** → coarctation
  - **Bounding pulses** → PDA, aortic regurgitation, or other large arterial run-off lesion
  - **Normal pulses** = supports innocent murmur
- **Perfusion**: Capillary refill time < 2 seconds (normal)

#### Precordial Examination

**Inspection:**
- **Precordial bulge**: Visible prominence of the left chest → chronic cardiomegaly (not expected with innocent murmur)
- **Visible apex beat**: Normal in thin children

**Palpation:**
- **Apex beat**: Normal position (4th intercostal space MCL in children < 7y; 5th ICS MCL in older children)
  - ***Displaced apex*** → ventricular dilatation (e.g., from volume overload in VSD, MR)
  - ***Thrusting apex*** → LV volume overload
  - ***Heaving/lifting apex*** → LV pressure overload (e.g., AS, coarctation)
- **Parasternal heave**: Abnormal — suggests ***RV pressure or volume overload*** (e.g., ASD, pulmonary HTN)
- ***No thrill***: A thrill (palpable vibration = Grade ≥4/6 murmur) is NEVER present with an innocent murmur [1][2]. If you feel a thrill → pathological.

**Auscultation — Characteristics of an Innocent Murmur:**

| Feature | Innocent Murmur | Pathological Murmur |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Intensity** | ***Grade 1–3/6*** (soft to moderate) | May be ≥ 4/6 (with thrill) |
| **Timing** | ***Systolic*** (ejection) or continuous (venous hum only) | Can be systolic, diastolic, or continuous |
| **Duration** | ***Short*** (does not occupy full systole) | May be pan-systolic, long ejection |
| **Quality** | ***Musical, vibratory (Still's), or soft blowing*** | Harsh, blowing, rumbling |
| **S1 and S2** | ***Normal S1, normal physiological splitting of S2*** | Abnormal S2 splitting (e.g., fixed splitting in ASD, single S2 in severe AS/PA), loud P2 |
| **Extra sounds** | ***No ejection clicks, no opening snaps, no S3/S4*** | Ejection clicks (bicuspid aortic valve), opening snaps (MS), gallop rhythms |
| **Radiation** | ***Localised*** (except PPS → bilateral, supraclavicular → neck) | Wide radiation (e.g., AS → carotids, MR → axilla, VSD → entire precordium) |
| **Position change** | ***Changes with position*** (↓ standing, ↑ supine for most; opposite for venous hum) | Usually does NOT change significantly with position |
| **Thrill** | ***NEVER*** | May be present |

<Callout title="S2 Splitting — A Key Auscultatory Feature">
***Normal physiological splitting of S2*** is present and NORMAL in children — it widens on inspiration (↑venous return → delayed pulmonic valve closure) and narrows on expiration.

**Abnormal S2 patterns that suggest pathology:**
- **Fixed splitting**: S2 split does not vary with respiration → ***ASD*** (equalised filling of both ventricles)
- **Single (unsplit) S2**: Only one component heard → severe AS (delayed A2 merges with P2), pulmonary atresia (absent P2), Eisenmenger (loud single P2), truncus arteriosus
- **Widely split S2**: RBBB, pulmonary stenosis
- **Loud P2**: Pulmonary hypertension (↑PA pressure → forceful closure of pulmonic valve)
</Callout>

#### Specific Manoeuvres for Innocent Murmurs

| Manoeuvre | Expected Change in Innocent Murmur | Mechanism |
|-----------|-----------------------------------|-----------|
| **Supine → Sitting/Standing** | ***Softer or disappears*** (except venous hum — louder) | ↓Venous return → ↓preload → ↓SV → ↓flow velocity |
| **Sitting → Supine** | ***Louder*** (except venous hum — disappears) | ↑Venous return → ↑preload → ↑SV → ↑flow velocity |
| **Valsalva** | Softer | ↓Venous return → ↓CO |
| **Head turning (for venous hum)** | ***Disappears*** | Compresses ipsilateral internal jugular vein → obliterates turbulence source |
| **Jugular compression (for venous hum)** | ***Disappears*** | Directly occludes the vein producing the turbulence |
| **Shoulder hyperextension (for supraclavicular bruit)** | ***Disappears*** | Compresses subclavian artery |
| **Fever/exercise** | ***Louder*** | ↑CO → ↑flow velocity → ↑turbulence |

<Callout title="The Bottom Line on Clinical Features" type="idea">
An innocent murmur is:
- Heard in an **asymptomatic, thriving, well-appearing** child
- With **normal pulses** (especially femorals), **no thrill**, **no heave**, **no displaced apex**
- **Normal S1 and S2** with physiological splitting
- **Soft (≤ Grade 3)**, **systolic** (or continuous only if venous hum), **short**, **musical or blowing**
- **Changes with position**
- **No associated clicks, snaps, or gallop rhythms**

If ALL of these criteria are met by an experienced clinician, no further investigation may be needed. When in doubt → echocardiography.
</Callout>

<Callout title="High Yield Summary">

**Definition**: Innocent murmur = heart murmur in a structurally and functionally normal heart; caused by physiological turbulence of normal blood flow.

**Key Facts**:
- 50–80% of children will have one at some point
- Peak age: 3–7 years (for Still's and venous hum)
- ***NEVER diastolic, NEVER ≥ Grade 4 (no thrill), NEVER associated with abnormal S2 or extra heart sounds***
- Normal S1, S2, physiological splitting, no clicks/snaps/gallops
- Changes with position (most ↓ on standing, venous hum ↓ supine)
- Asymptomatic child with normal growth, normal pulses (especially femorals), no cyanosis

**Types to Know**:
1. **Still's** — musical/vibratory, LLSB, 2–7y
2. **Pulmonary flow** — soft blowing ejection, LUSB, older children
3. **Peripheral PS** — neonates, radiates to axillae/back, resolves by 6 months
4. **Venous hum** — continuous, supraclavicular, disappears supine/head turn/jugular compression
5. **Supraclavicular bruit** — supraclavicular, disappears with shoulder hyperextension

**Red Flags (NOT innocent)**:
- Diastolic murmur
- Harsh or loud (≥ 4/6) murmur with thrill
- Fixed splitting or absent splitting of S2, loud P2
- Ejection clicks, gallop rhythms
- Pan-systolic murmur
- Absent/weak femoral pulses
- Cyanosis, failure to thrive, exercise intolerance, syncope
- Dysmorphic features or family history of CHD/sudden death

</Callout>

# Shortness of Breath on Exertion (SOBOE) in Children

## Definition

Shortness of breath on exertion (SOBOE) — also termed **exertional dyspnoea** or **exercise intolerance** — is defined as the subjective experience of an unexpected or disproportionate awareness of breathing difficulty that occurs during physical activity. In infants and young children who cannot verbalise, it manifests as objective signs such as tachypnoea, diaphoresis, or difficulty feeding during effort.

"Dyspnoea" derives from Greek: *dys-* = difficult/abnormal + *pnoia* = breathing. So "dyspnoea" literally means "difficult breathing."

The key physiological concept is a **mismatch** between ventilatory demand and the body's capacity to meet that demand. During exercise, metabolic rate rises → increased CO₂ production and O₂ consumption → the respiratory and cardiovascular systems must augment gas exchange and oxygen delivery. When any component of this chain is impaired — airway, lung parenchyma, chest wall, cardiovascular pump, oxygen-carrying capacity, or neuromuscular apparatus — the child perceives (or demonstrates) exertional breathlessness [1][2].

<Callout title="Paediatric Nuance">
In infants, "exertion" = feeding and crying. A baby who is breathless, sweaty, and tires during feeds is the paediatric equivalent of an adult who gets dyspnoeic walking up stairs. Always ask about feeding behaviour in infants when assessing for SOBOE.
</Callout>

## Epidemiology and Context in Hong Kong

SOBOE is a **symptom, not a diagnosis** — its epidemiology therefore reflects the underlying conditions:

- **Asthma**: Most common chronic respiratory disease in children. Prevalence ~8.6% in Hong Kong (with a declining trend), diagnosed in ~75% of cases before age 7 [3][4]. Hong Kong's subtropical climate, dense housing, and high exposure to house dust mites make atopic asthma particularly prevalent.
- **Congenital heart disease (CHD)**: Incidence ~6–8 per 1,000 live births globally. In Hong Kong, CHD remains the most common congenital anomaly. ***Acyanotic CHD with large left-to-right shunts (VSD, AVSD, PDA)*** are leading causes of heart failure presenting with SOBOE in infancy [1][5].
- **Anaemia**: Iron deficiency anaemia is common in toddlers (especially those on prolonged milk-only diets) and in adolescent girls (menstruation). Thalassaemia trait is highly prevalent in Southern Chinese populations (~3–4% carrier rate for β-thalassaemia, ~3–4% for α-thalassaemia) [6].
- **Obesity**: Rising prevalence in Hong Kong schoolchildren (~20% overweight/obese), contributing to deconditioning and obesity-hypoventilation.
- **Psychogenic hyperventilation**: Increasingly recognised in adolescents, particularly in the context of exam stress in Hong Kong's competitive academic environment.

## Age-Specific Risk Factors

| Age Group | Key Risk Factors for SOBOE |
|---|---|
| **Neonate (0–28 days)** | Prematurity (RDS, BPD), critical CHD (duct-dependent lesions), sepsis, congenital airway anomalies, persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn (PPHN) |
| **Infant (1–12 months)** | Large L→R shunts (VSD, AVSD, PDA) presenting at 2–3 months as PVR drops; bronchiolitis (RSV); aspiration (GOR, TEF); BPD |
| **Toddler/Preschool (1–5 years)** | Asthma (viral-induced wheeze), foreign body aspiration, myocarditis, anaemia |
| **School-age (6–12 years)** | Asthma, exercise-induced bronchoconstriction, rheumatic heart disease (less common in HK but still seen in new immigrants), cardiomyopathy, anaemia |
| **Adolescent (12–18 years)** | Asthma, exercise-induced laryngeal obstruction (EILO, previously "vocal cord dysfunction"), pneumothorax (tall thin males), psychogenic hyperventilation, arrhythmias (e.g. SVT, long QT syndrome), pulmonary embolism (rare but consider in OCP use, immobility, SLE) |

## Anatomy and Physiology Relevant to SOBOE

### A. The Oxygen Delivery Chain

To understand why a child gets breathless on exertion, think of the **oxygen delivery chain** — a series of steps, any of which can be the bottleneck:

```
Atmosphere → Upper airway → Lower airway → Alveoli → Alveolar-capillary membrane
→ Haemoglobin binding → Cardiac output → Peripheral O₂ delivery → Cellular utilisation
```

1. **Upper airway**: Must be patent. In children, the airway is proportionally narrower (subglottic region is the narrowest part in children < 8 years, unlike adults where it is the glottis). Poiseuille's Law tells us resistance ∝ 1/r⁴ — even 1 mm of mucosal oedema in an infant's 4 mm trachea reduces cross-sectional area by ~75% and increases resistance 16-fold.

2. **Lower airways**: Bronchi and bronchioles. Smooth muscle tone, mucosal inflammation, and mucus all affect calibre. In asthma, chronic inflammation → bronchial hyperreactivity → bronchoconstriction + mucus plugging + oedema during triggers.

3. **Alveoli and gas exchange surface**: Must have adequate surface area and thin alveolar-capillary membrane. In BPD or interstitial lung disease, this is compromised.

4. **Pulmonary vasculature**: Must allow adequate blood flow past alveoli for gas exchange. In pulmonary hypertension, elevated PVR reduces pulmonary blood flow or causes V/Q mismatch.

5. **Cardiac pump**: Must generate adequate cardiac output (CO = HR × SV). In heart failure, the heart cannot augment CO sufficiently during exercise. In left-to-right shunts, pulmonary overcirculation leads to pulmonary congestion → ↓lung compliance → ↑work of breathing.

6. **Oxygen-carrying capacity**: Haemoglobin concentration and function. In anaemia, reduced Hb → reduced O₂-carrying capacity → the cardiovascular system compensates by ↑HR and ↑SV, but this is insufficient during exercise → SOBOE.

7. **Neuromuscular apparatus**: The respiratory muscles (diaphragm, intercostals, accessory muscles) must function. In neuromuscular diseases (e.g. Duchenne muscular dystrophy, spinal muscular atrophy, myasthenia gravis), respiratory muscle weakness limits ventilatory capacity.

### B. Paediatric Physiological Differences from Adults

| Parameter | Paediatric Feature | Clinical Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **Resting respiratory rate** | Higher in younger children (neonate 30–60, infant 25–40, child 20–30, adolescent 12–20) | Must use age-appropriate normals to identify tachypnoea |
| **Tidal volume** | ~6–8 mL/kg (same as adults per kg) | Minute ventilation is rate-dependent — infants increase MV mainly by ↑RR, not ↑TV |
| **Chest wall compliance** | Very compliant (cartilaginous ribs) | Intercostal recession/subcostal retraction is readily seen with ↑work of breathing |
| **Functional residual capacity (FRC)** | Lower relative to closing volume | Prone to atelectasis and desaturation during increased demand |
| **Resting heart rate** | Higher in younger children (neonate 120–160, infant 100–150, child 70–120, adolescent 60–100) | Tachycardia thresholds differ by age |
| **Myocardial compliance** | Neonatal/infant myocardium is less compliant (more dependent on HR for CO) | Limited ability to increase SV → relies on HR to increase CO |
| **Oxygen consumption** | Higher per kg than adults (~6–8 mL/kg/min vs ~3.5 mL/kg/min) | Less reserve; desaturates faster when oxygen delivery is impaired |

### C. Normal Cardiovascular and Respiratory Response to Exercise in Children

During normal exercise:
- **↑ Sympathetic drive** → ↑HR, ↑myocardial contractility, ↑SV → ↑CO (up to 4–6× resting)
- **↑ Minute ventilation** (up to 20× resting) via ↑RR and ↑TV
- **Peripheral vasodilation** in exercising muscles → ↓SVR → allows greater flow
- **↑ O₂ extraction** by tissues (widened A-VO₂ difference)

When any step is impaired, the mismatch between metabolic demand and oxygen delivery is perceived as breathlessness.

## Aetiology (with Focus on Hong Kong Paediatric Practice)

The causes of SOBOE in children can be broadly categorised. The mnemonic **"CARDIAC RESP"** may help:

### I. Respiratory Causes (Most Common Overall)

#### 1. Asthma / Reactive Airway Disease

- ***Most common chronic respiratory disease in children*** [3][4]
- **Pathophysiology**: Chronic airway inflammation (Th2-mediated in atopic asthma: IL-4, IL-5, IL-13 → IgE production, eosinophil recruitment) → bronchial hyperresponsiveness → episodic bronchoconstriction + mucus hypersecretion + mucosal oedema → variable airflow obstruction → ↑work of breathing → dyspnoea
- ***Risk factors***: atopy (IgE-mediated sensitisation to environmental allergens — house dust mites, cockroach, pet dander particularly relevant in Hong Kong's dense housing), family history, male sex in childhood, obesity, environmental tobacco smoke exposure [3][4]
- **Why exertional?** Exercise → airway cooling and drying (especially in mouth-breathing during vigorous activity) → osmotic changes in airway lining fluid → mast cell degranulation → bronchoconstriction (exercise-induced bronchoconstriction, EIB). Typically occurs 5–15 minutes after onset of vigorous exercise and peaks 5–10 minutes after stopping.

#### 2. Recurrent/Chronic Lower Respiratory Tract Infections

- ***Recurrent chest infections*** are a feature of both cardiac and respiratory causes of SOBOE [1]
- Pulmonary congestion from left-to-right shunts creates a "wet lung" environment that predisposes to infection
- Primary immunodeficiency, ciliary dyskinesia, cystic fibrosis (rare in Chinese, ~1:30,000 vs 1:3,000 in Caucasians), bronchiectasis

#### 3. Foreign Body Aspiration

- Peak age 1–3 years (oral exploratory phase + immature laryngeal reflexes)
- Pathophysiology: FB lodges in bronchus (right > left due to anatomy) → ball-valve obstruction → hyperinflation distally, or complete obstruction → atelectasis → V/Q mismatch → dyspnoea
- History of acute choking episode followed by persistent/recurrent cough and wheeze

#### 4. Bronchopulmonary Dysplasia (BPD) / Chronic Lung Disease of Prematurity

- In ex-premature infants (especially < 28 weeks, < 1000 g)
- Pathophysiology: arrested alveolar and vascular development + inflammation from O₂ toxicity and barotrauma → reduced gas exchange surface area → chronic hypoxia → pulmonary hypertension → exertional dyspnoea persisting into childhood

#### 5. Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD) in Children (chILD)

- Rare but important. Includes conditions like neuroendocrine cell hyperplasia of infancy (NEHI), pulmonary alveolar proteinosis, surfactant dysfunction disorders
- Pathophysiology: thickened interstitium → impaired gas diffusion → hypoxaemia → dyspnoea

#### 6. Pneumothorax

- Spontaneous: tall, thin adolescent males
- Secondary: asthma, CF
- Pathophysiology: air in pleural space → lung collapse → V/Q mismatch + ↓ventilatory capacity → acute dyspnoea

#### 7. Pleural Effusion

- Parapneumonic (most common in children), TB (consider in Hong Kong), malignancy (lymphoma)
- Pathophysiology: fluid compresses lung → restrictive physiology → ↓lung volumes → dyspnoea

#### 8. Upper Airway Obstruction

- **Adenotonsillar hypertrophy**: Very common in preschool children → obstructive sleep apnoea (***enlarged tonsils or adenoids: important factor in children as small airway***) → chronic nocturnal hypoxia → daytime fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance [7]
- **Exercise-induced laryngeal obstruction (EILO)**: Adolescents, especially athletic females → paradoxical vocal cord adduction during exercise → inspiratory stridor and dyspnoea → often misdiagnosed as asthma

### II. Cardiac Causes

#### 1. Congenital Heart Disease — Acyanotic (Left-to-Right Shunts)

***Heart failure = cardiac output cannot meet demand → a mismatch!*** [1]

***Large L-to-R shunts: VSD, AVSD, PDA, ASD (rarely)*** are the commonest cause of heart failure in infants aged 2–3 months [1][5].

**Why does the timing matter?** At birth, pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) is high (nearly systemic levels). Over the first 6–8 weeks of life, PVR drops as the pulmonary vasculature remodels. As PVR falls, the pressure gradient across the defect increases → more blood shunts left-to-right → **pulmonary overcirculation** → pulmonary congestion → ↓lung compliance → ↑work of breathing → ***breathlessness, especially on exertion*** [1][5].

Additionally, the volume-overloaded left heart dilates → ↑myocardial wall stress → ↑myocardial O₂ demand → eventually the heart cannot cope → heart failure.

**Clinical features in infants with large L-to-R shunts** [1][5]:
- ***Poor feeding*** (infant equivalent of exercise intolerance — sucking is hard work for a baby!)
- ***Failure to thrive*** (↑metabolic demand from increased work of breathing + heart failure + recurrent infections)
- ***Excessive sweating*** (sympathetic activation compensating for ↓CO)
- ***Recurrent chest infections*** (wet, congested lungs are a good culture medium)
- ***Tachypnoea, tachycardia*** (compensatory)

#### 2. Congenital Heart Disease — Cyanotic

- Tetralogy of Fallot (most common cyanotic CHD): **hypercyanotic/"tet" spells** — sudden ↑R→L shunt → profound desaturation → SOBOE, squatting (increases SVR → reduces R→L shunt → improves pulmonary blood flow)
- Transposition of Great Arteries (TGA), Truncus arteriosus, TAPVR, etc.

#### 3. Left Ventricular Outflow Tract (LVOT) Obstruction

***Aortic stenosis (AS)***: Can be valvar, subvalvar, or supravalvar [5].
- ***Pathophysiology***: LV pressure overload → concentric LVH → ↑myocardial O₂ demand + ↓coronary perfusion (from ↑LV end-diastolic pressure compressing coronary arteries) → subendocardial ischaemia during exercise → ***↓exercise tolerance with SOB, chest pain, syncope, sudden cardiac death on exertion in adolescence*** [5]
- ***Valvar AS associated with Turner syndrome*** [5]
- ***Supravalvar AS: usually in Williams syndrome*** [5]

***Coarctation of the aorta (CoA)***: Narrowing of the aorta, usually at the isthmus. In neonates with critical CoA, duct-dependent systemic circulation → HF upon duct closure. In older children, may present with SOBOE, upper limb hypertension, and radio-femoral delay.

#### 4. Cardiomyopathy

- **Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM)**: Most common cardiomyopathy in children. Causes include idiopathic, post-viral myocarditis, metabolic (fatty acid oxidation defects, mitochondrial disease), neuromuscular (Duchenne), anthracycline cardiotoxicity [1]
- **Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM)**: ***LVOT obstruction occurs in ~1/3*** → ↑afterload → ↓CO → exertional dyspnoea + syncope + sudden cardiac death [8]. Diastolic dysfunction → ↑LV filling pressure → pulmonary venous congestion → SOBOE.

#### 5. Myocarditis

- Viral (Coxsackie B, adenovirus, parvovirus B19, SARS-CoV-2)
- Pathophysiology: inflammatory infiltrate in myocardium → ↓contractility → heart failure → SOBOE
- Can present acutely with fulminant HF or insidiously

#### 6. Arrhythmias

- ***SVT*** (most common childhood tachyarrhythmia, may be WPW-related) and ***VT***: sustained tachycardia → ↓diastolic filling time → ↓CO → HF [1]
- ***Congenital heart block***: ↓HR → ↓CO if severe → SOBOE [1]

#### 7. Acquired Valvular Disease

- ***Rheumatic heart disease***: Still seen in Hong Kong, particularly in children from mainland China or South/Southeast Asia. Mitral regurgitation (acute) or mitral stenosis (chronic) → pulmonary congestion → SOBOE
- ***Kawasaki disease complications***: Coronary artery aneurysms → MI → myocardial dysfunction [1]

#### 8. Pulmonary Hypertension

- Primary (idiopathic PAH — rare) or secondary (to CHD with Eisenmenger syndrome, chronic lung disease, thromboembolic disease)
- Pathophysiology: ↑PVR → RV pressure overload → RV failure → ↓CO → SOBOE [9]

### III. Haematological Causes

#### 1. Anaemia

- **Iron deficiency anaemia** (dietary, especially in toddlers on cow's milk), **thalassaemia** (very relevant in Hong Kong — β-thalassaemia major/intermedia, HbH disease), **chronic disease**, **aplastic anaemia**, **leukaemia**
- Pathophysiology: ↓Hb → ↓O₂-carrying capacity → compensatory ↑HR and ↑CO at rest → limited reserve during exercise → SOBOE + fatigue
- In severe chronic anaemia, high-output cardiac failure can develop [1]

### IV. Neuromuscular Causes

- **Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD)**: X-linked, progressive weakness → respiratory muscle weakness → ↓ventilatory capacity → SOBOE (typically by late childhood/adolescence) + cardiomyopathy
- **Spinal muscular atrophy (SMA)**: Anterior horn cell degeneration → respiratory muscle weakness
- **Myasthenia gravis**: Rare in children but can occur (neonatal transient or juvenile MG). Fatiguable weakness → respiratory muscles → SOBOE [10]
- **Guillain-Barré syndrome**: Acute ascending weakness → can affect respiratory muscles → respiratory failure

### V. Metabolic / Systemic Causes

- **Metabolic acidosis** (DKA, inborn errors of metabolism, renal tubular acidosis): Kussmaul's breathing — deep, laboured breathing is the body's attempt to blow off CO₂ to compensate for metabolic acidosis. This can be perceived as dyspnoea on exertion [2][11]
- **Thyrotoxicosis**: ↑metabolic rate → ↑O₂ demand → ↑HR → SOBOE + tremor, weight loss, goitre
- **Obesity**: ↑work of breathing (heavy chest wall, ↑abdominal pressure on diaphragm) + deconditioning + possible obesity-hypoventilation syndrome [12]

### VI. Psychogenic

- **Hyperventilation syndrome / panic disorder**: Particularly in adolescents. Anxiety → ↑RR → respiratory alkalosis → perioral/peripheral tingling, lightheadedness. Characteristically occurs at rest or after (not during) exertion, lacks objective signs of respiratory distress, and may be associated with sighing.
- **Functional breathing disorders**: Disproportionate symptom perception relative to normal physiological testing

<Callout title="Clinical Pearl" type="idea">
The most common causes of SOBOE in a paediatric outpatient setting are **asthma**, **deconditioning/obesity**, and **psychogenic hyperventilation**. In the inpatient setting, think **heart failure (CHD, myocarditis, cardiomyopathy)** and **severe respiratory infection**. Always remember **anaemia** as a silent cause — it's easy to miss if you don't check a haemoglobin.
</Callout>

## Classification of SOBOE in Children

### A. By Tempo

| Tempo | Examples | Clinical Clue |
|---|---|---|
| **Acute** (minutes to hours) | Asthma exacerbation, pneumothorax, anaphylaxis, acute myocarditis, arrhythmia, FB aspiration, acute chest syndrome (sickle cell) | Sudden onset, previously well |
| **Subacute** (days to weeks) | Pneumonia, pleural effusion, pericardial effusion, heart failure from L→R shunt (as PVR drops), anaemia | Progressive worsening |
| **Chronic** (weeks to months) | Asthma (poorly controlled), CHD, cardiomyopathy, chronic anaemia, ILD, BPD, obesity, neuromuscular disease | Longstanding, often with growth failure |

### B. By Organ System

| System | Conditions |
|---|---|
| **Respiratory** | Asthma, bronchiolitis, pneumonia, FB, BPD, ILD, CF, pleural effusion, pneumothorax, EILO, upper airway obstruction |
| **Cardiac** | CHD (acyanotic shunts, cyanotic lesions, LVOT obstruction), cardiomyopathy, myocarditis, arrhythmia, pericardial effusion/tamponade, acquired VHD, pulmonary HTN |
| **Haematological** | Iron deficiency anaemia, thalassaemia, sickle cell disease, leukaemia, aplastic anaemia |
| **Neuromuscular** | DMD, SMA, MG, GBS, diaphragmatic paralysis |
| **Metabolic/Systemic** | DKA, inborn errors, thyrotoxicosis, obesity, renal failure (acidosis) |
| **Psychogenic** | Hyperventilation, panic disorder, functional breathing disorder |

### C. By Mechanism (Pathophysiological Classification)

| Mechanism | Why Breathless? | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **↑ Airway resistance** | ↑work of breathing to overcome obstruction | Asthma, FB, croup, EILO |
| **↓ Lung compliance** | Stiff lungs require more effort to inflate | Pulmonary oedema (HF), pneumonia, ILD, ARDS |
| **↓ Gas exchange** | Hypoxaemia → ↑respiratory drive | ILD, pulmonary HTN, PE, V/Q mismatch |
| **↓ Cardiac output** | Inadequate O₂ delivery to tissues despite normal lungs | HF, cardiomyopathy, arrhythmia, AS, tamponade |
| **↓ O₂ carrying capacity** | Normal CO but insufficient Hb to carry O₂ | Anaemia, methaemoglobinaemia, CO poisoning |
| **↑ Metabolic demand** | Demand outstrips normal supply capacity | Thyrotoxicosis, fever, sepsis |
| **Respiratory muscle weakness** | Cannot generate sufficient ventilatory force | DMD, SMA, MG, GBS |
| **↑ Respiratory drive** | Central drive to breathe is abnormally high | Metabolic acidosis (DKA), psychogenic |
| **Mechanical restriction** | Chest wall/diaphragm movement limited | Obesity, kyphoscoliosis, massive ascites, abdominal distension |

## Clinical Features

### A. Symptoms (with Pathophysiological Basis)

The history is the most important tool. In young children, the history comes from caregivers; in older children and adolescents, take history from the child directly as well.

#### 1. Exertional Dyspnoea / Exercise Intolerance

- **In infants**: ***Poor feeding, failure to thrive, shortness of breath (especially on exertion), excessive sweating*** [1][5]
  - *Why poor feeding?* Feeding requires coordination of sucking, swallowing, and breathing. In HF or significant respiratory disease, the infant cannot sustain this effort → takes small volumes, falls asleep at the breast/bottle, is diaphoretic during feeds
  - *Why excessive sweating?* Sympathetic nervous system activation (catecholamine surge) to compensate for ↓CO → activates eccrine sweat glands. In infants, this is most prominent on the head/forehead during feeding
  - *Why FTT?* ↑metabolic demand (↑work of breathing + ↑cardiac work + recurrent infections) + ↓caloric intake (poor feeding) = negative energy balance → failure to thrive

- **In young children**: ***GI symptoms (abdominal pain, nausea/vomiting, decreased appetite), FTT, easy fatigability, recurrent or chronic cough with wheezing*** [1]
  - *Why GI symptoms?* Hepatic congestion from RV failure → capsular stretching → abdominal pain; mesenteric venous congestion → nausea and ↓appetite. Also, in asthma/respiratory disease, air swallowing (aerophagia) and increased abdominal pressure from accessory muscle use contribute

- **In older children and adolescents**: ***Exercise intolerance, anorexia, abdominal pain, wheezing, dyspnoea, oedema, palpitation, chest pain, syncope*** [1]
  - ***Malaise, SOB on exertion, ↓exercise tolerance, weight gain (fluid retention), orthopnoea (rare in children)*** [1]
  - *Why is orthopnoea rare in children?* Children have lower left atrial pressures and more compliant pulmonary vasculature → pulmonary oedema from postural redistribution is less pronounced than in adults

#### 2. Cough

- **Cardiac cough**: Pulmonary venous congestion → interstitial and peribronchial oedema → bronchial irritation → cough. Typically worse at night/when supine. ***Recurrent lower respiratory tract infections*** are common in L→R shunt lesions [1]
- **Asthmatic cough**: Airway inflammation + bronchoconstriction → cough (often nocturnal, exercise-induced, or triggered by cold air/allergens)
- **Chronic wet cough**: Think protracted bacterial bronchitis, bronchiectasis, CF, immunodeficiency

#### 3. Wheeze

- Polyphonic expiratory wheeze → small airway obstruction (asthma, bronchiolitis)
- ***Wheezing in infants with heart failure*** [1]: Peribronchial oedema from pulmonary venous congestion → compression of small airways → wheezing (often called "cardiac asthma" — a misleading term but important to recognise). This is why infants with VSD may be misdiagnosed with "recurrent bronchiolitis."

#### 4. Palpitations

- Awareness of heartbeat. In the context of SOBOE: consider arrhythmia (SVT, VT), or simply sinus tachycardia as a compensatory response to anaemia, HF, or hypoxia

#### 5. Chest Pain

- In children, chest pain is usually musculoskeletal or idiopathic. Cardiac causes are rare but must be considered:
  - ***Exertional chest pain in AS***: subendocardial ischaemia from ↑LV mass + ↓coronary perfusion [5]
  - Myocarditis: inflammation → pericardial irritation
  - Pericarditis: sharp, positional, relieved by leaning forward

#### 6. Syncope/Pre-syncope

- ***Syncope on exertion is a red flag*** — may indicate severe AS, HCM, long QT syndrome, or catecholaminergic polymorphic VT
- Mechanism in AS: inability to ↑CO during exercise + peripheral vasodilation → sudden ↓BP → cerebral hypoperfusion → syncope [5][8]

#### 7. Growth Failure / Failure to Thrive

- ***FTT is a hallmark of chronic cardiac disease in infancy*** [1]
- Also seen in chronic lung disease (BPD, CF) and chronic anaemia
- Mechanism: ↑resting metabolic rate + ↓intake + possible malabsorption

#### 8. Recurrent Respiratory Infections

- ***Recurrent chest infections*** [1][5]: pulmonary congestion from L→R shunt → fluid-filled lungs → impaired mucociliary clearance → bacterial superinfection
- Also a feature of immunodeficiency, aspiration, ciliary dyskinesia, CF

### B. Signs (with Pathophysiological Basis)

#### 1. General Inspection

- **Failure to thrive / wasting**: Plot weight, height, and head circumference on growth charts. Chronic cardiorespiratory disease → weight affected before height (suggesting caloric deficit rather than endocrine cause)
- **Cyanosis**: Central (tongue, lips) suggests intracardiac R→L shunt or severe V/Q mismatch. Peripheral (acrocyanosis) may be normal in neonates or reflect poor perfusion (↓CO)
- **Clubbing**: In paediatrics, consider cyanotic CHD (most common cause), bronchiectasis, CF, lung abscess, ILD, infective endocarditis
- **Dysmorphic features**: ***Turner syndrome (valvar AS, CoA)***, ***Williams syndrome "elfin facies" (supravalvar AS)*** [5], Down syndrome (AVSD), DiGeorge syndrome (conotruncal defects — ToF, interrupted aortic arch, truncus arteriosus)
- **Nutritional status**: Obesity (deconditioning), pallor (anaemia)

#### 2. Vital Signs (Age-Appropriate Normal Values)

| Age | Heart Rate (bpm) | Respiratory Rate (/min) | Systolic BP (mmHg) | SpO₂ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Neonate | 120–160 | 30–60 | 60–80 | > 95% (> 3% pre-post ductal difference is abnormal) |
| Infant | 100–150 | 25–40 | 70–90 | > 95% |
| 1–5 years | 80–130 | 20–30 | 80–100 | > 95% |
| 6–12 years | 70–120 | 15–25 | 90–110 | > 95% |
| Adolescent | 60–100 | 12–20 | 100–120 | > 95% |

- ***Tachycardia***: Compensatory mechanism for ↓CO (Frank-Starling mechanism exhausted → HR must increase to maintain CO). Also reflects sympathetic activation [1]
- ***Tachypnoea***: Compensatory for ↓lung compliance (pulmonary oedema), hypoxaemia, or acidosis [1]

<Callout title="Exam Tip" type="error">
Do NOT use adult heart rate and respiratory rate thresholds for children. A "normal" adult HR of 90 bpm would be bradycardic in an infant. Always reference age-appropriate values.
</Callout>

#### 3. Signs of Increased Work of Breathing

- ***Subcostal/intercostal recession (insucking)***: The highly compliant paediatric chest wall is drawn inward when negative intrapleural pressures are excessive (from ↑airway resistance or ↓lung compliance) [1]
- ***Nasal flaring***: Attempts to ↓airway resistance by widening nasal aperture
- ***Head bobbing*** (in infants): Sternocleidomastoid contraction with each breath = accessory muscle use
- ***Grunting***: Expiration against a partially closed glottis to generate auto-PEEP and maintain FRC → prevents alveolar collapse (seen in neonatal RDS, pneumonia, HF)
- ***Laboured breathing*** [1]
- **Use of accessory muscles**: Trapezius, sternocleidomastoid → indicates significant respiratory distress

#### 4. Respiratory Signs

- **Wheeze**: Expiratory → small airway obstruction (asthma, cardiac asthma); inspiratory (stridor) → large airway obstruction
- **Crackles/crepitations**: Fine inspiratory crackles at lung bases → pulmonary oedema (LV failure) or ILD; coarse crackles → secretions (bronchitis, pneumonia, bronchiectasis)
- **Reduced air entry**: Pleural effusion, pneumothorax, consolidation
- **Hyperinflated chest**: Chronic obstructive airway disease (asthma, BPD)

#### 5. Cardiovascular Signs

**Compensatory mechanisms** [1]:
- ***Tachycardia*** (as above)
- ***Cardiomegaly*** (displaced apex beat — palpable lateral to the mid-clavicular line): Volume overload → chamber dilatation → apex displacement. In neonates, the **cardiothoracic ratio (CTR) ≥ 0.6 is abnormal** (vs ≥ 0.5 in older children/adults) [1]

**Pulmonary venous congestion** [1]:
- ***Tachypnoea*** (as above)
- ***Fine basal crepitations***: Fluid transudation into alveoli from elevated pulmonary venous pressure

**Systemic venous congestion** [1]:
- ***Hepatomegaly***: The liver is the most reliable sign of right heart failure in children (because JVP is difficult to assess in infants and peripheral oedema is a late finding). Mechanism: ↑systemic venous pressure → hepatic venous congestion → liver enlargement → palpable, tender liver edge
- ***Distension of neck veins*** (JVP): Difficult to assess in infants due to short, fat necks [1]
- ***Facial puffiness*** (more common than ankle oedema in children) [1]: Why? In children, tissue turgor is different, they spend more time supine/recumbent → gravitational oedema distributes to face and periorbital regions rather than ankles
- ***Peripheral oedema***: Late sign in children; in infants, may manifest as sacral oedema or generalised oedema

**↓ Cardiac output** [1]:
- ***Cool extremities***: Peripheral vasoconstriction to redistribute blood to vital organs
- ***Decreased pulse volume*** (weak, thready pulses): ↓SV → ↓pulse pressure
- ***Prolonged capillary refill*** ( > 2 seconds): Reflects ↓peripheral perfusion
- ***Gallop rhythm (S3)***: Rapid ventricular filling into a dilated, volume-overloaded ventricle → third heart sound. Pathological in older children (normal in some young children)

**Murmurs**: Depend on underlying lesion:
- Pansystolic murmur at LLSE → VSD
- Continuous machinery murmur → PDA
- ***Ejection systolic murmur at LMSB to RUSB radiating to bilateral neck → aortic stenosis*** [5]
- ***Ejection click*** before ESM → indicates valvar AS [5]
- Mid-diastolic rumble at apex → relative MS from increased flow across MV in large VSD/PDA

#### 6. Signs Specific to Aortic Stenosis (Important for Exams) [5]

| Severity | Signs |
|---|---|
| ***Mild valvar AS*** | Ejection click + ESM at LVOT (LMSB → RUSB) radiating to neck |
| ***Severe valvar AS*** | ***Weak, slow-rising anacrotic pulse*** + LV impulse + suprasternal thrill + ***delayed/absent A2*** + ejection click + ESM |
| ***Critical AS (neonate)*** | ***HF with shock: ↓BP, weak pulses, cold peripheries*** + RV impulse (RV supporting systemic circulation via PDA) + ***absent/soft murmur*** (minimal flow across AV) |
| ***Subvalvar AS*** | As above but ***no ejection click + no change in A2*** + commonly associated ***EDM of AR*** |
| ***Supravalvar AS*** | As above but ***no ejection click*** + ***signs of Williams syndrome (elfin facies)*** + ***RUL pulse > LUL*** (Coandă effect: jet directed preferentially into innominate artery) |

#### 7. Other Important Signs

- **Pulse oximetry**: Pre- and post-ductal saturations in neonates (> 3% difference suggests R→L ductal shunting)
- **Blood pressure in all four limbs**: Radio-femoral delay + upper > lower limb BP gradient > 20 mmHg → coarctation of aorta
- **Pallor**: Anaemia → inspect conjunctivae, palms, nail beds

## Paediatric Heart Failure Staging and Severity

### A. Paediatric Heart Failure Staging [1]

| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| **A** | At risk for HF but no structural heart disease or symptoms |
| **B** | Structural heart disease present but no symptoms of HF |
| **C** | Structural heart disease with current or past symptoms of HF |
| **D** | End-stage HF requiring advanced therapies (mechanical support, transplant) |

### B. Ross Classification (Paediatric Heart Failure Functional Classification) [1]

This is the paediatric equivalent of the NYHA classification used in adults:

| Class | Infants | Older Children |
|---|---|---|
| **I** | Asymptomatic | Asymptomatic |
| **II** | Mild tachypnoea or diaphoresis with feeding; no growth failure | Dyspnoea on exertion |
| **III** | Marked tachypnoea or diaphoresis with feeding; prolonged feeding time; growth failure | Marked dyspnoea on exertion |
| **IV** | Symptomatic at rest: tachypnoea, retractions, grunting, diaphoresis | Symptoms at rest |

<Callout title="High Yield: Ross Classification">
The Ross Classification is the standard way to grade heart failure severity in paediatrics, analogous to NYHA in adults. For infants, feeding difficulty is the key discriminator. For older children, exercise intolerance is the key feature. ***SOBOE in older children corresponds to Ross Class II–III.***
</Callout>

## History-Taking Framework for SOBOE in a Child

When approaching a child with SOBOE, a systematic history is essential:

### 1. Characterise the Symptom
- **Onset**: Acute (hours) vs chronic (weeks/months)? Any precipitant?
- **Severity**: What level of activity triggers it? How far can the child walk/run? Can the infant complete a feed? How much milk per feed and how long does it take?
- **Progression**: Getting worse, stable, or improving?
- **Timing**: Constant vs intermittent? Worse at particular times (nocturnal → asthma, HF; exercise-related → asthma, cardiac)?
- **Associated symptoms**: Cough (dry/wet?), wheeze, chest pain, palpitations, syncope, cyanosis, ankle swelling, orthopnoea, PND

### 2. Respiratory Screen
- ***Cough*** character (dry, wet, barking), timing, triggers [3]
- Wheeze (expiratory → small airway; inspiratory stridor → large airway)
- History of atopy: eczema, allergic rhinitis, food allergy
- Environmental triggers: dust, pets, tobacco smoke, incense
- Recurrent chest infections
- Choking episode (FB aspiration)

### 3. Cardiac Screen
- ***Breathlessness, especially on exertion*** [5]
- ***Poor feeding / excessive sweating*** during feeds (infants) [1][5]
- ***Failure to thrive*** [1][5]
- ***Recurrent chest infections*** [1][5]
- ***Exercise incapacity in older children*** [5]
- Cyanotic episodes (blue spells)
- Squatting (in ToF)
- Syncope/pre-syncope on exertion (red flag for AS, HCM, arrhythmia)

### 4. Growth and Development
- Growth chart review (weight, height, head circumference)
- Developmental milestones (***delayed motor milestones may result from chronic cardiac disease***) [1]

### 5. Past History
- Prematurity and neonatal history (BPD, mechanical ventilation)
- Previous cardiac/respiratory diagnoses
- Previous surgeries (especially cardiac)
- Recurrent admissions for respiratory infections

### 6. Drug History
- Current medications (inhalers, cardiac medications)
- Compliance

### 7. Family History
- CHD, cardiomyopathy, sudden cardiac death (HCM, channelopathies)
- Asthma, atopy
- Thalassaemia, sickle cell
- Neuromuscular diseases

### 8. Social History
- Passive smoke exposure
- Housing conditions (dust, damp, overcrowding — all relevant for asthma in Hong Kong)
- School performance and physical activity level
- Psychological stress (consider psychogenic causes in adolescents)

## Approach to Examination for SOBOE

A structured examination should assess:

1. **General**: Growth parameters, dysmorphic features, nutritional status, conscious level
2. **Vital signs**: HR, RR, BP (four limbs if cardiac cause suspected), SpO₂ (pre- and post-ductal in neonates), temperature
3. **Respiratory examination**: Inspection (chest shape, work of breathing, symmetry), palpation (tracheal position, chest expansion), percussion, auscultation
4. **Cardiovascular examination**: Inspection (precordial bulge, visible pulsations), palpation (apex beat location and character, heaves, thrills), auscultation (heart sounds, murmurs, added sounds), peripheral perfusion, hepatomegaly, JVP (if assessable), oedema
5. **Abdominal examination**: Hepatomegaly (RHF), splenomegaly (haematological causes), ascites
6. **Neuromuscular**: Tone, power, Gowers' sign (DMD), facial weakness, ptosis (MG)

<Callout title="High Yield Summary">

**Definition**: SOBOE = disproportionate awareness of breathing difficulty during physical activity. In infants: manifests as poor feeding, diaphoresis, tachypnoea during feeds.

**Most Common Causes in Children**:
- Respiratory: ***Asthma*** (most common chronic cause), recurrent LRTI, FB aspiration
- Cardiac: ***Large L→R shunts (VSD, AVSD, PDA)*** causing HF at 2–3 months as PVR drops; ***LVOT obstruction (AS)*** → exertional syncope/chest pain/SOB in adolescence; cardiomyopathy; arrhythmias
- Haematological: ***Anaemia*** (iron deficiency, thalassaemia in HK)
- Other: Obesity/deconditioning, psychogenic, neuromuscular disease

**Key Pathophysiology**:
- ***HF = cardiac output cannot meet demand → a mismatch!***
- L→R shunt → pulmonary overcirculation → ↓lung compliance → ↑work of breathing
- AS → LV pressure overload → LVH → ↑myocardial O₂ demand + ↓coronary perfusion → exertional ischaemia
- Asthma → chronic airway inflammation → bronchoconstriction + oedema + mucus → variable airflow obstruction

**Key Clinical Features**:
- Infants: ***Poor feeding, FTT, SOB on exertion, excessive sweating, recurrent LRTI***
- Older children: ***SOBOE, ↓exercise tolerance, chest pain, syncope (red flag!), palpitations, oedema***

**Key Signs**:
- Compensatory: ***tachycardia, cardiomegaly***
- Pulmonary congestion: ***tachypnoea, subcostal recession, fine crackles***
- Systemic congestion: ***hepatomegaly*** (most reliable in children), facial puffiness
- ↓CO: ***cool extremities, weak pulses, prolonged CRT***
- Respiratory: wheeze, crackles, hyperinflation, ↓air entry

**Ross Classification** = paediatric NYHA equivalent. ***Class II = dyspnoea on exertion***

**Age-appropriate normal values are essential** — always use paediatric reference ranges for HR, RR, BP, SpO₂, and CTR (≥0.6 abnormal in infants vs ≥0.5 in older children)

</Callout>

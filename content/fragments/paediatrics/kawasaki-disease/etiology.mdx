# Kawasaki Disease (KD) — Paediatrics

## Definition

**Kawasaki disease (KD)**, also historically known as ***mucocutaneous lymph node syndrome*** (川崎症), is an **acute, self-limiting systemic vasculitis** of unknown aetiology that predominantly affects **small- to medium-sized arteries** throughout the body, with a particular predilection for the **coronary arteries** [1][2].

Breaking down the terminology:
- "Mucocutaneous" = mucous membranes + cutaneous (skin) — reflects the hallmark clinical features (lips, tongue, conjunctivae, rash, extremity changes)
- "Lymph node syndrome" = cervical lymphadenopathy is one of the diagnostic criteria
- Named after **Tomisaku Kawasaki**, the Japanese paediatrician who first described the condition in 1967

The disease is **self-limiting** — it typically resolves on its own over an average of **12 days without treatment** [1]. However, the critical concern is that **untreated KD carries a ~25% risk of coronary artery aneurysms (CAA)**, which can lead to myocardial infarction, sudden death, or long-term ischaemic heart disease. This is why early recognition and treatment is paramount.

<Callout title="Key Concept">
KD is the **most common cause of acquired heart disease in children** in developed countries, having surpassed acute rheumatic fever in high-income settings [1]. In Hong Kong and East Asia, this is especially relevant given the higher incidence in Asian populations.
</Callout>

## Epidemiology

### Incidence
- ***Incidence varies with population, with increasing incidence recently*** [1]
- **Japan**: highest worldwide incidence ~330 per 100,000 children < 5 years (2018 data)
- **Hong Kong / East Asia**: ~100–150 per 100,000 children < 5 years — among the highest globally
- **USA / Europe**: ~20–25 per 100,000 children < 5 years
- The incidence has been **steadily rising** over past decades, likely due to improved recognition, but also possibly a true increase

### Demographics
- ***More common in Asians, M > F, age 6 months to 5 years (peak at 2–3 years)*** [1]
- **Male-to-female ratio**: approximately **1.5:1**
- **Age distribution**:
  - Rare < 3 months (protected by maternal antibodies) and rare > 5 years
  - Children **< 6 months** and **> 5 years** are at **higher risk of coronary complications** — partly because atypical presentations delay diagnosis
  - Peak incidence: **2–3 years of age**
- **Ethnicity**: Japanese and East Asian children have significantly higher risk, even when living in non-Asian countries — strongly implicating a **genetic susceptibility component**

### Seasonality
- ***Seasonal peak in winter/spring*** [1]
- Epidemic clusters have been described, particularly in Japan (1979, 1982, 1986)

### Recurrence
- Recurrence rate: ~3% overall (higher in Japan, ~4%)
- Siblings of affected children have a **10-fold increased risk**

| **Family history / affected sibling** | Shared genetic susceptibility |
| **Winter / spring season** | Possible infectious trigger |

## Anatomy and Function: The Coronary Arteries (Why KD Matters)

Understanding **why KD targets coronary arteries** requires understanding the anatomy:

### Coronary Artery Anatomy in Children
- **Left main coronary artery (LMCA)** → branches into:
  - **Left anterior descending (LAD)**: supplies anterior wall and septum of LV
  - **Left circumflex (LCx)**: supplies lateral and posterior LV
- **Right coronary artery (RCA)**: supplies RV and inferior LV, also the SA/AV nodes in most people
- In children, these arteries are relatively small-calibre vessels

### Why Coronary Arteries Are Vulnerable in KD
The coronary arteries are **medium-sized muscular arteries** — exactly the calibre targeted by the vasculitis of KD. The proposed reasons for their particular vulnerability include:
1. **Haemodynamic stress**: coronary arteries experience high-pressure pulsatile flow directly from the aortic root
2. **Branching points**: bifurcations and ostia are sites of turbulent flow and endothelial stress — preferential sites for aneurysm formation
3. **Limited external support**: coronary arteries run along the epicardial surface without substantial surrounding tissue to buttress the vessel wall during inflammation-induced weakening
4. **Rich vasa vasorum**: abundant microvascular supply in coronary arterial walls may facilitate inflammatory cell infiltration

### Structure of the Arterial Wall (Relevant to Aneurysm Formation)
- **Intima**: endothelial cells — initial site of immune activation
- **Media**: smooth muscle cells + elastic laminae — destruction of this layer leads to weakening and aneurysm formation
- **Adventitia**: connective tissue + vasa vasorum — inflammatory infiltrate often originates here

<Callout title="Clinical Relevance" type="idea">
Aneurysm formation in KD occurs because inflammation **destroys the internal elastic lamina and media** of the coronary artery wall. Without structural support, the wall balloons outward under arterial pressure → **aneurysm**. Think of it like a weak spot on a garden hose that bulges under water pressure.
</Callout>

## Aetiology

The aetiology of KD remains ***unclear*** [1], but it is thought to result from an **abnormal immunological response** triggered by an **environmental stimulus** (likely infectious) in a **genetically susceptible** host.

### 1. Genetic Susceptibility

***Increased risk among family members*** [1]

***Suggested genes: ITPKC, FCGR2A, BLK, CD40*** [1]

| Gene | Function | Role in KD |
|---|---|---|
| **ITPKC** (inositol-1,4,5-trisphosphate 3-kinase C) | Negative regulator of T-cell activation via the calcineurin/NFAT pathway | Functional polymorphisms → impaired negative regulation → excessive T-cell activation and cytokine release |
| **FCGR2A** (Fc gamma receptor IIA) | IgG Fc receptor on macrophages and neutrophils; involved in immune complex clearance | Polymorphisms affect ability to clear immune complexes → prolonged vascular inflammation |
| **BLK** (B-lymphocyte kinase) | Signalling in B-cell receptor pathway | Variants may alter B-cell activation threshold |
| **CD40** | Co-stimulatory molecule on antigen-presenting cells; interacts with CD40L on T-cells | Polymorphisms enhance immune activation; CD40 signalling promotes vascular inflammation |

Additional associated genes include **CASP3** (caspase 3 — apoptosis regulation) and various HLA types.

The **10-fold increased risk in siblings** and the strong **ethnic predilection** (Asian > other ethnicities, even after migration to non-Asian countries) strongly support a genetic contribution.

### 2. Infectious Trigger

***Evidence: seasonal peak in winter/spring, epidemics, peak in toddlers, similarity with infectious disease*** [1]

**Arguments for an infectious trigger:**
- **Seasonality**: winter/spring peak (like many respiratory infections)
- **Epidemics**: temporal and spatial clustering of cases
- **Age distribution**: peak in toddlers (post-waning of maternal antibodies, before full adaptive immunity develops — similar to many childhood infections)
- **Clinical similarity**: fever, rash, lymphadenopathy, mucosal changes mimic many infectious diseases
- **Self-limiting course**: like infections
- **Rarity in neonates** (protected by maternal antibodies) **and adults** (presumably previously exposed)

**Proposed infectious agents** (none definitively proven):
- Various RNA viruses (coronavirus, paramyxovirus)
- Bacterial superantigens (staphylococcal, streptococcal)
- Novel RNA virus (proposed based on inclusion body findings in ciliated bronchial epithelium)

> Despite decades of research, **no single causative organism has been identified**. The current consensus is that KD is likely triggered by a **ubiquitous infectious agent** that causes disease only in genetically susceptible individuals.

### 3. Immunological Basis (The Unifying Mechanism)

The prevailing model: **Infection → Innate and adaptive immune activation → Systemic vasculitis**

The immune cascade involves:
1. **Innate immune activation**: macrophages, neutrophils, and dendritic cells are activated early
2. **T-cell activation**: oligoclonal IgA-producing plasma cells and CD8+ T cells infiltrate arterial walls (particularly coronary arteries)
3. **Cytokine storm**: massive release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, IFN-γ) → systemic inflammation, fever, and endothelial damage
4. **Endothelial dysfunction**: activated endothelium → increased vascular permeability, oedema, inflammatory cell infiltration into vessel walls
5. **Vascular wall destruction**: elastase and matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) from neutrophils and macrophages → destruction of the **internal elastic lamina and media** → weakening → **aneurysm formation**

## Pathophysiology (Detailed, Phase-by-Phase)

### Phase 1: Acute Necrotising Arteritis (Days 0–14)
- Neutrophilic infiltration of the arterial wall
- Necrosis of the media and destruction of the internal elastic lamina
- This is the phase when **coronary artery damage begins**
- Corresponds to the **acute febrile phase** clinically

### Phase 2: Subacute / Chronic Vasculitis (Weeks 2–8)
- Transition from neutrophilic to **lymphocytic and macrophage** infiltration
- Progressive inflammation may extend the damage
- **Aneurysms** become detectable (typically from week 2 onwards)
- Thrombocytosis develops (reactive) → **thrombotic risk within aneurysmal segments**
- Corresponds to the **subacute phase** clinically (desquamation, risk of coronary events)

### Phase 3: Luminal Myofibroblast Proliferation (Months to Years)
- Myofibroblast proliferation within the intima → **progressive luminal stenosis**
- This is the mechanism by which **giant aneurysms** may develop stenotic segments over time
- Late complication: **ischaemic heart disease** in young adults due to coronary stenosis

<Callout title="The Paradox of Aneurysms and Stenosis">
In KD, aneurysmal segments can **both dilate AND later stenose**. Initial media destruction → aneurysm (dilation). Later myofibroblastic proliferation → stenosis. A patient may therefore develop angina or MI years after KD due to stenosis at the site of a previous aneurysm — even one that appeared to have "resolved" on imaging.
</Callout>

### Pathophysiological Basis of Each Clinical Feature

| Feature | Pathophysiological Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **High fever** | Massive cytokine release (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α) → hypothalamic set-point elevation |
| **Bilateral conjunctival injection** | Vasculitis / inflammation of conjunctival vessels; non-exudative because it is vasculitis, not infection |
| **Oral mucosal changes (strawberry tongue, cracked lips)** | Mucosal vasculitis → inflammation, oedema, erythema of oral mucosa; desquamation of papillae on tongue |
| **Polymorphous rash** | Cutaneous vasculitis → perivascular inflammatory infiltrate → varied skin eruptions |
| **Extremity changes (oedema → desquamation)** | Acutely: increased vascular permeability → soft tissue oedema of hands/feet. Subacutely: periungual desquamation reflects resolving dermal inflammation |
| **Cervical lymphadenopathy** | Regional immune activation; lymph node vasculitis and reactive hyperplasia |
| **Coronary artery aneurysms** | Destruction of the internal elastic lamina and muscular media → loss of structural integrity → dilation under arterial pressure |
| **Irritability** | Aseptic meningitis (CSF pleocytosis); vasculitis of meningeal vessels; possibly direct CNS cytokine effects |
| **Thrombocytosis (subacute phase)** | Reactive thrombocytosis from IL-6–driven hepatic thrombopoietin production; creates prothrombotic state |

## Classification

### A. By Clinical Criteria Fulfilment

#### 1. ***Classic (Complete) Kawasaki Disease***
- Fever ≥ 5 days **PLUS** ≥ 4 of 5 principal clinical features (see below)
- **OR** fever ≥ 5 days + < 4 features **but** coronary artery abnormalities detected on echocardiography
- Experienced clinicians may diagnose KD before day 5 of fever if classic features are present ("day 4 rule" — AHA 2017 guidelines allow diagnosis at day 4 with ≥ 4 criteria)

#### 2. ***Incomplete (Atypical) Kawasaki Disease***
- Fever ≥ 5 days + **2 or 3** principal clinical features
- Supported by laboratory findings and/or echocardiographic evidence
- **This is NOT a milder form** — it is simply a presentation with fewer diagnostic criteria. The risk of coronary aneurysm is **at least as high** (arguably higher, because diagnosis is often delayed)
- More common in **infants < 6 months** and **children > 5 years** — precisely the groups at highest risk for coronary complications

<Callout title="Common Exam Trap" type="error">
"Incomplete" KD does NOT mean "mild" or "less serious." It means the child has fewer classical features — making diagnosis harder and potentially delaying treatment. These children are at **equal or greater risk** of coronary complications. Always have a low threshold for echocardiography and IVIG in suspected incomplete KD.
</Callout>

#### 3. ***Kawasaki Disease Shock Syndrome (KDSS)***
- KD with haemodynamic instability (systolic hypotension, or > 20% drop from baseline)
- Features overlap with toxic shock syndrome and septic shock
- Higher risk of coronary artery involvement and IVIG resistance

### B. By Coronary Artery Involvement

| Classification | Z-score of Coronary Artery Internal Diameter | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **No involvement** | Z-score always < 2 | Normal |
| **Dilation only** | Z-score 2 to < 2.5 (or initial Z < 2 but decrease in Z > 1) | Mild ectasia |
| **Small aneurysm** | Z-score ≥ 2.5 to < 5 | |
| **Medium aneurysm** | Z-score ≥ 5 to < 10, or absolute dimension 4–8 mm | |
| **Giant aneurysm** | Z-score ≥ 10, or absolute dimension ≥ 8 mm | Highest risk of thrombosis, stenosis, MI |

> Z-scores are used because absolute coronary artery dimensions vary with body surface area in children. A Z-score adjusts for the child's body size.

### C. By Phase of Illness

| Phase | Timeframe | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| ***Acute phase*** | ***Week 1–2*** | ***High fever, conjunctival injection, mucosal changes, rash, extremity oedema, lymphadenopathy; peri/myo/endocarditis possible*** [1] |
| ***Subacute phase*** | ***Week 2–4*** | ***Resolution of fever, skin desquamation (especially fingers and toes), ± coronary artery aneurysms, thrombocytosis, highest risk of sudden death*** [1] |
| ***Convalescent phase*** | ***Week 6–8*** | ***Disappearance of clinical symptoms until ESR normalises*** [1] |

## Clinical Features

### Temporal Phases and Corresponding Features

#### A. ***Acute Phase (Week 1–2)*** [1]

##### Principal Diagnostic Features (the "5 criteria")

**Mnemonic: "CRASH and Burn" (Conjunctivitis, Rash, Adenopathy, Strawberry tongue, Hands/feet + Burn = Fever)**

Or use **"CREAM"**: **C**onjunctivitis, **R**ash, **E**xtremity changes, **A**denopathy, **M**ucosal changes

---

**1. FEVER (the mandatory criterion)**

- ***Sudden onset of high fever: no apparent source and unresolved by antipyretics, typically > 38.5°C*** [1]
- Typically **high-grade** (often 39–40°C), **remittent** pattern
- **Unresponsive to antibiotics** (because it is not caused by a typical bacterial infection)
- **Poorly responsive to antipyretics** (paracetamol/ibuprofen may temporarily reduce but do not resolve fever)
- Lasts an average of **11 days** untreated; with IVIG treatment, typically resolves within **36 hours**
- **Pathophysiological basis**: Massive cytokine release (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α) acts on the hypothalamus → elevation of the thermoregulatory set-point → sustained high fever. The persistent nature reflects ongoing systemic immune activation rather than a localised infection.

<Callout title="Why the Fever is Unresponsive to Antibiotics">
KD fever is driven by an **immunological cascade**, not a bacterial infection. The cytokine storm driving the fever is not suppressed by antibiotics. This is a key clinical clue: a child with prolonged high fever unresponsive to antibiotics should raise suspicion for KD (among other conditions like JIA, malignancy, etc.).
</Callout>

---

**2. ***Bilateral conjunctival erythema: non-suppurative bulbar conjunctivitis with perilimbal sparing*** [1]**

- **Bilateral** (both eyes — unilateral would suggest other causes)
- **Non-exudative / non-suppurative** (no pus or discharge — distinguishes from bacterial/viral conjunctivitis)
- Involves the **bulbar conjunctiva** predominantly (the white of the eye)
- ***Perilimbal sparing***: the zone immediately around the cornea (the limbus) is characteristically spared, creating a pale ring around the iris. This is relatively specific to KD.
- May also have **anterior uveitis** (seen on slit-lamp examination in ~80%)
- **Pathophysiological basis**: Vasculitis of conjunctival vessels → congestion and erythema. The absence of exudate differentiates it from infectious conjunctivitis — this is an **inflammatory, not infectious** process. Perilimbal sparing occurs because the limbal vasculature has different immune microenvironment (rich in regulatory T cells and immunosuppressive factors to protect the cornea).

---

**3. ***Mucosal changes: dry cracked lips, strawberry tongue*** [1]**

- **Lips**: erythema → dryness → fissuring → cracking → bleeding
- **Tongue**: diffuse erythema with prominent, hypertrophied fungiform papillae → "**strawberry tongue**" (similar to that seen in scarlet fever)
- **Oropharynx**: diffuse erythema of the oral and pharyngeal mucosa
- **No ulceration or pharyngeal exudates** (important negative — ulcers suggest other diagnoses like HSV, Stevens-Johnson syndrome; exudates suggest streptococcal pharyngitis)
- **Pathophysiological basis**: Mucosal vasculitis → inflammation and oedema of the oral mucosa → hyperaemia (redness), desiccation (cracking). Strawberry tongue occurs because inflammation causes swelling and prominence of the fungiform papillae while the overlying white coating sloughs off.

---

**4. ***Cervical lymphadenopathy: usually unilateral*** [1]**

- **Least common** of the 5 principal features (~50–75% of complete KD cases)
- Usually **unilateral** (unlike many infectious causes which are bilateral)
- Typically **≥ 1.5 cm** in diameter
- **Firm, non-fluctuant, non-suppurative** (may mimic bacterial cervical lymphadenitis — a common initial misdiagnosis)
- **Pathophysiological basis**: Reactive lymphoid hyperplasia in cervical lymph nodes draining the oropharyngeal region. Vasculitis of the lymph node's own vasculature contributes. Unilaterality may reflect asymmetric drainage patterns or localised immune activation.

---

**5. Extremity Changes**

**Acute phase:**
- ***Swelling of hands and feet*** [1] — **indurative oedema** (hard, non-pitting) with erythema of the palms and soles
- **Pathophysiological basis**: Vasculitis → increased vascular permeability → leakage of fluid and proteins into the interstitial space of the acral tissues. The induration (firmness) reflects proteinaceous oedema rather than simple transudative oedema.

**Subacute phase (week 2–3):**
- ***Skin desquamation: especially fingers and toes*** [1] — **periungual desquamation** (peeling beginning around the nail beds and spreading proximally)
- **Pathophysiological basis**: The acute vasculitis damages the dermal-epidermal junction. As inflammation resolves, the damaged superficial skin layers slough off, starting where the skin is thinnest and most tightly adherent (fingertips and toes). Analogous to the desquamation seen after scarlet fever.

---

**6. Polymorphous Rash**

- **Not mentioned as a separate criterion** in some frameworks but is one of the 5 principal features
- **Polymorphous** = can take many forms: maculopapular (most common), diffuse erythroderma, erythema multiforme–like, or scarlatiniform
- **NOT vesicular or bullous** (vesicles or bullae should prompt consideration of other diagnoses: varicella, Stevens-Johnson)
- Often accentuated in the **perineal / groin area** (early perineal desquamation is actually a useful early sign)
- **Pathophysiological basis**: Cutaneous small-vessel vasculitis → perivascular inflammatory infiltrate → diverse skin manifestations depending on depth and pattern of inflammation.

<Callout title="Important Negative Findings" type="error">
In KD, the rash is **never vesicular or bullous**. Conjunctivitis is **non-exudative**. Oral changes **do not include ulcers or tonsillar exudate**. These negatives help distinguish KD from mimics (varicella, Stevens-Johnson syndrome, adenovirus, streptococcal infection).
</Callout>

---

##### Other System Involvement (Acute Phase)

***Multi-system involvement: inflammation of small to medium sized arteries*** [1]

| System | Features | Pathophysiological Basis |
|---|---|---|
| ***CVS*** | ***Peri/myo/endocarditis, heart failure*** [1] | Pancarditis: vasculitis of coronary arteries + direct myocardial inflammation → impaired contractility → HF. Pericarditis → effusion. Valvulitis → regurgitation (especially mitral). |
| ***GI (60%)*** | ***Diarrhoea, vomiting, hepatitis, pancreatitis, hydrops of gallbladder*** [1] | GI mucosal vasculitis → abdominal pain, diarrhoea. Hepatic vasculitis → transaminitis. Gallbladder hydrops (acalculous distension) → RUQ pain; due to vasculitis of cystic artery and gallbladder wall oedema. |
| ***Respiratory (35%)*** | ***Pneumonitis*** [1] | Pulmonary vasculitis → interstitial pneumonitis. |
| ***CNS*** | ***Irritability, aseptic meningitis*** [1] | Meningeal vasculitis → CSF pleocytosis (predominantly mononuclear) without bacterial infection. Extreme irritability is a hallmark and is more marked than expected for the degree of illness — possibly due to cerebral vasculitis and raised ICP. |
| ***Urogenital*** | ***Urethritis*** [1] | Urethral mucosal inflammation → sterile pyuria (WBCs in urine without bacterial growth). This is a classic lab finding that can mislead clinicians into diagnosing UTI. |
| ***Rheumatological*** | ***Arthritis*** [1] | Synovial vasculitis → joint inflammation, particularly affecting large joints (knees, ankles) and small joints of the hands. |
| ***Other*** | ***BCG scar erythema/induration (BCGitis), HLH, SNHL*** [1] | **BCGitis**: Cross-reactivity between heat-shock proteins of mycobacteria (from BCG vaccine) and vascular endothelial antigens activated during KD → localised immune reaction at the BCG inoculation site. This is a useful early sign, particularly in **Hong Kong and Asia** where BCG vaccination is routine. **HLH**: Macrophage activation syndrome (a form of secondary HLH) can complicate KD. **SNHL**: Sensorineural hearing loss from vasculitis of the labyrinthine artery (cochlear blood supply). |

<Callout title="BCGitis as a Diagnostic Clue in Hong Kong">
In Hong Kong, where BCG vaccination is given at birth, **erythema and induration at the BCG scar** is a very useful early clinical sign of KD, especially in infants. It occurs in up to 50% of KD cases in BCG-vaccinated populations and may appear before other classic features. Always inspect the BCG scar in a febrile child being evaluated for KD [1].
</Callout>

---

#### B. ***Subacute Phase (Until Week 4)*** [1]

- ***Gradual resolution of symptoms*** [1]
- ***Skin desquamation: especially fingers and toes*** [1]
- ***± Coronary artery aneurysms*** [1]
- **Thrombocytosis**: platelet count rises (often > 1,000 × 10⁹/L), peaking around week 3
  - **Why?**: Reactive thrombocytosis driven by IL-6 → hepatic thrombopoietin production. Combined with endothelial damage in aneurysmal segments, this creates a **highly thrombogenic environment** → risk of coronary artery thrombosis.
- This is the **highest-risk period** for:
  - **Coronary artery thrombosis** (within aneurysms)
  - **Myocardial infarction**
  - **Sudden death**
- Irritability improves, fever resolves (if treated with IVIG, fever usually resolves in acute phase)

---

#### C. ***Convalescent Phase (Week 6–8)*** [1]

- ***From disappearance of clinical symptoms until ESR normalises*** [1]
- All clinical signs and symptoms have resolved
- Laboratory markers (CRP, ESR) gradually normalise
- Platelet count normalises
- Coronary artery changes may persist, improve, or progress
- **Beau's lines**: transverse grooves on fingernails and toenails, appearing 1–2 months after illness — represent temporary arrest of nail growth during the acute systemic inflammation

### Summary of Signs and Symptoms by System

| Feature | Symptoms (what the child/parent reports) | Signs (what you find on examination) | Pathophysiological Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fever** | Persistent high fever, irritable child, poor feeding | Temperature > 38.5°C (often 39–40°C), flushed | Cytokine-driven hypothalamic set-point elevation |
| **Conjunctivae** | "Red eyes," photophobia | Bilateral bulbar conjunctival injection, perilimbal sparing, no discharge | Conjunctival vasculitis |
| **Oral** | Sore mouth, difficulty eating | Strawberry tongue, cracked red lips, oropharyngeal erythema | Mucosal vasculitis |
| **Neck** | Neck lump, pain on turning | Unilateral cervical lymph node ≥ 1.5 cm, firm, non-fluctuant | Reactive lymphoid hyperplasia + nodal vasculitis |
| **Extremities** | Swollen painful hands/feet → later peeling skin | Indurative oedema with palmar/plantar erythema → periungual desquamation | Acral vasculitis → vascular leak → later epidermal sloughing |
| **Skin** | Rash (variable appearance) | Polymorphous exanthem; perineal erythema/desquamation; BCG scar erythema | Cutaneous vasculitis |
| **Cardiac** | Fatigue, breathlessness, chest pain (older child) | Tachycardia, gallop rhythm, murmur (MR), pericardial rub, signs of HF | Pancarditis; coronary vasculitis → aneurysm → ischaemia |
| **GI** | Abdominal pain, vomiting, diarrhoea | RUQ tenderness (hydrops of GB), hepatomegaly, jaundice | GI mucosal vasculitis, gallbladder vasculitis |
| **CNS** | Extreme irritability, lethargy | Neck stiffness (if meningitis), irritability disproportionate to fever | Aseptic meningitis from meningeal vasculitis |

## Laboratory Findings (Supporting Diagnosis)

While KD is a **clinical diagnosis**, laboratory findings support the diagnosis — particularly in **incomplete KD**:

### Acute Phase

| Test | Typical Finding | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| **WBC** | Leucocytosis with neutrophilia and left shift | Acute inflammatory response |
| **CRP** | Markedly elevated (often > 30 mg/L) | Acute phase reactant from hepatic synthesis (IL-6 driven) |
| **ESR** | Markedly elevated (often > 40 mm/hr) | Acute phase reactant; note: **IVIG artificially elevates ESR** (because it adds immunoglobulins that increase rouleaux formation). Post-IVIG, follow CRP instead. |
| **Albumin** | Low (< 30 g/L) | Hepatic acute phase response redirects synthesis to CRP/fibrinogen; also capillary leak |
| **ALT** | Mildly elevated | Hepatic vasculitis |
| **Anaemia** | Normocytic normochromic anaemia | Anaemia of chronic inflammation (hepcidin-mediated iron sequestration) |
| **Sterile pyuria** | WBCs in urine, negative culture | Urethral mucosal inflammation, not a true UTI |
| **CSF** | Mononuclear pleocytosis, normal glucose, normal/mildly elevated protein | Aseptic meningitis |
| **Platelets** | Normal or low in first week | May be consumed in coagulopathy; thrombocytopenia is a risk factor for coronary disease |

### Subacute Phase

| Test | Typical Finding | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| **Platelets** | Thrombocytosis (often > 700, may exceed 1,000 × 10⁹/L) | Reactive; IL-6 driven thrombopoietin production |
| **ESR** | Remains elevated (may be falsely elevated by IVIG) | Persistent inflammation |
| **CRP** | Declining (more reliable than ESR post-IVIG) | Resolution of acute inflammation |

<Callout title="ESR vs CRP After IVIG" type="error">
After IVIG administration, the **ESR will be artificially elevated** for weeks (because IVIG contains immunoglobulins that promote rouleaux formation). Use **CRP** to monitor disease activity and treatment response post-IVIG. This is a commonly tested concept.
</Callout>

---

## AHA 2017 Supplemental Laboratory Criteria for Incomplete KD

For children with **fever ≥ 5 days + 2–3 principal features**, the following laboratory criteria support the diagnosis if **≥ 3 of the following** are met:

1. **Albumin ≤ 3.0 g/dL**
2. **Anaemia for age**
3. **Elevation of ALT**
4. **Platelets after 7 days ≥ 450,000/mm³**
5. **WBC ≥ 15,000/mm³**
6. **Urine ≥ 10 WBC/HPF** (sterile pyuria)

If ≥ 3 met → proceed to **echocardiography**. If echo shows coronary abnormalities → treat as KD.

---

> **Key take-home message**: KD is a **clinical diagnosis** based on **fever + clinical criteria**. There is no single diagnostic test. The most important complications are **coronary artery aneurysms**, which are preventable with timely IVIG therapy. Always think of KD in any child (especially age 6 months to 5 years) with **prolonged unexplained fever unresponsive to antibiotics**, even if not all criteria are met — incomplete KD is common and carries equal or greater coronary risk.

<Callout title="High Yield Summary">

1. **Definition**: Acute self-limiting systemic vasculitis of unknown aetiology; targets small-to-medium arteries, especially coronary arteries
2. **Epidemiology**: Most common acquired heart disease in children (developed countries); peak age 2–3 years; M > F; highest incidence in Japanese/East Asian children; increasing incidence
3. **Aetiology**: Unknown — likely immunological response to an infectious trigger in a genetically susceptible host (ITPKC, FCGR2A, BLK, CD40)
4. **Pathophysiology**: Immune activation → cytokine storm → vasculitis → destruction of internal elastic lamina and media → coronary artery aneurysm. Three phases: necrotising arteritis → subacute inflammation → luminal myofibroblast proliferation (stenosis)
5. **Classification**: Complete (≥ 4/5 criteria + fever) vs Incomplete (2–3 criteria + supportive labs/echo) — incomplete is NOT milder
6. **Clinical Features**: **CRASH and Burn** or **CREAM** — Fever ≥ 5 days + Conjunctivitis (bilateral, non-exudative, perilimbal sparing), Rash (polymorphous, non-vesicular), Adenopathy (cervical, unilateral, ≥ 1.5cm), Strawberry tongue/cracked lips, Hands/feet (oedema → desquamation)
7. **Multi-system**: CVS (pancarditis, CAA), GI (hydrops of GB, hepatitis), CNS (irritability, aseptic meningitis), UG (sterile pyuria)
8. **BCGitis**: Useful early sign in Hong Kong/Asian children vaccinated with BCG
9. **Phases**: Acute (weeks 1–2) → Subacute (weeks 2–4, highest risk of MI/death, thrombocytosis) → Convalescent (weeks 6–8, until ESR normalises)
10. **Lab**: Elevated WBC/CRP/ESR, low albumin, sterile pyuria, thrombocytosis (subacute). After IVIG: follow CRP, not ESR (IVIG falsely elevates ESR)

</Callout>

_Sketchy memory palace for Carotid Artery Stenosis_

| No. | Visual Cue | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | An elderly man in a '65+' hat driving a car with a 'Systemic Atherosclerosis' bumper sticker, through a toll booth that is narrowing and surrounded by cigarette smoke. | - Definition: Narrowing of the carotid artery (usually at the bifurcation / proximal ICA) predominantly due to atherosclerosis. Classified as symptomatic (ipsilateral TIA/stroke within 6 months) or asymptomatic.<br />- Epidemiology: Accounts for 10–20% of ischaemic strokes. More common in males, age &gt;65, with cardiovascular risk factors. Intracranial atherosclerosis is relatively more common in Asian (including Hong Kong) populations, but extracranial carotid disease remains highly relevant.<br />- Key Risk Factors: Smoking, hypertension, diabetes mellitus, hyperlipidaemia, family history — atherosclerosis is a systemic disease. |
| 2 | A split where the 'ICA Highway' has zero exits, while the 'ECA Road' has many off-ramps. A 'Vagus Nerve' rope hangs behind the main road. A 'Circle of Willis' map is missing the 'MCA Road', and a glosso-ninth-shaped stick (CN IX) pokes a pressure sensor. | - Anatomy: CCA bifurcates at C3/4 into ICA (no extracranial branches) and ECA (multiple branches, provides collateral flow). MCA is NOT part of the Circle of Willis. Vagus nerve lies posterior to CCA (90–95%). Carotid baroreceptors at ICA origin are innervated by CN IX — manipulation causes bradycardia/hypotension. |
| 3 | Plaque-like asphalt cracking, with a piece flying forward to clog the road ahead (thromboembolism) while water flow behind it slows to a trickle (hemodynamic failure). A nearby garden labeled 'Penumbra' is wilting but salvageable with a hose. | - Pathophysiology: Atherosclerotic plaque → plaque rupture/ulceration → thrombosis → artery-to-artery thromboembolism (dominant mechanism) or haemodynamic failure (less common, very high-grade stenosis). The ischaemic penumbra is potentially salvageable with timely reperfusion. |
| 4 | A driver with a patch over his left eye (amaurosis fugax) and a limp right arm/face. He is silent (aphasia) and ignoring everything on his left (neglect). A bright 'Hollenhorst' gold coin lies on his eye patch. A sign nearby reads 'No Vertigo or Fainting Allowed'. | - Clinical Features<br />- Asymptomatic: No symptoms; found incidentally. Even complete occlusion can be silent if collaterals are adequate.<br />- Symptomatic: Amaurosis fugax (ipsilateral), contralateral hemiparesis (face/arm &gt; leg), hemisensory loss, aphasia (dominant hemisphere), neglect (non-dominant), homonymous hemianopia.<br />- Signs: Carotid bruit (poor predictor — absent in complete occlusion), Hollenhorst plaque on fundoscopy, contralateral UMN signs, cortical signs.<br />- Vertigo and syncope are NOT carotid symptoms. |
| 5 | Four buckets labeled '&lt;50%', '50-69%', '70-99%', and '100%'. Next to them are a 'String of Beads' (FMD), a 'Radiation' hazard sign (NPC), and a 'Puff of Smoke' (Moyamoya) next to an 'Asia' map. | - Classification: By symptom status (symptomatic vs asymptomatic), by NASCET grade (mild &lt; 50%, moderate 50–69%, severe 70 –99%, complete occlusion 100%), by plaque vulnerability.<br />- Three aetiological buckets for ischaemic stroke (from lecture slides): cardioembolism, critical arterial stenosis, arterial dissection. Carotid stenosis falls under "critical arterial stenosis" — but you must exclude the other two.<br />- Non-atherosclerotic causes of carotid narrowing: dissection (trauma/connective tissue disorder, Horner syndrome, retroorbital pain), FMD (young women, string of beads), vasculitis (Takayasu, GCA), radiation-induced stenosis (NPC in HK), Moyamoya (young, East Asian, puff of smoke).<br />- In Hong Kong: have a low threshold for considering intracranial atherosclerosis (more common in Asians than Caucasians), radiation-induced carotid stenosis (post-NPC treatment), and Moyamoya disease. |
| 6 | A medic checking a driver's sugar level with a giant sugar cube (glucose) and a heart-shaped monitor (ECG/Echo/Holter) checking for AF and valves. | - Top stroke mimics to exclude urgently: hypoglycaemia (check glucose first), ICH (NCCT brain), Todd's paralysis (seizure history).<br />- Cardioembolism is the most important differential: AF, valvular disease, mural thrombus. Always do ECG + echo + Holter even if carotid stenosis is found. |
| 7 | A radar gun reading 'PSV 230' and 'Ratio 4.0'. A gold trophy (DSA) sits in a dusty corner. A brain scanner showing a dark MRI-DWI shadow. A checklist includes 'CXR', 'MI check', and 'Neck Mirror' (laryngoscopy). | - Clinical classification: Symptomatic (ipsilateral TIA/stroke within 6 months) vs. asymptomatic.<br />- Imaging quantification: NASCET grade — mild ( &lt; 50%), moderate (50–69%), severe (70–99%), occlusion (100%).<br />- Acute presentation → exclude mimics (glucose, NCCT) → urgent carotid duplex → confirm with CTA/MRA → cardiac workup in parallel.<br />- Incidental finding → carotid duplex → confirm with CTA/MRA if significant → classify and risk-stratify.<br />- First-line vascular imaging: Carotid duplex USG — PSV &gt; 230 cm/s and ICA/CCA ratio &gt; 4.0 suggest ≥70% stenosis.<br />- Confirmatory imaging: CTA or MRA. Two concordant non-invasive tests preferred before surgery.<br />- Gold standard: DSA (cerebral angiography) — rarely used, reserved for discordant results or suspected non-atherosclerotic disease.<br />- Brain imaging: Urgent NCCT to exclude haemorrhage. MRI DWI more sensitive for ischaemic infarction.<br />- Cardiac workup: ECG + Holter + Echo — essential to exclude cardioembolism (especially AF).<br />- Pre-operative workup: Cardiac evaluation (MI is the leading perioperative killer), CXR, brain CT/MRI, repeat duplex to confirm ICA is not totally occluded, laryngoscopy if prior neck surgery. |
| 8 | A calendar showing '2 weeks'. A 'Best Medical' kit containing an Aspirin bottle, a 'High-Stat' statin, and an ACE card. A scale comparing a scalpel (CEA) and a spring-coil (CAS). A 'CLOSED' sign over a 100% occlusion bridge segment. | - All patients: Best medical therapy (BMT) = lifestyle modification + aspirin + statin (high-intensity, regardless of lipid levels) + antihypertensive (ACEI).<br />- Symptomatic 70–99%: BMT + revascularisation (CEA preferred in most; CAS if high surgical risk / anatomical contraindication). Aim within 2 weeks of event. Perioperative morbidity/mortality must be &lt; 6% for symptomatic patients.<br />- Symptomatic 50–69%: BMT ± CEA in selected patients (moderate benefit).<br />- Symptomatic &lt; 50% or 100% occlusion: BMT alone. Complete occlusion is an absolute contraindication to surgery.<br />- Asymptomatic ≥70%: BMT for all. CEA in selected patients (life expectancy ≥5y, surgical risk &lt; 3%).<br />- CEA vs CAS: CEA is preferred in standard cases, age ≥80, tortuous vessels; CAS is preferred if surgically inaccessible, prior radiation, re-stenosis after CEA, unfit for GA. CAS needs DAPT for 3 months; CEA needs aspirin alone.<br />- Acute stroke: CEA/CAS is NOT for acute treatment — only for secondary prevention. |
| 9 | A patient with their tongue sticking out sideways (CN XII injury), a red swelling in their neck (hematoma), a heart monitor showing a 'Heart Attack' (MI), and a 'BP 100-150' monitor. | - Key CEA complications: Stroke, hyperperfusion syndrome, MI, nerve injury (CN XII most common), cervical haematoma, restenosis (2–10% at 5 years).<br />- Stroke (plaque emboli, improper flushing, hypotension, poor cerebral protection)<br />- Hyperperfusion syndrome (loss of autoregulation → breakthrough haemorrhage/oedema; prevented by BP control 100–150 mmHg)<br />- MI (leading cause of perioperative mortality — atherosclerosis is systemic)<br />- Cranial nerve injury (CN XII most common → tongue deviation; also CN VII, IX, X, sympathetics)<br />- Cervical haematoma (can cause abrupt airway obstruction — surgical emergency)<br />- Carotid restenosis (2–10% at 5 years; early = neointimal hyperplasia, late = recurrent atherosclerosis) |
| 10 | A patient with a very slow pulse (bradycardia) being injected with Atropine. A 'Kidney' jar filled with contrast dye. A bleeding groin (access site), and a 'DAPT' pill bottle for 3-month use. | - Key CAS complications: Higher periprocedural stroke than CEA, baroreceptor-mediated bradycardia/hypotension (treat with atropine), contrast nephropathy, access-site complications, stent restenosis.<br />- Higher periprocedural stroke than CEA (but similar long-term outcomes)<br />- Baroreceptor-mediated bradycardia/hypotension (treat with atropine)<br />- Contrast-induced nephropathy<br />- Access-site complications (haematoma, pseudoaneurysm, peripheral embolisation)<br />- Stent-related (fracture, in-stent restenosis, thrombosis) |
| 11 | A calendar showing '48-72 hours' and '6 months, Annual'. A brain with a 'Sad Cloud' (29% depression), water (edema), a lightning bolt (seizure), and a 'Watershed' landscape painting. | - Post-op: BP control (100–150 mmHg). Duplex surveillance at 3–6 weeks (baseline), 6 months, then annually.<br />- TIA (warning sign — highest stroke risk in first 48–72 hours)<br />- Ischaemic stroke (territorial or watershed infarction)<br />- Post-stroke complications: cerebral oedema, herniation, seizures, haemorrhagic transformation, MI, aspiration pneumonia, DVT/PE, pressure sores, post-stroke depression (29% prevalence)<br />- Surveillance: Duplex at 3–6 weeks (baseline), 6 months, then annually for both CEA and CAS. |

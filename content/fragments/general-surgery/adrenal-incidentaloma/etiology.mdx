## Definition

An **adrenal incidentaloma** is defined as an adrenal mass **> 1 cm in diameter** that is **discovered incidentally** during radiological investigation (CT, MRI, or ultrasound) performed for reasons **unrelated** to suspected adrenal disease [1][2][3].

Let's break that down:
- **"Incidental"** — the clinician was not looking for an adrenal lesion. The patient had imaging for, say, abdominal pain, trauma staging, or cancer follow-up, and the adrenal mass was an unexpected finding.
- **> 1 cm** — this size threshold is used because smaller nodules are extremely common with advancing age and carry negligible clinical significance.

The two critical questions you must answer for **every** adrenal incidentaloma are [1][2][3]:

> **1. Is it functional?** (Is it secreting hormones autonomously?)
>
> **2. Is it malignant?** (Does it have features of primary adrenal cancer or metastasis?)

These two questions drive the entire workup and management algorithm.

## Epidemiology

### Prevalence
- **Autopsy studies**: adrenal masses are found in approximately **2–8.7%** of autopsies, with 10–15% being bilateral [3][2].
- **CT studies**: discovered in approximately **0.5–5%** of all abdominal CT scans [1]. As cross-sectional imaging becomes more frequent, the detection rate is rising — this is a condition you will see often.
- **Prevalence increases with age**: rare in patients < 30 years; increases to ~7% in patients aged > 70 years. This makes sense because most incidentalomas are benign cortical adenomas, and adenomas are age-related degenerative/hyperplastic nodules.

### Distribution of Aetiologies (Among Incidentalomas)
| Aetiology | Approximate Frequency |
|:---|:---|
| **Non-functioning cortical adenoma** | **~75–85%** (most common by far) |
| Subclinical Cushing's syndrome (autonomous cortisol secretion) | ~5–8% |
| Phaeochromocytoma | ~5–7% |
| Primary aldosteronism (Conn's syndrome) | ~1–2% (higher if hypertensive) |
| Adrenocortical carcinoma (ACC) | ~2–5% |
| Metastatic disease | ~2–5% (much higher if known primary cancer) |
| Myelolipoma | ~6–16% |
| Adrenal cyst | ~5% |
| Other (ganglioneuroma, haematoma, granuloma, haemangioma) | Rare |

[1][2][3]

<Callout title="Key Exam Point">
In a patient **with known extra-adrenal malignancy** (especially lung, breast, kidney, melanoma), an adrenal incidentaloma is a **metastasis until proven otherwise** — up to 50–75% of adrenal lesions in cancer patients are metastatic. However, even in cancer patients, ~50% of adrenal incidentalomas are still benign adenomas, so characterisation is still essential.
</Callout>

### Risk Factors for Adrenal Incidentaloma
- **Increasing age** (peak in 5th–7th decades)
- **Obesity** and **metabolic syndrome** — associated with increased prevalence of non-functioning adenomas and subclinical autonomous cortisol secretion
- **Hypertension and type 2 diabetes** — these patients get more imaging and also have higher rates of functional adenomas
- **Known malignancy** — higher detection of metastatic disease
- **Genetic syndromes** (see below under Aetiology)

## Anatomy and Function of the Adrenal Glands

Understanding adrenal anatomy is fundamental because the **location within the gland** determines the **type of tumour** and the **hormone secreted**.

### Gross Anatomy
- The adrenal (suprarenal) glands sit on the superior poles of each kidney, enclosed within Gerota's fascia but separated from the kidney by perirenal fat.
- **Right adrenal**: pyramidal/triangular, tucked between the right lobe of the liver anteriorly, the IVC medially, and the right crus of the diaphragm posteriorly. The short right adrenal vein drains directly into the IVC — this is surgically important (short, fragile, easy to tear → haemorrhage during right adrenalectomy) [4].
- **Left adrenal**: semilunar/crescent-shaped, related to the stomach, pancreatic tail, and spleen anteriorly, and the left crus of the diaphragm posteriorly. The left adrenal vein drains into the left renal vein (longer vein, easier to control surgically).

### Microanatomy: "GFR" Mnemonic for the Cortex

The adrenal gland has two embryologically distinct parts:

| Layer | Zone | Hormone(s) | Regulated By | Mnemonic |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Cortex** (mesoderm-derived) | **Zona Glomerulosa** (outermost) | **Aldosterone** (mineralocorticoid) | RAAS, K⁺ | "**G**lomerulosa = **G**o **S**alt" |
| | **Zona Fasciculata** (middle, thickest) | **Cortisol** (glucocorticoid) | ACTH (HPA axis) | "**F**asciculata = **F**uel (sugar)" |
| | **Zona Reticularis** (innermost) | **DHEA, androstenedione** (androgens) | ACTH | "**R**eticularis = **R**eally sexy" |
| **Medulla** (neural crest-derived) | Chromaffin cells | **Catecholamines** (epinephrine >> norepinephrine) | Sympathetic preganglionic fibres (ACh) | Modified post-ganglionic neurone |

> **Mnemonic: "GFR — Salt, Sugar, Sex"** — from the outside in: Glomerulosa makes aldosterone (salt), Fasciculata makes cortisol (sugar/fuel), Reticularis makes androgens (sex).

### Functional Physiology Relevant to Incidentaloma Workup

#### 1. Cortisol (Zona Fasciculata)
- **HPA axis**: Hypothalamus → CRH → Anterior pituitary → ACTH → Zona fasciculata → Cortisol → Negative feedback on hypothalamus and pituitary.
- **Circadian rhythm**: Cortisol peaks at ~6–8 AM and nadirs at ~midnight. This is why late-night salivary cortisol and the overnight dexamethasone suppression test (ONDST) exploit this physiology.
- **Dexamethasone suppression**: Dexamethasone is a synthetic glucocorticoid that suppresses ACTH release via negative feedback. In autonomous cortisol secretion (adrenal adenoma), cortisol remains unsuppressed because the adenoma produces cortisol independently of ACTH.
- **11β-HSD2**: In mineralocorticoid-responsive tissues, the enzyme 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 2 converts cortisol to cortisone (inactive). This normally prevents cortisol (which has 30–40% mineralocorticoid activity) from overwhelming mineralocorticoid receptors [5][3]. When cortisol is in massive excess (e.g. ectopic ACTH), 11β-HSD2 is overwhelmed → cortisol activates mineralocorticoid receptors → hypokalaemia, hypertension, metabolic alkalosis.

#### 2. Aldosterone (Zona Glomerulosa)
- **Regulation**: Primarily by the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) and serum K⁺. ACTH has a minor role.
- **Action**: Acts on principal cells of the collecting duct → inserts ENaC (epithelial sodium channels) and ROMK channels → Na⁺ reabsorption, K⁺ and H⁺ secretion.
- **Conn's syndrome**: Autonomous aldosterone secretion → hypertension + hypokalaemia + metabolic alkalosis. Renin is suppressed (negative feedback). Detected by plasma aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR).

#### 3. Catecholamines (Medulla)
- The adrenal medulla is essentially a modified sympathetic ganglion. Chromaffin cells synthesise catecholamines from tyrosine:
  - **Tyrosine → DOPA** (tyrosine hydroxylase, rate-limiting)
  - **DOPA → Dopamine** (DOPA decarboxylase)
  - **Dopamine → Norepinephrine** (dopamine β-hydroxylase)
  - **Norepinephrine → Epinephrine** (phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase, **PNMT** — this enzyme is **cortisol-induced**, which is why the adrenal medulla, bathed in high cortisol from the cortex via the portal system, primarily makes epinephrine) [1].
- **Catecholamine metabolism**: Catecholamines are metabolised by **COMT** (catechol-O-methyltransferase) and **MAO** (monoamine oxidase):
  - Norepinephrine → **normetanephrine** (via COMT)
  - Epinephrine → **metanephrine** (via COMT)
  - Final common metabolite: **vanillylmandelic acid (VMA)** — less sensitive, less used now.
- **Why measure metanephrines?** Phaeochromocytomas continuously metabolise catecholamines to metanephrines within the tumour via COMT. Even between symptomatic "spells," metanephrines are elevated because metabolism is ongoing. This makes fractionated metanephrines the most sensitive screening test (~96–99% sensitivity for plasma free metanephrines) [3][6].

#### 4. Adrenal Androgens (Zona Reticularis)
- DHEA and androstenedione are weak androgens. Overproduction causes **virilisation** in women (hirsutism, acne, deepening voice, clitoromegaly) and may indicate adrenocortical carcinoma, which often co-secretes cortisol + androgens.

### Adrenal Blood Supply
- **Arterial**: Multiple small arteries from 3 sources — inferior phrenic artery (superior suprarenal), aorta (middle suprarenal), and renal artery (inferior suprarenal). These form a subcapsular plexus.
- **Venous**: Single vein on each side.
  - **Right adrenal vein → IVC** (short, ~1 cm, easy to injure)
  - **Left adrenal vein → left renal vein** (longer, easier to access for adrenal venous sampling in Conn's)

## Aetiology and Pathophysiology

The differential diagnosis of an adrenal mass can be systematically divided by: (1) benign vs. malignant, (2) functional vs. non-functional, and (3) cortical vs. medullary vs. other.

### Systematic Aetiological Classification

| Category | Aetiology | Key Points |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Benign, Non-Functional** | **Cortical adenoma** (most common overall) | Lipid-rich cells, < 10 HU on unenhanced CT |
| | Myelolipoma | Contains fat and haematopoietic tissue; pathognomonic macroscopic fat on CT (negative HU values) |
| | Adrenal cyst | Simple cyst, pseudocyst, endothelial cyst |
| | Haemangioma | Rare |
| | Ganglioneuroma | Benign neural crest tumour of medulla |
| | Granuloma (TB, histoplasmosis, sarcoidosis) | **TB is important in Hong Kong/Asia** — calcified adrenals |
| | Adrenal haematoma/haemorrhage | Trauma, anticoagulation, sepsis (Waterhouse-Friderichsen), postop |
| **Benign, Functional** | **Subclinical Cushing's** (autonomous cortisol secretion) | Cortisol overproduction without classic Cushingoid phenotype; now called "mild autonomous cortisol secretion" (MACS) [European guidelines 2023] |
| | **Phaeochromocytoma** | Catecholamine-secreting; must rule out before any biopsy or surgery |
| | **Conn's syndrome** (aldosterone-producing adenoma) | Hypertension + hypokalaemia |
| | Androgen/oestrogen-secreting adenoma | Rare; think adrenocortical carcinoma if virilisation |
| **Malignant, Primary** | **Adrenocortical carcinoma (ACC)** | Usually > 4 cm; may be functional (cortisol ± androgens); aggressive |
| | Malignant phaeochromocytoma | Defined by metastasis, not histology |
| **Malignant, Secondary** | **Metastasis** (more common than primary adrenal malignancy) | Lung, breast, kidney (RCC), melanoma, lymphoma |

[1][2][3]

<Callout title="Hong Kong Context" type="idea">
In Hong Kong and endemic regions, **tuberculosis** is an important cause of bilateral adrenal involvement leading to adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease). A bilateral adrenal mass with calcification in a patient from an endemic region should raise suspicion for TB [7]. Additionally, **hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)** — common in HK due to high hepatitis B prevalence — can metastasise to the adrenal glands.
</Callout>

### Pathophysiology of Specific Aetiologies

#### A. Non-Functioning Cortical Adenoma (85%)
- Clonal proliferation of adrenocortical cells, most commonly from the zona fasciculata.
- **Lipid-rich**: The cells are filled with intracytoplasmic lipid (cholesterol esters — the precursor for steroidogenesis). This is why they appear **low attenuation (< 10 Hounsfield Units)** on unenhanced CT [2][3].
- By definition, they do not produce clinically significant hormone excess. However, they may produce small amounts of cortisol that are insufficient to cause overt Cushing's but enough to partially suppress the contralateral adrenal (this is the concept of **mild autonomous cortisol secretion / MACS**, discussed below).

#### B. Subclinical Cushing's Syndrome / Mild Autonomous Cortisol Secretion (MACS) (~5–8%)
- The adrenal adenoma autonomously secretes cortisol **independent of ACTH control**.
- The cortisol excess is **not enough** to produce the classic full-blown Cushingoid phenotype (moon face, buffalo hump, striae, proximal myopathy), but it is enough to:
  - Suppress the HPA axis (low ACTH → contralateral adrenal atrophy)
  - Contribute to metabolic comorbidities: hypertension, type 2 diabetes, central obesity, osteoporosis, dyslipidaemia
- **Pathophysiology**: The adenoma has activating mutations (e.g. in *PRKACA* — the catalytic subunit of protein kinase A, or *GNAS* — encoding Gsα) that constitutively activate the PKA signalling pathway, mimicking constant ACTH stimulation → autonomous cortisol production.
- **Clinical significance**: Patients with MACS have higher cardiovascular risk and mortality compared to patients with truly non-functioning adenomas. This is why we screen every incidentaloma for cortisol excess.
- **Detected by**: ONDST (post-dexamethasone cortisol > 50 nmol/L = possible MACS; > 138 nmol/L = definite MACS) [1][2].

#### C. Phaeochromocytoma (~5–7%)

**Phaeochromocytoma** — let's break down the name:
- "Phaeo" (Greek *phaios*) = dusky/dark
- "Chromo" (Greek *chroma*) = colour
- "Cytoma" = tumour of cells
- → Tumour of cells that turn dark when stained with chromaffin salts (potassium dichromate)

A catecholamine-secreting tumour derived from **chromaffin cells of the adrenal medulla** [1][4][6].

**Paraganglioma** = catecholamine-secreting tumour arising from chromaffin cells **outside** the adrenal gland (sympathetic or parasympathetic paraganglia) [4].

##### The "Rule of 10" — Historically Taught but Now Outdated

***The traditional "Rule of 10" is no longer considered valid*** [6][8]:

| Classic "Rule of 10" | Updated Estimates |
|:---|:---|
| 10% extra-adrenal (paraganglioma) | **10–20%** |
| 10% bilateral | **10%** (higher in familial syndromes) |
| 10% malignant | **Up to 36%** (especially extra-adrenal) |
| 10% familial | **Up to 40%** (this was grossly underestimated) |
| 10% in children | ~20% in familial forms |
| 10% recurrence | ~10–15% |
| 10% not associated with HTN | ~10% |

<Callout title="Exam Pearl" type="error">
The single biggest revision to the "Rule of 10" is the familial component — **up to 40% of phaeochromocytomas/paragangliomas have a hereditary basis**. All patients should be offered genetic counselling and testing [6][8].
</Callout>

##### Hereditary Syndromes Associated with Phaeochromocytoma [6][8][9]

| Syndrome | Gene | Associated Features |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **MEN2A** | *RET* proto-oncogene (AD) | **Medullary thyroid carcinoma**, **phaeochromocytoma**, **parathyroid hyperplasia** |
| **MEN2B** | *RET* proto-oncogene (AD) | **Medullary thyroid carcinoma**, **phaeochromocytoma**, mucosal neuromas, marfanoid habitus |
| **Von Hippel-Lindau (VHL)** | *VHL* tumour suppressor (AD) | Retinal and cerebellar **haemangioblastomas**, clear cell **RCC**, pancreatic cysts/NETs |
| **Neurofibromatosis type 1 (NF1)** | *NF1* (neurofibromin) (AD) | Café-au-lait spots, neurofibromas, optic glioma, Lisch nodules |
| **Paraganglioma syndromes (SDHx)** | *SDHB*, *SDHC*, *SDHD* (AD) | Head & neck paragangliomas; *SDHB* mutations carry highest malignancy risk (~40%) |
| **Carney triad** (sporadic, not Carney complex) | Unclear (possibly *SDHC* epigenetic changes) | GIST + pulmonary chondroma + paraganglioma |

##### Pathophysiology of Catecholamine Excess

- **Hypertension** (sustained or paroxysmal): α₁-receptor activation → peripheral vasoconstriction → ↑SVR → ↑BP. β₁ activation → ↑HR and ↑contractility → ↑CO.
  - **Why paroxysmal?** The tumour intermittently releases bursts of catecholamines, especially when mechanically compressed (change of position, palpation of abdomen, straining, anaesthesia induction).
  - **Why postural hypotension in some patients?** Chronic catecholamine excess leads to **downregulation of α-receptors** and **chronic volume contraction** (sustained vasoconstriction → pressure natriuresis). When the patient stands, the normal baroreceptor reflex is blunted → orthostatic drop [3][6].
- **Headache**: Acute hypertension → cerebral vasodilation as an autoregulatory response → stretching of pain-sensitive meningeal vessels.
- **Sweating** (diaphoresis): β₂ and cholinergic receptor activation on eccrine sweat glands + generalised sympathetic discharge.
- **Palpitations**: β₁ stimulation → tachycardia, ↑contractility, arrhythmias.
- **Pallor** (NOT flushing): α₁-mediated cutaneous vasoconstriction during spells.
- **Anxiety/tremor**: Central and peripheral sympathetic activation.
- **Hyperglycaemia/glucose intolerance**: Catecholamines promote hepatic glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis (β₂), inhibit insulin release (α₂), and promote insulin resistance.
- **Weight loss**: Hypermetabolism driven by catecholamines → ↑BMR, lipolysis.

> ***5 P's of Phaeochromocytoma: Pressure (hypertension), Pain (headache, chest pain), Palpitation, Perspiration, Pallor (vasoconstriction)*** [4]

##### Phaeochromocytoma Crisis
A life-threatening emergency: **hypertensive crisis or haemodynamic instability + hyperthermia + altered mental status + multi-organ dysfunction** [3][6]. Can be triggered by:
- Tumour manipulation (surgery, biopsy — this is why you **never biopsy** a suspected phaeochromocytoma)
- Anaesthetic agents, IV contrast, certain drugs (TCAs, metoclopramide, opioids, sympathomimetics)
- Tyramine-rich foods (cheese, wine) in the setting of concurrent MAO inhibition

#### D. Primary Aldosteronism / Conn's Syndrome (~1–2%)

**Conn's syndrome** — named after Jerome Conn (1955).

- **Aldosterone-producing adenoma (APA)**: Autonomous aldosterone secretion independent of RAAS.
- **Pathophysiology**: Excess aldosterone → ↑ENaC activity in principal cells → Na⁺ retention (→ volume expansion → hypertension) + K⁺ wasting (→ hypokalaemia) + H⁺ wasting (→ metabolic alkalosis).
- **Why does the patient not become oedematous?** "Aldosterone escape" — after initial Na⁺ retention and volume expansion, ANP is released and GFR increases → pressure natriuresis → Na⁺ balance is re-established. So you get hypertension but NOT peripheral oedema. K⁺ wasting, however, continues relentlessly.
- **Screening**: Plasma aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR). In Conn's: aldosterone ↑, renin ↓ (suppressed by volume expansion) → ARR markedly elevated [1][2].

#### E. Adrenocortical Carcinoma (ACC) (~2–5%)

- Rare but aggressive malignancy (incidence ~1–2 per million/year).
- **Bimodal age distribution**: < 10 years (childhood ACC, often associated with *TP53* germline mutations — Li-Fraumeni syndrome) and 40–60 years.
- **~60% are functional**: Most commonly secrete cortisol ± androgens simultaneously → Cushing's syndrome + virilisation (in women). Pure androgen or oestrogen secretion is rare but highly suspicious for ACC.
- **Pathophysiology**: Mutations in *TP53*, *CTNNB1* (β-catenin), *IGF2* overexpression (paternal isodisomy at 11p15), alterations in Wnt/β-catenin pathway.
- **Key features suggesting ACC** [2][3]:
  - Size **> 4 cm** (90% of adrenal malignancies are > 4 cm)
  - Irregular margins, heterogeneous enhancement
  - High CT attenuation (> 10 HU on unenhanced CT) — lipid-poor
  - Slow contrast washout (< 50% absolute washout at 15 minutes)
  - Local invasion (IVC, renal vein, surrounding structures)
  - Mixed hormone secretion (cortisol + androgens)

#### F. Adrenal Metastases

- The adrenal glands are a **common site for metastatic disease** due to their rich sinusoidal blood supply.
- **Common primary sites**: Lung (most common), breast, kidney (RCC), melanoma, lymphoma/leukaemia, GI cancers, HCC [10].
- **In Hong Kong**, given the high prevalence of lung cancer and HCC (hepatitis B-related), these are particularly important considerations.
- **Bilateral adrenal metastases** may cause **adrenal insufficiency** if > 90% of both glands are destroyed.
- **Imaging**: Lipid-poor (> 10 HU), irregular, heterogeneous. Often bilateral.
- **Biopsy**: This is the **one indication** where adrenal biopsy is useful — to confirm metastatic disease and avoid unnecessary adrenalectomy. **Must exclude phaeochromocytoma biochemically first** [2][3].

<Callout title="Critical Rule" type="error">
***Biopsy of an adrenal mass is rarely indicated. It is NOT useful for differentiating benign from malignant primary adrenal tumours (histology looks similar). It is only used to confirm metastatic disease. ALWAYS exclude phaeochromocytoma biochemically before any adrenal biopsy — biopsy of an unsuspected phaeochromocytoma can precipitate a fatal hypertensive crisis.*** [2][3]
</Callout>

#### G. Myelolipoma
- Benign tumour composed of mature adipose tissue and haematopoietic elements (resembling bone marrow).
- **Pathognomonic on imaging**: Macroscopic fat (negative HU values on CT, signal loss on fat-suppressed MRI).
- Non-functional. Usually managed conservatively unless very large or symptomatic (haemorrhage).

#### H. Other
- **Adrenal cysts**: Simple (endothelial, epithelial) or pseudocysts. Thin-walled, homogeneous, non-enhancing on CT.
- **Adrenal haemorrhage**: Can occur in neonates (birth trauma), anticoagulated patients, sepsis (Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome in meningococcal septicaemia), or post-surgical.
- **Granulomatous disease**: TB (common in HK), histoplasmosis, sarcoidosis → bilateral adrenal enlargement ± calcification → may lead to Addison's disease.

## Classification

### By Functionality

| Functional Status | Examples | Proportion |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Non-functional** (~85–90%) | Cortical adenoma, myelolipoma, cyst, ganglioneuroma, haematoma | Most common |
| **Functional** (~10–15%) | Subclinical Cushing's/MACS (~5–8%), phaeochromocytoma (~5–7%), Conn's (~1–2%), androgen-secreting (~rare) | Must screen all |

### By Benign vs. Malignant

| Benign | Malignant |
|:---|:---|
| Non-functioning adenoma | **Adrenocortical carcinoma** |
| Functioning adenoma | Malignant phaeochromocytoma |
| Myelolipoma, cyst, ganglioneuroma | **Metastasis** (lung, breast, RCC, melanoma, lymphoma, HCC) |
| Haematoma, granuloma | |

### By CT Characteristics (Imaging-Based Classification)

This is arguably the most clinically useful classification:

| Feature | Likely Benign Adenoma | Likely Malignant (ACC or Metastasis) |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Size** | < 4 cm | **> 4 cm** |
| **Unenhanced CT attenuation** | **≤ 10 HU** (lipid-rich) | **> 10 HU** (lipid-poor) |
| **Margins** | Smooth, well-defined, homogeneous | Irregular, heterogeneous, necrosis/calcification |
| **Contrast washout (15-min delayed)** | **Absolute washout > 60%** or relative washout > 40% | Absolute washout < 60% |
| **Growth rate** | Stable on serial imaging | **Growing > 0.5–1 cm over 6–12 months** |

[2][3]

<Callout title="CT Attenuation Threshold — Why 10 HU?">
Benign cortical adenomas are rich in intracytoplasmic lipid (cholesterol esters used for steroidogenesis). Fat attenuates X-rays less than water → lower Hounsfield Units. A threshold of ≤ 10 HU on unenhanced CT has a **specificity of ~98%** for benign adenoma. If the lesion is > 10 HU, it is "lipid-poor" — which can be a lipid-poor adenoma (about 30% of adenomas), but also ACC, phaeochromocytoma, or metastasis → further characterisation with contrast washout CT or chemical-shift MRI is needed.
</Callout>

## Clinical Features

### A. Symptoms

The majority of adrenal incidentalomas are **asymptomatic** — that is the very definition of "incidental." However, a thorough history may uncover subtle symptoms of hormone excess or features suggestive of malignancy.

#### 1. Symptoms of Cortisol Excess (Subclinical Cushing's / MACS)

Most patients with MACS do **not** have classic Cushingoid symptoms. Instead, look for:

| Symptom | Pathophysiological Basis |
|:---|:---|
| **Weight gain** (especially central/truncal) | Cortisol promotes visceral adipogenesis (redistribution of fat to central compartment) via activation of lipoprotein lipase in visceral fat and insulin resistance |
| **Easy bruising** | Cortisol causes catabolism of dermal collagen and subcutaneous tissue → thin, fragile skin and capillaries |
| **Fatigue and weakness** | Cortisol excess causes protein catabolism in skeletal muscle (proximal myopathy) and disrupts normal diurnal cortisol rhythm |
| **Mood disturbance** (depression, irritability, insomnia) | Cortisol excess disrupts serotonergic and GABAergic neurotransmission; also disrupts sleep architecture via loss of normal cortisol nadir at night |
| **Polyuria and polydipsia** | Hyperglycaemia from cortisol-induced gluconeogenesis and insulin resistance → osmotic diuresis |
| **Recurrent infections** | Cortisol is immunosuppressive (↓T-cell function, ↓neutrophil margination, ↓cytokine production) |
| **Menstrual irregularities** | Cortisol excess suppresses GnRH pulsatility → hypogonadotropic hypogonadism |
| **Poor wound healing** | Cortisol inhibits fibroblast proliferation and collagen synthesis |

#### 2. Symptoms of Catecholamine Excess (Phaeochromocytoma)

***The classic triad: episodic headache + sweating + palpitations*** [1][4][6]

| Symptom | Pathophysiological Basis |
|:---|:---|
| ***Episodic headache*** (80%) | Paroxysmal hypertension → cerebral autoregulatory vasodilation → meningeal vessel stretching |
| ***Sweating*** (diaphoresis, 60%) | Generalised sympathetic activation; β₂ and muscarinic receptor activation on sweat glands |
| ***Palpitations*** (70%) | β₁ stimulation → ↑HR, ↑contractility, arrhythmias |
| **Anxiety / panic-attack symptoms** | Catecholamine-mediated CNS stimulation; especially prominent with epinephrine-secreting tumours |
| **Tremor** | β₂ activation in skeletal muscle |
| **Weight loss** | Catecholamine-driven ↑BMR, lipolysis, thermogenesis |
| **Chest pain / dyspnoea** | Catecholamine-induced coronary vasospasm, myocardial oxygen demand exceeding supply, or catecholamine cardiomyopathy |
| **Constipation / abdominal pain** | α-receptor mediated inhibition of GI motility |
| **Pallor during attacks** (NOT flushing) | α₁-mediated cutaneous vasoconstriction |
| **Flushing BETWEEN attacks** | As catecholamine levels drop, compensatory vasodilation may occur |

<Callout title="Pressor Response">
A ***pressor response*** (sudden severe hypertension) may occur during procedures (anaesthesia induction, surgery, endoscopy), after administration of certain drugs (TCAs, metoclopramide, IV contrast, β-blockers given without prior α-blockade), or after eating tyramine-rich foods. This is a clue to an undiagnosed phaeochromocytoma [4][6].
</Callout>

#### 3. Symptoms of Aldosterone Excess (Conn's Syndrome)

| Symptom | Pathophysiological Basis |
|:---|:---|
| **Headache** | Hypertension |
| **Muscle cramps / weakness** | Hypokalaemia → hyperpolarisation of muscle cell membranes → impaired depolarisation |
| **Polyuria / nocturia** | Hypokalaemia → nephrogenic DI (impaired aquaporin-2 insertion in collecting ducts) |
| **Paraesthesia** | Hypokalaemia-related nerve dysfunction |
| Often **asymptomatic** — discovered on screening | Many patients have resistant hypertension without obvious symptoms |

#### 4. Symptoms of Androgen/Oestrogen Excess (Rare — Think ACC)

| Symptom | Pathophysiological Basis |
|:---|:---|
| **Virilisation in women**: hirsutism, acne, deepening voice, clitoromegaly, male-pattern baldness | Excess adrenal androgens (DHEA-S, androstenedione, testosterone) → peripheral conversion → androgenic effects |
| **Feminisation in men**: gynaecomastia, testicular atrophy, decreased libido | Excess adrenal oestrogens or aromatisation of androgens to oestrogens |
| **Rapid onset** of virilisation/feminisation strongly suggests ACC | Slow-onset virilisation more likely PCOS or CAH |

#### 5. Symptoms Suggestive of Malignancy

| Symptom | Significance |
|:---|:---|
| **Abdominal/flank pain or fullness** | Large tumour (ACC typically > 4 cm, often > 6 cm) causing local compression |
| **Constitutional symptoms**: weight loss, anorexia, fatigue | Malignancy (ACC or metastasis) |
| **Symptoms from known primary cancer** | Lung (cough, haemoptysis), breast (mass), kidney (haematuria), melanoma (skin lesion) |

#### 6. History to Elicit (Structured Approach)

***When you see an adrenal incidentaloma, take a targeted history*** [1][2]:
- **History of hypertension**: onset, severity, resistance to treatment, paroxysmal vs sustained
- **History of diabetes**: new-onset, difficulty controlling
- **Symptoms of Cushing's syndrome**: weight gain, striae, easy bruising, mood changes
- ***Triad of phaeochromocytoma: episodic headache, sweating, palpitations*** [1]
- **Symptoms of Conn's**: muscle cramps, weakness, polyuria
- **Symptoms of virilisation/feminisation**
- **Cancer history**: prior malignancy, current symptoms
- ***Family history / history of endocrine tumours (MEN)*** [1]: medullary thyroid cancer, hyperparathyroidism, pituitary tumours, phaeochromocytoma, renal cancers
- **Drug history**: exogenous steroids (can cause bilateral adrenal hyperplasia then atrophy), OCP
- **Pressor response during previous procedures**

### B. Signs

#### 1. Signs of Cortisol Excess (Cushing's Syndrome/MACS)

| Sign | Pathophysiological Basis |
|:---|:---|
| ***Moon face*** (plethora) | Cortisol → redistribution of fat to face; plethora from polycythaemia (cortisol stimulates erythropoiesis) and skin thinning revealing vasculature |
| ***Buffalo hump*** (dorsocervical fat pad) | Cortisol-induced selective visceral/central lipogenesis |
| ***Central obesity*** | Cortisol-induced insulin resistance → hyperinsulinaemia → visceral adipogenesis |
| ***Proximal muscle wasting*** | Cortisol-induced protein catabolism preferentially affects type II (fast-twitch) proximal muscle fibres |
| ***Striae*** (wide, violaceous/purple, > 1 cm wide) | Cortisol breaks down dermal collagen → skin thins → underlying vasculature visible through stretched, atrophied skin. Red/purple because the skin is so thin the blood shows through. *Unlike pregnancy striae which are white/silvery and < 1 cm.* |
| ***Easy bruising*** | Capillary fragility from collagen loss in vessel walls and perivascular tissue |
| ***Hirsutism*** (in women) | Adrenal androgen co-secretion (DHEA-S) OR cortisol acting on hair follicles |
| **Thin skin** | Cortisol-induced dermal collagen catabolism |
| **Hypertension** | Cortisol: (a) mineralocorticoid effect (overwhelms 11β-HSD2 at high levels), (b) sensitises vasculature to catecholamines, (c) ↑angiotensinogen synthesis |
| **Hyperglycaemia** (on H'stix) | Cortisol → hepatic gluconeogenesis + peripheral insulin resistance |
| **Interscapular fat pad** | Same mechanism as buffalo hump — redistribution |
| **Supraclavicular fullness** | Fat deposition |
| **Dorsal kyphosis** | Vertebral compression fractures from osteoporosis (cortisol inhibits osteoblasts, promotes osteoclast activity) |

[1][5][3]

#### 2. Signs of Catecholamine Excess (Phaeochromocytoma)

| Sign | Pathophysiological Basis |
|:---|:---|
| **Hypertension** (sustained or paroxysmal) | α₁ vasoconstriction + β₁ ↑CO |
| **Tachycardia** | β₁ stimulation of SA node |
| **Orthostatic hypotension** (despite hypertensive during attacks) | Chronic volume contraction + α-receptor downregulation |
| **Pallor** | α₁ cutaneous vasoconstriction |
| **Tremor** | β₂ activation |
| **Diaphoresis** | Sympathetic activation |
| **Dilated pupils** | Sympathetic stimulation of dilator pupillae |
| **Hypertensive retinopathy** | Chronic HTN damage to retinal vasculature |
| ***Neurofibromatosis skin stigmata*** (café-au-lait spots, neurofibromas) | NF1-associated phaeochromocytoma [6] |
| **Thyroid mass** | If associated with MEN2 (medullary thyroid carcinoma) |

[4][6]

#### 3. Signs of Aldosterone Excess (Conn's Syndrome)

| Sign | Pathophysiological Basis |
|:---|:---|
| **Hypertension** (often resistant to treatment) | Na⁺ retention → volume expansion → ↑BP |
| **No peripheral oedema** (despite Na⁺ retention) | "Aldosterone escape" — ANP release and pressure natriuresis re-establish Na⁺ balance |
| **Muscle weakness** (especially lower limbs) | Hypokalaemia → impaired membrane depolarisation |
| **Hyporeflexia** | Hypokalaemia |
| **Arrhythmias** (e.g. AF) | Hypokalaemia → prolonged repolarisation → ↑risk of arrhythmias |

[2][6]

#### 4. Signs Suggesting Malignancy

| Sign | Significance |
|:---|:---|
| **Palpable abdominal/flank mass** | Very large adrenal tumour (ACC can grow > 10 cm) |
| ***Thyroid mass*** | If MEN2 (medullary thyroid carcinoma) [1] |
| **Lymphadenopathy** | Metastatic disease |
| **Hepatomegaly** | Metastatic disease |
| **Cachexia** | Advanced malignancy |
| **Skin findings**: neurofibromas, café-au-lait spots | NF1 |
| **Skin findings**: ash-leaf macules (not adrenal-related, but tuberous sclerosis can have renal angiomyolipomas — don't confuse with adrenal) | — |

#### 5. Physical Examination — Structured Approach

***Key components of the physical examination for adrenal incidentaloma*** [1][2]:
1. **Blood pressure** — both arms, supine and standing (to detect orthostatic hypotension in phaeochromocytoma)
2. **Bedside glucose** (H'stix) — hyperglycaemia in Cushing's or phaeochromocytoma
3. **BMI and body habitus** — central obesity, Cushingoid features
4. **Cushingoid features**: Moon face, buffalo hump, striae, thin skin, easy bruising, proximal myopathy, hirsutism
5. ***Abdominal examination***: palpable mass (large ACC), hepatomegaly (metastasis)
6. ***Thyroid examination***: goitre/nodule (MEN2 → medullary thyroid carcinoma)
7. **Skin**: neurofibromas, café-au-lait spots (NF1), skin lesions suggestive of melanoma
8. **Eye examination**: proptosis (thyroid eye disease if associated), hypertensive retinopathy
9. **Cardiovascular**: signs of LVH, heart failure
10. **Neurological**: proximal myopathy (Cushing's), peripheral neuropathy

## Investigations — Screening Overview (Detailed Workup in Part 2)

***For every adrenal incidentaloma > 1 cm, perform the following baseline screening investigations*** [1][2][3]:

| Condition Screened | Screening Test | Threshold for Positive |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Cushing's syndrome / MACS** | ***1 mg overnight dexamethasone suppression test (ONDST)*** | Post-dex cortisol > 50 nmol/L (1.8 μg/dL) |
| **Phaeochromocytoma** | ***24-hour urine fractionated metanephrines*** (or plasma free metanephrines) | > 2× ULN highly suspicious |
| **Primary aldosteronism** (if hypertensive or hypokalaemic) | ***Plasma aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR)*** | Elevated ratio with elevated aldosterone |
| **Androgen excess** (if virilised female or suspected ACC) | DHEA-S, testosterone, androstenedione | Elevated |
| **Malignancy assessment** | Unenhanced CT attenuation, size, contrast washout | > 10 HU, > 4 cm, < 60% washout |

> ***Standard screening panel: ONDST + 24-hour urine metanephrines + spot ARR (if hypertensive)*** [1][2]

<Callout title="Why Screen Every Incidentaloma?">
Even if the patient is completely asymptomatic, up to 15% of incidentalomas are functional. Missing a phaeochromocytoma before surgery is catastrophic (hypertensive crisis, death). Missing subclinical Cushing's means missing a treatable cause of metabolic syndrome, osteoporosis, and increased cardiovascular mortality. Missing Conn's means missing a curable cause of hypertension.
</Callout>

---

<Callout title="High Yield Summary">

**Definition**: Adrenal mass > 1 cm found incidentally on imaging not performed for suspected adrenal disease.

**Two key questions**: (1) Is it functional? (2) Is it malignant?

**Most common cause**: Non-functioning cortical adenoma (~85%).

**Functional causes**: Subclinical Cushing's/MACS (~5–8%), phaeochromocytoma (~5–7%), Conn's syndrome (~1–2%).

**Malignant causes**: Adrenocortical carcinoma (size > 4 cm, lipid-poor, slow washout), metastasis (lung, breast, RCC, melanoma — more common than primary malignancy in cancer patients).

**Standard screening**: ONDST (cortisol excess) + 24h urine fractionated metanephrines (phaeochromocytoma) + ARR if hypertensive (Conn's).

**Imaging red flags for malignancy**: > 4 cm, unenhanced CT > 10 HU (lipid-poor), irregular/heterogeneous, contrast retention (< 60% absolute washout), growth > 1 cm on follow-up.

**Never biopsy an adrenal mass without excluding phaeochromocytoma** — risk of fatal hypertensive crisis. Biopsy only used to confirm metastatic disease.

**Phaeochromocytoma**: "Rule of 10" is outdated — up to 40% familial (MEN2, VHL, NF1, SDHx). Classic triad: headache + sweating + palpitations. 5 P's: Pressure, Pain, Palpitation, Perspiration, Pallor.

**Hong Kong relevance**: TB (bilateral adrenal calcification → Addison's), HCC metastases, lung cancer metastases.

**CT threshold**: ≤ 10 HU on unenhanced CT = lipid-rich adenoma (98% specific for benign).

**Surgical indications**: Functioning tumour, radiologically suspicious, or > 4 cm.
</Callout>

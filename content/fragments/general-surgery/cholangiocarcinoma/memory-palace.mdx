_Sketchy memory palace for Cholangiocarcinoma_

| No. | Visual Cue | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Ace-of-Spades card and 'Excluded' sign on the gallbladder shed. | - Definition: Cancer arising from bile duct epithelium; &gt; 90% adenocarcinoma. Excludes gallbladder and ampullary carcinoma. |
| 2 | Dark, silent vats without yellow alarm lights. | - Intrahepatic (10%): behaves as liver mass, NO early jaundice<br />- Intrahepatic CCA: NO jaundice until late |
| 3 | Strobe lights at a pipe fork and a flat balloon (Negative Courvoisier). | - Perihilar (60%, MC): Klatskin tumour if at bifurcation, EARLY jaundice, Courvoisier NEGATIVE<br />- CCA at hilum (Klatskin tumour) |
| 4 | Swollen green balloon tied to the lower pipe. | - Distal extrahepatic (20–30%): behaves like periampullary tumour, Courvoisier POSITIVE |
| 5 | A ladder with 5 distinct marked rungs near the Y-fork. | - Bismuth-Corlette (perihilar): I (below confluence) → II (at confluence) → IIIa (+ RHD) → IIIb (+ LHD) → IV (both/multicentric) |
| 6 | Raw fish, stones, HBV logo, and a scarred pipe section. | - Key risk factors (HK): RPC, hepatolithiasis, Clonorchis sinensis (raw freshwater fish), HBV, choledochal cysts, obesity/DM. In West: PSC + UC. |
| 7 | Sparks turning into rust with roots invading nearby wires. | - Pathophysiology: Chronic inflammation → cholestasis → DNA damage → metaplasia → dysplasia → adenocarcinoma. Perineural invasion is hallmark.<br />- Metastasis: perineural invasion (hallmark), peritoneal seeding, haematogenous to lung/bone/brain |
| 8 | Concrete, lumps, and leafy fronds next to keys 7 and 19. | - Histology: Sclerosing (desmoplastic, MC), Nodular (annular), Papillary (bulky, best prognosis). CK7+/CK19+ on IHC.<br />- Biopsy: FNAC/Trucut ONLY for unresectable cases (risk of tumour seeding). CK7+/CK19+ on IHC. |
| 9 | Yellow worker scratching himself with dark oil and white clay. | - Painless progressive jaundice (extrahepatic CCA)<br />- Dark urine + pale stools (obstructive)<br />- Pruritus (bile salt deposition in skin) |
| 10 | Worker holding his right side, shivering, and looking emaciated. | - RUQ pain, hepatomegaly (capsular stretch)<br />- Weight loss, fever (cholangitis) |
| 11 | Old man foreman pointing at a silent, growing pipe blockage. | - Determine if presentation is obstructive jaundice (extrahepatic CCA) or liver mass (intrahepatic CCA)<br />- For obstructive jaundice: differentiate stone vs tumour (intermittent/painful/fever vs progressive/painless/constitutional)<br />- Key rule: Painless progressive obstructive jaundice in elderly = malignant biliary obstruction until proven otherwise. |
| 12 | 19-9 and CEA vials; a 'Normal' sticker on an AFP vial. | - Tumour markers: CEA, CA 19-9 (non-specific, may be normal)<br />- For liver mass: differentiate HCC vs iCCA vs metastasis (AFP, imaging characteristics, IHC)<br />- Tumour markers: CA 19-9 (sensitivity ~70%, NOT specific, requires Lewis antigen), CEA (not sensitive)<br />- AFP: to differentiate from HCC (normal in CCA) |
| 13 | Drawing of a pancreas head and a gallbladder clamp on a chalkboard. | - CA head of pancreas<br />- Periampullary carcinoma (ampulla, duodenum, distal CBD)<br />- CA gallbladder (cystic duct LN, direct infiltration, tumour fragments)<br />- HCC (compression from Seg 4/5, direct infiltration, tumour fragments in CBD)<br />- Porta lymphadenopathy (CRC, lymphoma, gastric cancer) |
| 14 | Steroid bottle and a string of beads (beaded MRCP appearance). | - IgG4-related sclerosing cholangitis (check IgG4, responds to steroids)<br />- PSC with dominant stricture (beaded appearance on MRCP)<br />- RPC (intrahepatic pigment stones, left lobe predilection)<br />- IgG4: to exclude IgG4-related sclerosing cholangitis (critical mimic) |
| 15 | An Ultrasound wand next to a scanner showing peripheral rim enhancement. | - No single diagnostic criterion for CCA — diagnosis is based on combination of clinical, biochemical, radiological, and histological findings.<br />- Investigation sequence: Bloods → USG (1st line) → CT triphasic / MRI-MRCP → Cholangiography (ERCP for distal, PTC for proximal) → Tissue diagnosis ONLY if unresectable.<br />- LFT: cholestatic pattern (ALP/GGT &gt;&gt; AST/ALT), conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia<br />- Clotting: prolonged PT/INR from Vitamin K malabsorption<br />- USG findings: Ductal dilatation without stones = malignant until proven otherwise. Level of dilatation localises the obstruction.<br />- CT/MRI: Triphasic CT for staging + resectability. MRI/MRCP superior for ductal anatomy and nodal disease. iCCA shows peripheral rim enhancement with delayed centripetal fill-in (opposite of HCC washout).<br />- Cholangiography: ERCP for distal CCA (diagnostic + therapeutic). PTC for proximal/perihilar CCA. MRCP has largely replaced both for pure diagnostic purposes. |
| 16 | Blueprints where only 20% are approved for fixing; a fire extinguisher. | - Resection: Only curative treatment; resectability rate ~20%. FNAC/biopsy ONLY for unresectable cases.<br />- Resectability rate: Only ~20%. Unresectable if: major vessel invasion, bilateral 2° radicle involvement, distant LN or organ metastasis.<br />- Three management steps: (1) Treat sepsis first → (2) Assess resectability → (3) Operate if fit and resectable, palliate if not.<br />- Resectability criteria: No distant mets, no SMA/celiac involvement, patent SMV-PV confluence. PV involvement is NOT absolute contraindication.<br />- Only ~20% resectable at presentation. R0 resection is the strongest prognostic factor. |
| 17 | Internal metal pipe sleeve and an external bag with a '50' gauge. | - Pre-operative drainage: ERCP stenting 1st line. Target bilirubin &lt; 50 µmol/L (or &lt; 20 for concomitant hepatectomy). QMH practice: drain ALL patients (surgery wait 6–8 weeks).<br />- Palliation: ERCP stenting (preferred) or PTBD or surgical bypass. Metal stents for confirmed inoperable disease. Pain: celiac plexus block.<br />- Stenting: occlusion (tumour ingrowth/sludge), migration, cholangitis, post-ERCP pancreatitis<br />- PTBD: bleeding (portal triad puncture), haemobilia, fluid/electrolyte loss<br />- Pre-op biliary drainage itself increases complications (pancreatitis 7%, cholangitis 26%, blocked stent 15%) but is necessary when surgery is delayed. |
| 18 | Three distinct surgical repair kits for top, middle, and bottom sections. | - iCCA → Hepatectomy ± portal LN dissection<br />- pCCA → Bile duct resection + hepatic lobectomy + caudate lobectomy + LN dissection + Roux-en-Y HJ<br />- dCCA → Whipple procedure |
| 19 | 6-month calendar next to a 'TOPAZ' brand liquid bottle. | - Adjuvant: Capecitabine × 6 months (BILCAP trial)<br />- Advanced disease (2025 standard): Gemcitabine + Cisplatin + Durvalumab (TOPAZ-1). Molecular profiling for IDH1/FGFR2/HER2/BRAF/MSI-H. |
| 20 | Three drains marked with symbols for infection, liver rot, and a skeleton. | - Three causes of mortality in MBO (must know): Biliary sepsis, Liver failure, Cancer cachexia. |
| 21 | Leaking toxins and crumbling support pillars (representing bones). | - Impaired protein synthesis, clotting factor synthesis, gluconeogenesis, ketogenesis<br />- Endotoxaemia, decreased reticuloendothelial function, decreased cell-mediated immunity<br />- Vitamin K deficiency → coagulopathy; Vitamin D deficiency → osteomalacia |
| 22 | Algae (bacteria), rot, bulging pipes, and red blood in the water. | - Ascending cholangitis (recurrent; biliary pressure &gt; 25 cmH₂O → bacteraemia)<br />- Progressive liver failure (bile acid toxicity to hepatocytes)<br />- Portal hypertension (PV invasion or secondary biliary cirrhosis)<br />- Haemobilia (tumour erosion into hepatic artery/PV) |
| 23 | Ghost icon over liver/pancreas and a narrow, leaking HJ joint. | - Post-hepatectomy: liver failure (THE feared complication), bile leak, haemorrhage<br />- Post-Whipple: pancreatic fistula (most feared), delayed gastric emptying, exocrine/endocrine insufficiency<br />- HJ stricture: most common long-term complication → recurrent cholangitis/jaundice |

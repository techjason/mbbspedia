# CA Lung (Lung Cancer / Bronchial Carcinoma)

---

## 1. Definition

Lung cancer (CA lung) refers to **malignant neoplasms arising from the epithelium of the respiratory tract** — most commonly from the bronchial epithelium or bronchial mucous glands, hence the classical term **bronchial carcinoma**. It is broadly divided into two major categories based on histology and clinical behaviour:

- **Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC)** (~85%) — includes adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma
- **Small cell lung cancer (SCLC)** (~15%) — a neuroendocrine tumour with aggressive biology

The distinction matters enormously because SCLC and NSCLC have fundamentally different staging systems, treatment paradigms, and prognoses. The name "bronchial carcinoma" distinguishes these primary epithelial cancers from other rarer primary lung tumours (lymphoma, sarcoma, carcinoid) and from metastatic deposits to the lung from other organs (breast, GI, renal, ovarian cancers, etc.) [1][2].

---

## 2. Epidemiology

### Global Perspective

- Lung cancer is the **leading cause of cancer deaths worldwide** — it kills more people than breast, colorectal, and prostate cancers combined [1].
- Incidence is strongly linked to tobacco use, with a lag of ~20–30 years between peak smoking prevalence in a population and peak lung cancer incidence.

### Hong Kong Perspective

This is critical for HKUMed exams:

- ***2nd most common cancer overall in HK*** (2nd in males, 3rd in females) [1]
- ***1st in cancer mortality*** in both sexes — meaning it is the biggest cancer killer in HK [1]
- **Demographics**: M:F ≈ 2:1 (contrast with Western countries where ratio is 5–6:1; the gap is narrowing) [1]
- **Peak incidence**: 60–70 years
- **Unique HK/Asian pattern**:
  - In males: ~80% are smokers — similar to the West [1]
  - ***In females: < 40% are smokers, yet > 75% have adenocarcinoma*** [1] — this is a distinctive East Asian pattern. Postulated causes include passive smoking, cooking fume exposure (wok cooking with oil at high temperatures generating polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons), and a high prevalence of **EGFR mutations** in Asian non-smoking adenocarcinoma patients.
- At presentation: **60–75% already have hilar lymph node involvement** [1]
- **85% are NSCLC** [1]

<Callout title="HK-Specific Exam Point" type="idea">
The female lung cancer pattern in HK is a favourite exam topic: young, non-smoking female with peripheral adenocarcinoma harbouring an EGFR mutation. Know this clinical archetype.
</Callout>

## 3. Risk Factors

### Environmental

| Risk Factor | Mechanism / Notes |
|---|---|
| **Tobacco smoking** | Responsible for ~90% of lung cancer. Contains >60 known carcinogens (benzopyrene, nitrosamines, etc.) that cause DNA adducts and mutations (TP53, KRAS). **40× increased death rate** in heavy smokers vs never-smokers [1][2]. Risk falls after cessation but never returns to baseline — it continues to rise with age faster than in never-smokers [1]. |
| **Passive (second-hand) smoking** | 20–30% increased risk. Sidestream smoke contains higher concentrations of some carcinogens than mainstream smoke [1][2]. |
| **Asbestos** | Synergistic effect with smoking (multiplicative, not merely additive). Causes direct DNA damage and chronic inflammation. Ask about occupation — ***renovation/demolition workers*** [1][2]. |
| **Cooking fumes** | Particularly relevant in HK/China — high-temperature wok frying generates carcinogenic volatile compounds. Partially explains high adenocarcinoma rates in non-smoking Asian women [1]. |
| **Radiation** | Prior thoracic radiotherapy (e.g., for Hodgkin lymphoma), radon gas exposure (a naturally occurring radioactive gas from soil/rock that accumulates indoors) [1][2]. |
| **Air pollution** | Particulate matter (PM2.5) classified as Group 1 carcinogen by IARC. |
| **TB / Scar tissue** | ***"Scar tumours"*** — classically described in textbooks; chronic inflammation and fibrosis provide a mutagenic microenvironment [1]. |

### Genetic / Host Factors

| Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| **Family history** | **1.5× risk** with positive family history [1] |
| **EGFR mutation** | ***55% of local (HK) adenocarcinomas*** harbour EGFR mutations — much more common in Asians vs Caucasians [1]. This is a driver mutation and a therapeutic target (erlotinib, gefitinib, osimertinib). |
| **Previous lung disease** | **Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF)** is a recognized risk factor — chronic epithelial injury and aberrant repair [1]. COPD also independently increases risk. |

<Callout title="Smoking and Risk" type="error">
A common mistake is to say "risk returns to normal after quitting." It does NOT. Risk falls substantially (especially after 10–15 years), but it never equals that of a never-smoker — the accumulated genetic damage persists. Specifically, ***risk of CA lung falls in those who stopped smoking, but still continues to rise with age faster than never smokers*** [1].
</Callout>

## 4. Anatomy and Function (Relevant to Lung Cancer)

Understanding the anatomy is essential because the **location** of the tumour determines the **clinical presentation** and **resectability**.

### Bronchial Anatomy

- **Trachea** → bifurcates at the **carina** (T4/5 level) into:
  - **Right main bronchus**: shorter, wider, more vertical → aspirated foreign bodies and haematogenous metastases favour the right side
  - **Left main bronchus**: longer, narrower, more horizontal
- **Lobar bronchi**: RUL, RML, RLL (right); LUL, LLL (left)
- **Segmental bronchi** → subsegmental → terminal bronchioles → respiratory bronchioles → alveoli
- The bronchial epithelium is **pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium** — the cell of origin for squamous cell carcinoma (via squamous metaplasia) and SCLC
- The peripheral airways and alveoli are lined by **type I and type II pneumocytes** and **Clara cells** — the cell of origin for adenocarcinoma

### Key Adjacent Structures (Why Location Matters)

| Structure | Clinical Relevance |
|---|---|
| **Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN)** | Left RLN loops under the aortic arch — left-sided tumours (especially left hilar) can compress it → **hoarseness** |
| **Phrenic nerve** | Runs along the pericardium — involvement → **diaphragm paralysis** (elevated hemidiaphragm on CXR) |
| **Superior vena cava (SVC)** | Runs through the right superior mediastinum — compression → **SVCO syndrome** |
| **Oesophagus** | Posterior mediastinum — compression or invasion → **dysphagia** |
| **Brachial plexus (C8-T1)** | Apical (Pancoast) tumours invade the brachial plexus → arm pain, weakness, small muscle wasting |
| **Sympathetic chain / stellate ganglion** | Also at the lung apex — invasion → **Horner's syndrome** (miosis, ptosis, anhidrosis, enophthalmos) |
| **Pleura** | Visceral and parietal pleura — invasion → **pleural effusion**, pleuritic chest pain |
| **Pericardium** | Contiguous with the lung — invasion → **pericardial effusion ± tamponade** |
| **Chest wall / ribs** | Direct invasion → localised chest wall pain, pathological rib fractures |
| **Pulmonary arteries and veins** | Vascular invasion affects staging and resectability |

### Lymphatic Drainage

- **Intrapulmonary** → **hilar (N1)** → **ipsilateral mediastinal (N2)** → **contralateral mediastinal or supraclavicular (N3)**
- Lymph node stations are defined by the IASLC map — relevant for surgical staging
- Supraclavicular lymphadenopathy (especially left-sided — **Virchow's node**) indicates advanced disease

### Metastatic Pathways

Lung cancer metastasises via:
1. **Direct invasion** (chest wall, mediastinum, pleura, pericardium)
2. **Lymphatic spread** (hilar → mediastinal → supraclavicular nodes)
3. **Haematogenous spread** — common sites: **Brain, Bone, Liver, Adrenals** (mnemonic: **"B-B-L-A"** or think "**B**lood **B**orne to **L**iver and **A**drenals")

## 5. Etiology and Pathogenesis

### Stepwise Carcinogenesis

Lung cancer develops through a ***stepwise progression with accumulation of genetic mutations and increasing degree of dysplasia*** [1]. This is analogous to the adenoma-carcinoma sequence in colorectal cancer:

**For squamous cell carcinoma:**
Normal columnar epithelium → **squamous metaplasia** (from chronic irritation by smoking) → **dysplasia** (mild → moderate → severe) → **carcinoma in situ** → **invasive carcinoma**

**For adenocarcinoma:**
Normal type II pneumocytes/Clara cells → **atypical adenomatous hyperplasia (AAH)** → **adenocarcinoma in situ (AIS)** → **minimally invasive adenocarcinoma (MIA)** → **invasive adenocarcinoma**

This progression is driven by accumulation of driver mutations in oncogenes and tumour suppressor genes.

### Key Genetic Changes

***The disease is highly heterogeneous between different subtypes and within the same subtype*** [1].

| Genetic Alteration | Prevalence (HK/Asian) | Subtype Association | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| ***EGFR mutation*** | ***55% of local adenocarcinoma*** [1] | Adenocarcinoma, non-smokers, females, Asians | Targetable with TKIs (gefitinib, erlotinib, osimertinib). Much more common in Asians vs Caucasians (~15%) |
| ***ALK translocation*** | ***5% of local adenocarcinoma*** [1] | Adenocarcinoma, younger patients, non-/light smokers | Targetable with ALK inhibitors (crizotinib, alectinib, lorlatinib) |
| ***KRAS mutation*** | ***5–10% of local adenocarcinoma*** [1] | Adenocarcinoma, smokers | Historically "undruggable" but now targetable with sotorasib (KRAS G12C inhibitor, FDA-approved 2021) |
| **TP53 mutation** | 50–70% across all subtypes | All subtypes | Loss of tumour suppressor — allows unchecked proliferation |
| **RB1 loss** | Near-universal in SCLC | SCLC | Combined with TP53 loss → hallmark of SCLC |
| **ROS1 rearrangement** | 1–2% | Adenocarcinoma | Targetable with crizotinib |
| **BRAF V600E** | 1–3% | Adenocarcinoma | Targetable with dabrafenib + trametinib |
| **PD-L1 expression** | Variable | All subtypes | Predicts response to immune checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, nivolumab, atezolizumab) |
| **MET exon 14 skipping** | 3–4% | Adenocarcinoma, sarcomatoid | Targetable with capmatinib, tepotinib |

<Callout title="Why Molecular Testing Matters">
In 2025–2026 practice, **every NSCLC patient** (especially non-squamous) should have comprehensive molecular profiling — at minimum EGFR, ALK, ROS1, BRAF, PD-L1. Next-generation sequencing (NGS) panels are now standard. This is because targeted therapy dramatically improves outcomes in driver-positive disease (e.g., EGFR+ adenocarcinoma median OS > 3 years with osimertinib vs ~1 year with chemo alone).
</Callout>

## 6. Classification / Pathological Subtypes

### Overview Table

| | **Adenocarcinoma (ADC)** | **Squamous Cell Ca (SCC)** | **Large Cell Ca (LCLC)** | **Small Cell Ca (SCLC)** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Frequency** | ***40–55%*** [1][2] | ***20–30%*** [1][2] | ***5–10%*** [1][2] | ***5–15%*** [1][2] |
| **Smoking association** | ***Weakest*** (young female, non-smoker archetype) [2] | ***Strongest*** [2] | Moderate | ***Strong*** [2] |
| **Typical location** | ***Peripheral*** [2] | ***Central*** [2] | ***Peripheral*** [2] | ***Central*** [2] |
| **Cell of origin** | Type II pneumocytes / Clara cells | Bronchial epithelium (via squamous metaplasia) | Undifferentiated | Neuroendocrine (Kulchitsky) cells |
| **IHC markers** | ***TTF-1*** [2] | ***P40, P63*** [2] | Variable | ***Synaptophysin, Chromogranin*** [2] |
| **Growth pattern** | Glandular / lepidic / papillary / micropapillary / solid | Keratin pearls, intercellular bridges | Sheets of large undifferentiated cells | Small round blue cells, high N:C ratio, nuclear moulding, crush artefact |
| **Key molecular** | EGFR, ALK, KRAS, ROS1 | PIK3CA, FGFR | Variable | TP53 + RB1 (near-universal) |
| **Behaviour** | Variable | Moderate | Aggressive | ***Tends to metastasize early*** [2], most aggressive |
| **Paraneoplastic** | Hypertrophic pulmonary osteoarthropathy (HPOA), thrombosis | Hypercalcaemia (PTHrP) | Variable | SIADH, ectopic ACTH, Lambert-Eaton |

### Understanding the IHC Markers

- **TTF-1** (Thyroid Transcription Factor-1): "TTF" = thyroid transcription factor. It is expressed in type II pneumocytes and thyroid follicular cells. Positive in adenocarcinoma — helps distinguish primary lung adenocarcinoma from metastatic adenocarcinoma of non-pulmonary origin.
- **P40, P63**: squamous markers — these are isoforms of the p53 family that are selectively expressed in squamous epithelium.
- **Synaptophysin, Chromogranin**: neuroendocrine markers — synaptophysin is a synaptic vesicle glycoprotein; chromogranin is stored in dense-core neurosecretory granules. Their presence confirms neuroendocrine differentiation in SCLC.

### Adenocarcinoma Subtypes (WHO 2021)

Understanding the spectrum:
1. **Atypical adenomatous hyperplasia (AAH)** — pre-invasive, ≤0.5 cm
2. **Adenocarcinoma in situ (AIS)** — ≤3 cm, purely lepidic growth (tumour cells grow along alveolar walls without invasion), no invasion. 100% 5-year survival after resection
3. **Minimally invasive adenocarcinoma (MIA)** — ≤3 cm, predominantly lepidic, ≤5 mm invasion. Near 100% 5-year survival
4. **Invasive adenocarcinoma** — classified by predominant pattern: lepidic, acinar, papillary, micropapillary, solid. Micropapillary and solid patterns carry the worst prognosis.

### SCLC: Why It's Different

SCLC deserves special attention because it behaves completely differently:
- Arises from **neuroendocrine cells** (Kulchitsky cells) in the bronchial mucosa
- **Doubling time** is extremely short (~30 days vs ~180 days for adenocarcinoma)
- **Two-thirds have extensive-stage disease** at diagnosis (already metastasised)
- Exquisitely **chemosensitive** initially (responds dramatically to cisplatin/etoposide) but almost always relapses
- Staged as **Limited Stage** (confined to one hemithorax + regional nodes, can be encompassed in one radiation field) vs **Extensive Stage** (anything beyond) — TNM staging is rarely used for SCLC
- **Surgery** is almost never appropriate (except rare stage I disease)

<Callout title="NSCLC vs SCLC" type="error">
Do not confuse the staging and treatment paradigms. NSCLC uses TNM staging and surgery is the mainstay for early disease. SCLC uses Limited vs Extensive staging and is treated primarily with chemoradiation (limited) or chemo-immunotherapy (extensive). Surgery has almost no role in SCLC.
</Callout>

## 7. Clinical Features

### General Approach

Lung cancer can present in many ways. It is helpful to organise the clinical features by **mechanism**:

1. **Local effects of the primary tumour** (endobronchial / parenchymal)
2. **Regional spread** (mediastinal / chest wall invasion)
3. **Distant metastasis**
4. **Paraneoplastic syndromes** (remote effects not due to direct tumour invasion)
5. **Constitutional symptoms**

Many patients are asymptomatic early on — lung cancer is often discovered **incidentally** on imaging done for other reasons, or presents late with advanced disease (which is why the prognosis is so poor overall).

---

### 7A. Symptoms

#### Constitutional Symptoms [2]
- **Malaise, weight loss, cachexia, loss of appetite** — due to tumour-derived cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1) that induce a systemic inflammatory response, suppress appetite via hypothalamic effects, and promote skeletal muscle wasting (proteolysis) and lipolysis. Cachexia is mediated by proteolysis-inducing factor (PIF) and lipid-mobilising factor (LMF) secreted by tumour cells.

#### Symptoms from Endobronchial Growth (Local Effects) [2]
These occur when the tumour grows within or compresses a bronchus:

| Symptom | Pathophysiological Basis |
|---|---|
| **Cough** | Irritation of airway mucosal receptors by tumour mass. The most common presenting symptom. A new persistent cough or change in chronic cough character in a smoker is a red flag. |
| **Haemoptysis** | Erosion of bronchial mucosal blood vessels by tumour. Can range from blood-streaked sputum to massive haemoptysis (if tumour erodes into a pulmonary artery). |
| **Dyspnoea (SOB)** | Airway obstruction by tumour mass → distal atelectasis → loss of ventilated lung volume. Also contributed by pleural effusion, lymphangitis, or pericardial effusion. |
| **Wheeze / Stridor** | Fixed, localised airway narrowing by intraluminal tumour → monophonic wheeze (unlike the polyphonic wheeze of asthma/COPD). Stridor indicates tracheal or main bronchial obstruction — an emergency. |
| **Post-obstructive pneumonia** | Tumour obstructs a bronchus → mucus trapping and impaired clearance → secondary bacterial infection distal to the obstruction. A non-resolving or recurrent pneumonia in the same lobe is highly suspicious for underlying malignancy. |

<Callout title="Red Flag: Non-Resolving Pneumonia">
Any pneumonia that fails to resolve after appropriate antibiotics, or recurrent pneumonia in the same lobe, mandates a CXR and likely CT ± bronchoscopy to exclude an obstructing endobronchial tumour. This is a classic exam scenario.
</Callout>

#### Symptoms from Regional Spread [2]

| Symptom | Structure Involved | Pathophysiological Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Pleuritic chest pain** | Parietal pleura / chest wall | Tumour invades the parietal pleura (visceral pleura has no pain fibres). Constant, dull, aching pain suggests chest wall invasion; sharp, pleuritic pain suggests pleural inflammation. |
| **Pleural effusion** → increasing dyspnoea | Pleura | Direct pleural invasion, lymphatic obstruction, or inflammatory exudate. Effusion compresses underlying lung → restrictive physiology → SOB. |
| **Hoarseness of voice** | **Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN)** | Left RLN loops under the aortic arch — left hilar or mediastinal tumour/nodes compress it → **left vocal cord paralysis** → breathy hoarseness. Why left more than right? Because the left RLN has a longer intrathoracic course. |
| **Dysphagia** | **Oesophagus** | Direct compression or invasion of the oesophagus by tumour or enlarged subcarinal lymph nodes. |
| **Shoulder pain, arm pain** | **Brachial plexus (C8-T1)** | Pancoast (superior sulcus) tumour at the lung apex invades the lower trunk of the brachial plexus → pain radiating down the medial arm (T1 dermatome), weakness of intrinsic hand muscles. |
| **Small hand muscle wasting** | **T1 nerve root / lower trunk of brachial plexus** | Pancoast tumour destroys motor neurons to intrinsic hand muscles (interossei, thenar/hypothenar eminence via T1). |
| **Horner's syndrome** | **Sympathetic chain / stellate ganglion** | Pancoast tumour invades the paravertebral sympathetic chain at the apex → ipsilateral **miosis** (loss of sympathetic pupil dilation), **ptosis** (loss of Müller's muscle tone in the upper eyelid), **anhidrosis** (loss of sympathetic innervation to facial sweat glands), **enophthalmos** (relaxation of orbitalis muscle). |
| **Puffy face, arm swelling** | **SVC** | SVC obstruction (SVCO) — tumour or nodes compress the thin-walled SVC → venous congestion of the head, neck, and upper limbs → facial/periorbital oedema, headache (worse on bending forward / lying down). |

#### Symptoms from Distant Metastasis [2]

| Symptom | Site | Pathophysiological Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Bone pain, back pain, pathological fractures** | Bone (vertebrae, ribs, pelvis, long bones) | Haematogenous spread → osteolytic metastases → weakened bone → fractures with minimal trauma; periosteal stretching → pain. Vertebral metastases may cause **spinal cord compression** (oncological emergency). |
| **Headache, seizures, focal neurological deficits** | Brain | Brain metastases cause mass effect → raised ICP → headache (classically worse in morning), nausea/vomiting. Cortical metastases → seizures. Location-specific deficits (e.g., hemiparesis from motor cortex involvement). |
| **Hepatomegaly, RUQ pain, deranged LFTs** | Liver | Hepatic metastases → organ enlargement, capsular stretching → pain. Disruption of hepatocyte function → elevated ALP, GGT, bilirubin. |
| **Adrenal insufficiency (rare)** | Adrenal glands | Bilateral adrenal metastases (adrenals are a common site but clinical Addison's is rare because you need > 90% destruction). May present with fatigue, hypotension, hyponatraemia. |

#### Paraneoplastic Syndromes [2]

These are remote effects of cancer mediated by tumour-secreted hormones, peptides, or antibodies — **not** due to direct tumour invasion. They are **clinically important** because (a) they may be the presenting feature, (b) they can mimic other diseases, and (c) some are specific to certain subtypes.

**Endocrine Paraneoplastic Syndromes:**

| Syndrome | Mediator | Tumour Type | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypercalcaemia** | **PTHrP** (parathyroid hormone-related peptide) | **Squamous cell carcinoma** | Tumour secretes PTHrP → binds PTH receptor → ↑osteoclast activity → ↑bone resorption → ↑serum Ca²⁺. Also ↑renal Ca²⁺ reabsorption. This is "humoral hypercalcaemia of malignancy." |
| **SIADH** | **ADH** (vasopressin) | **Small cell carcinoma** | Ectopic ADH secretion → ↑water reabsorption in collecting ducts → dilutional hyponatraemia, concentrated urine, euvolaemia. Presents with confusion, seizures, nausea. |
| **Cushing's syndrome** | **Ectopic ACTH** | **Small cell carcinoma** | Ectopic ACTH → bilateral adrenal hyperplasia → ↑cortisol. Unlike pituitary Cushing's, ectopic ACTH Cushing's presents acutely with hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis, hyperglycaemia, proximal myopathy — often without classical cushingoid features because it's too rapid. |
| **Carcinoid syndrome** | Serotonin (5-HT) | Carcinoid tumours (bronchial) | Flushing, diarrhoea, wheezing, right-sided valvular heart disease (left-sided valves are protected because serotonin is metabolised in the pulmonary circulation — but bronchial carcinoid drains directly into the pulmonary veins, so can cause left-sided lesions too). |
| **Gynaecomastia** | β-hCG | Large cell carcinoma | Ectopic β-hCG → stimulates aromatase → ↑oestrogen → breast tissue proliferation in males. |

**Neurological Paraneoplastic Syndromes:**

| Syndrome | Antibody/Mediator | Tumour Type | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS)** | Anti-VGCC antibodies (voltage-gated calcium channel) | **Small cell carcinoma** | Antibodies against presynaptic VGCCs at the neuromuscular junction → ↓acetylcholine release → proximal weakness that **improves** with repeated use (opposite of myasthenia gravis). Hyporeflexia that improves after sustained contraction. |
| **Limbic encephalitis** | Anti-Hu (ANNA-1), anti-CV2 | SCLC | Autoimmune inflammation of medial temporal lobes → subacute memory loss, confusion, seizures, psychiatric symptoms. |
| **Cerebellar degeneration** | Anti-Yo, anti-Hu | SCLC | Autoimmune destruction of Purkinje cells → progressive cerebellar ataxia, dysarthria, nystagmus. |
| **Sensory neuropathy** | Anti-Hu | SCLC | Autoimmune dorsal root ganglion destruction → painful sensory neuropathy, sensory ataxia. |
| **Encephalomyelitis** | Anti-Hu | SCLC | Widespread CNS inflammation. |

**Other Paraneoplastic Syndromes:**

| Syndrome | Tumour Type | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Hypertrophic pulmonary osteoarthropathy (HPOA)** | Adenocarcinoma (most common), NSCLC | Triad of: (1) digital clubbing, (2) periostitis of long bones (painful wrists/ankles with subperiosteal new bone formation on X-ray), (3) synovitis. Mechanism poorly understood — possibly neural reflex involving vagus nerve or humoral factors (VEGF, PDGF) causing periosteal vascular proliferation. May regress after tumour resection (which supports vagal reflex theory). |
| **Dermatomyositis / Polymyositis** | Various | Autoimmune myositis — may be the presenting feature of underlying malignancy. |
| **Thrombocytosis, DIC** | Various | Tumour procoagulant activity → Trousseau's syndrome (migratory thrombophlebitis), DVT/PE. |
| **Nephrotic syndrome** | Various | Membranous nephropathy — tumour antigens deposited in glomerular basement membrane → immune complex formation. |

<Callout title="Paraneoplastic Syndromes — Quick Associations">

- **SCC** → Hypercalcaemia (PTHrP) — "**S**quamous **C**auses **C**alcium"
- **SCLC** → SIADH, Ectopic ACTH, Lambert-Eaton, Anti-Hu syndromes — "**S**mall **C**ells **L**ove **C**omplications"
- **Adenocarcinoma** → HPOA, thrombosis
- **Large cell** → Gynaecomastia (β-hCG)

</Callout>

### 7B. Signs

#### General Examination

| Sign | Pathophysiological Basis |
|---|---|
| **Cachexia** | Tumour-derived cytokines (TNF-α/IL-6) → catabolic state, skeletal muscle wasting, adipose tissue loss |
| **Clubbing** | Associated with NSCLC (especially adenocarcinoma, squamous). Mechanism debated — likely involves VEGF and PDGF → nail bed vascular proliferation and connective tissue hypertrophy. Clubbing can be the first sign of lung cancer. |
| **Tar staining of fingers** | Evidence of smoking — risk factor |
| **Pallor** | Anaemia of chronic disease or bone marrow infiltration |
| **Lymphadenopathy** (especially supraclavicular — **Virchow's node** on the left) | Lymphatic metastasis. Left supraclavicular node specifically receives drainage from the thoracic duct. |

#### Chest Examination

| Sign | Pathophysiological Basis |
|---|---|
| **Dullness to percussion + reduced breath sounds + reduced vocal resonance** | Pleural effusion (fluid conducts sound poorly; stony dull percussion). |
| **Dullness + bronchial breathing** | Consolidation/collapse distal to obstructing tumour (lung collapse transmits sound differently). |
| **Fixed monophonic wheeze** | Partial obstruction of a single bronchus by tumour → localised turbulent airflow. Unlike asthma which is diffuse and polyphonic. |
| **Stridor** | Upper airway / tracheal obstruction — a sign of critical airway compromise. |
| **Tracheal deviation** | Towards collapse (pulled by volume loss); away from massive effusion (pushed by fluid). |
| **Reduced chest expansion** | Ipsilateral effusion or collapse. |

#### Signs of Regional Spread

| Sign | Structure | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Horner's syndrome** | Sympathetic chain | Miosis, ptosis, anhidrosis, enophthalmos — ipsilateral. Best tested in a dim room (affected pupil fails to dilate). |
| **SVCO signs** | SVC | ***Puffy face, dilated chest veins, Pemberton's sign*** [2]. Pemberton's sign: raising both arms overhead → facial plethora and cyanosis (because the thoracic inlet is further compressed). Dilated veins over the anterior chest wall represent collateral venous drainage. |
| **Phrenic nerve palsy** | Phrenic nerve | **Elevated hemidiaphragm** on CXR [2]. On examination: paradoxical abdominal movement (abdomen moves inward on inspiration on the affected side). |
| **Wasting of small hand muscles** | T1/brachial plexus | Pancoast tumour — visible guttering of interossei, thenar/hypothenar wasting. |

#### Pancoast Syndrome (Superior Sulcus Tumour) [2]

***Pancoast's syndrome*** is a specific clinical constellation caused by a tumour at the **apex** of the lung invading local structures:

1. **Horner's syndrome** — invasion of sympathetic chain/stellate ganglion
2. **Brachial plexopathy** — invasion of C8/T1 roots → medial arm/forearm pain (T1 dermatome), weakness and wasting of intrinsic hand muscles
3. **Shoulder pain** — invasion of the first rib, subclavian vessels, or chest wall
4. **Rib destruction** — visible on imaging (first rib erosion)

The tumour is usually an NSCLC (often adenocarcinoma or squamous). Despite its dramatic presentation, Pancoast tumours are potentially curable with combined chemoradiotherapy followed by surgery if there is no N2/N3 disease or distant metastasis.

#### Signs of Metastasis

| Sign | Site |
|---|---|
| **Hepatomegaly** (hard, irregular, nodular liver edge) | Liver metastases |
| **Bony tenderness** (especially spine, pelvis) | Bone metastases |
| **Focal neurological signs** (hemiparesis, ataxia, cranial nerve palsies) | Brain metastases |
| **Papilloedema** | Raised ICP from brain metastases |
| **Pathological fractures** | Bone metastases (osteolytic) |
| **Skin nodules** (rare) | Cutaneous metastases |

## 8. Surgical Oncology Perspective

***From lecture GC 202: "Surgery may cure your cancer — Surgical Oncology":***

### ***Principles of Surgical Oncology*** [3]

- ***Surgery is the only curative treatment for most solid tumours*** — this is a foundational principle. For CA lung, complete surgical resection (R0 resection) offers the best chance of cure in early-stage NSCLC.
- ***The goal is complete (R0) resection with adequate margins and appropriate lymph node dissection.***
- ***Staging determines resectability*** — thorough pre-operative staging (PET-CT, mediastinal lymph node sampling) is essential to avoid futile thoracotomy.

### ***Minimally Invasive Thoracic Surgery (MITS)*** [4]

***From lecture GC 196: "Minimally Invasive Thoracic Surgery":***

- ***Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery (VATS)*** has become the standard approach for early-stage lung cancer resection in many centres, including HK.
- ***Advantages of VATS over open thoracotomy:***
  - ***Less postoperative pain***
  - ***Shorter hospital stay***
  - ***Faster recovery***
  - ***Reduced inflammatory response***
  - ***Equivalent oncological outcomes*** for early-stage disease
- ***VATS lobectomy*** is the standard of care for stage I–II NSCLC at most high-volume centres.
- ***Robotic-assisted thoracic surgery (RATS)*** is also emerging — provides 3D visualisation, wristed instruments, and enhanced dexterity.
- ***Key point: minimally invasive does NOT mean less radical — the oncological operation (anatomical resection + lymph node dissection) should be identical whether done open or VATS.***

<Callout title="Surgical Resection in CA Lung">

***The standard surgical procedure for early-stage NSCLC is anatomical lobectomy with systematic mediastinal lymph node dissection*** [3][4]. Sublobar resection (segmentectomy or wedge resection) may be considered in patients with small peripheral tumours (≤2 cm) or those with limited cardiopulmonary reserve. Pneumonectomy is reserved for central tumours that cannot be managed with lobectomy.

</Callout>

## 9. Relevant Imaging Points

### ***From lecture GC 212: "Weight loss and vomiting — gastric cancer; abdominal imaging":***

While this lecture focuses on gastric cancer, the ***principles of cross-sectional imaging for staging*** apply to lung cancer as well [5]:

- ***CT thorax with contrast*** is the initial staging investigation — evaluates primary tumour size, location, mediastinal invasion, lymphadenopathy, and pleural/pericardial disease.
- ***PET-CT*** is essential for assessing mediastinal lymph node involvement and distant metastases. PET-positive mediastinal nodes should ideally be confirmed histologically (via EBUS-TBNA or mediastinoscopy) before deciding on surgery.
- ***Brain MRI*** is recommended for staging of stage II+ NSCLC (brain is a common metastatic site and PET is unreliable for brain metastases due to high background FDG uptake).

### Relevant to ***WCS 064: "A Large Liver"*** [6]

***Liver is a common site of lung cancer metastasis.*** When evaluating a patient with CA lung and suspected liver metastases:
- ***Hepatomegaly may be the presenting sign***
- ***Multiple hypodense lesions on CT*** (or hyperechoic on USS) suggest metastatic disease
- ***Deranged LFTs*** (especially ↑ALP, ↑GGT) — obstructive pattern if near hilum, hepatocellular if parenchymal infiltration
- ***A large liver in the context of known CA lung is metastatic disease until proven otherwise***

## 10. Summary of Clinical Features by Subtype

| Feature | Central Tumours (SCC, SCLC) | Peripheral Tumours (ADC, LCLC) |
|---|---|---|
| **Cough, haemoptysis, wheeze** | Early and prominent | Less common early |
| **Post-obstructive pneumonia** | Common | Rare |
| **SVCO, RLN palsy, dysphagia** | More common (mediastinal involvement) | Less common |
| **Chest wall pain, pleural effusion** | Less common early | More common (pleural involvement) |
| **Pancoast syndrome** | Rare | Occurs with apical tumours (any type but often SCC or ADC) |
| **Paraneoplastic syndromes** | SCLC: SIADH, ACTH, LEMS; SCC: hypercalcaemia | ADC: HPOA, thrombosis |
| **Early metastasis** | SCLC: very early | LCLC: moderate; ADC: variable |

<Callout title="High Yield Summary">

**Definition**: Malignant neoplasm of bronchial epithelium; 85% NSCLC (ADC > SCC > LCLC), 15% SCLC.

**Epidemiology (HK)**: 2nd most common cancer, 1st in cancer mortality. M:F = 2:1. In HK females: < 40% smokers, > 75% adenocarcinoma, high EGFR mutation rate (55%).

**Risk Factors**: Smoking (90%), passive smoking, asbestos, cooking fumes (HK!), radiation, IPF, family history, EGFR mutation.

**Pathology Pearls**:
- ADC: peripheral, TTF-1+, EGFR/ALK/KRAS mutations, weakest smoking association
- SCC: central, P40/P63+, strongest smoking association, PTHrP → hypercalcaemia
- SCLC: central, synaptophysin/chromogranin+, TP53+RB1 loss, earliest metastasis, SIADH/ectopic ACTH/LEMS
- LCLC: peripheral, diagnosis of exclusion

**Clinical Features**: Organise by local (cough, haemoptysis, dyspnoea, wheeze), regional (Pancoast, SVCO, RLN palsy, phrenic nerve palsy, pleural effusion, dysphagia), distant metastasis (B-B-L-A: brain, bone, liver, adrenal), paraneoplastic (SCC→Ca²⁺, SCLC→SIADH/ACTH/LEMS, ADC→HPOA), constitutional (cachexia, weight loss).

**Surgery**: VATS lobectomy is standard for early-stage NSCLC. Complete R0 resection with mediastinal lymph node dissection. Molecular profiling (EGFR, ALK, ROS1, PD-L1) is mandatory for all NSCLC.

</Callout>
